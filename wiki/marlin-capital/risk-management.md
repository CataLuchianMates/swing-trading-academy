# Risk Management — Marlin Capital

## Core Philosophy

"Risk management over everything." — Article title from July 2025, a recurring theme.

Marlin's risk management is not a separate overlay — it IS the system. The stage analysis framework, the position sizing rules, the cash management, the cutting of losers — all of it is risk management.

## The "Size for 0" Rule (Options)

The most important options risk rule:

"I always size for 0 on all options trades. That framework is exactly why a -100% losing options trade like this does not move the needle on this portfolio. It is the same as a -5% stock trade due to proper position sizing."

**The math:** If you're willing to risk $2,500 on a trade in a $50k account (5%), and you buy options that cost $2,500, you've "sized for 0" — the worst case is that $2,500 goes to 0. You then treat those options like any other 5% position, never adding to a losing options position.

This prevents the catastrophic mistake of letting losing options become portfolio-destroying positions.

## Position Sizing (Active Trading)

Standard position sizing formula:

**Step 1:** Decide your account risk (typically 1-3% of account)
**Step 2:** Determine your stop loss distance (distance from entry to stop in $)
**Step 3:** Calculate shares: Account Risk $ / Stop Distance $ = Number of Shares
**Step 4:** Calculate position size: Shares × Entry Price

**Example ($100k account, 1% risk, $9.50 stop distance):**
- Account risk: $1,000 (1% of $100k)
- $1,000 / $9.50 = 105 shares
- 105 × $212.03 = $22,263 position (22.3% of account)

Key insight: **You can have a large position as a % of account while risking very little**, as long as your stop is tight. Risk management is about $ risk, not position size %.

**Adjusting risk based on conditions:**
- Risk MORE when on a winning streak
- Risk LESS when on a losing streak
- Risk LESS in choppy/uncertain market environments

## Cutting Losers

"Holding stocks through huge drawdowns, hoping and praying it'll recover, and calling it 'conviction'? That's just not how I do things."

**Rules for cutting losers:**

1. **Active trades:** Exit if stock closes below key moving average (21 EMA). No exceptions. "On to the next."

2. **Long-term portfolio:** Exit when Stage 3/4 characteristics appear — topping behavior, broken uptrend, stock acting weak relative to market. Don't wait for a thesis article to justify holding.

3. **The laggard test:** "If a stock simply cannot get out of its own way, and the market clearly does not view it as a leader — exit." Applied to CRWV (exited -11%) and AMPX (exited -24%) in May 2026.

4. **Portfolio-level rule:** Having 2-3 earnings losers out of 16 positions is expected and acceptable. "Having a couple of earnings losers from that group is to be expected." What's not acceptable is letting those losers become large detractors.

## Managing Through Drawdowns

### The Feb-March 2026 Selloff Protocol
When the market turned ugly in Feb 2026 (reminding Marlin of 2022):

1. **Added shorts as hedges** — QUBT, NNE, ACHR (Stage 4 downtrend names)
2. **Raised cash** — went from ~70% long to majority cash by late March
3. **Did NOT cut all longs** — held Stage 2 names that were still acting well
4. **Waited for confirmation** — did not re-deploy until the April 8 ceasefire gap up AND charts confirming (leaders breaking out simultaneously)

"I wouldn't call holding stocks through stage 4 downtrends/bear cycles 'conviction'. I would call it poor risk management."

### Signs It's Time to Raise Cash
- Market action resembling 2022 (stage 4 breakdowns across many names)
- SPX acting weak relative to historical patterns for this stage of cycle
- Leaders refusing to bounce even as the index recovers
- Most speculative names entering stage 4 downtrends while you're early in the move

## Trailing Stops

Marlin uses **closing stops** (not intraday) to avoid being shaken out by volatility:
- "My trailing stop will remain below $160 (closing stop)"
- Moves the stop up as the stock advances, locking in gains
- Never moves the stop DOWN once set

**Trailing stop ladder for a 4-part scale-out:**
- Sell 1/4 at first target → stop covers cost on remaining
- Sell 1/4 at second target → stop well in-profit
- Final 50%: tighten stop to first technical support below current price

## Short Selling as Portfolio Insurance

In the late stages of the AI bubble, Marlin added shorts as hedges:

**Two approaches:**
1. **Buy Jan 2027 puts** on speculative names (limited risk, no margin call risk)
2. **Short the stock** directly (requires more active management)

Shorts serve a dual purpose:
1. Hedge portfolio exposure to market beta
2. Generate gains if the speculative bubble unwinds

"I'm holding the shorts for now. They act as hedges for our longs. I will let everyone know if anything changes."

## Going to Cash Is a Strategy

Jesse Livermore principle: "There's a time to go long, a time to go short, and a time to go fishing."

**When to go fishing:**
- SPX has been flat for 2+ months with no clear direction
- Active trading setups aren't materializing
- The market environment doesn't reward your edge

"My goal is to be very active during the meat of the trends (like April-October), and passive during the choppy/pullback phases (like October-now). When this is the case, I have no problem simply waiting it out."

This patience — doing nothing for months — is itself a form of risk management that preserves capital for the next big opportunity.

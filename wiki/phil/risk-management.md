# Risk Management

## The Most Important Topic in the Entire Academy

Phil places risk management first in the Academy curriculum because without it, failure is guaranteed. Not likely — guaranteed. The reason is harsh and simple: "Without risk management, you will fail."

The fundamental truth of trading is that you cannot control when the market moves, where it moves, or how fast it moves. The only thing you can control is **how much money you are willing to lose if you are wrong.** Risk management is the system that enforces that control.

---

## Investing vs. Swing Trading — Two Different Frameworks

Risk management applies to swing trading, not to long-term investing. This distinction matters.

**Investing:** No risk management needed. If an investment runs against you, you do not exit — you buy more. The only "risk management" is capital allocation across asset classes. There is no stop loss.

**Swing trading:** Full risk management is required. Every trade has a defined entry, stop loss, and take profit. If the trade goes against you, you take the loss and exit. There is nothing wrong with taking a loss on a swing trade — the strategy is profitable overall because probabilities work in your favour across many trades.

---

## The Cardinal Sin

The worst thing a swing trader can do:
1. Set a stop loss before entering
2. Watch the market approach the stop
3. Stay in anyway, praying for a reversal
4. Watch the losses compound beyond what was planned

"More often than not, if you ignore your stop loss, the market will keep going against you." The stop loss is not a suggestion. It is a commitment made before the trade was entered. Honouring it is what keeps a losing trade from becoming a catastrophic one.

## The Three Steps of Risk Management

### Step 1 — Know Your Account Size

Maintain **separate accounts** for investing and swing trading. All risk management calculations are based on the swing trading account size. Mixing the two makes it impossible to measure swing trading performance accurately.

Phil recommends a 50/50 split of total capital: 50% for investing, 50% for swing trading.

### Step 2 — Determine Risk Per Trade

**Maximum 5% of total swing trading capital per trade.** For a $100,000 swing trading account, that is $5,000 maximum risk per trade.

| Experience Level | Risk Per Trade |
|---|---|
| Beginner | 1–2% |
| Intermediate | 2–3% |
| Advanced | 3–5% |

Start at 1–2% while learning. Prove to yourself that the system works. Scale up gradually as confidence grows. Never exceed 5%.

Why is 5% enough? With an approximately 80% win rate and 1:1 RRR, 10 trades at 5% risk produces:
- 8 wins at +$5,000 = +$40,000
- 2 losses at -$5,000 = -$10,000
- **Net: +$30,000 on a $100,000 account (30% return)**

There is no reason to risk more than 5%. The math already outperforms all markets.

### Step 3 — Calculate Position Size

**Formula: Position Size = Risk Amount ÷ % Distance to Stop Loss**

Example:
- Account: $100,000
- Risk per trade: 5% = $5,000
- Stock at $100, stop at $90 (10% away)
- Position Size = $5,000 ÷ 10% = **$50,000** (500 shares)

Verification: 500 shares × $10 loss = $5,000 ✓

For LEAP options, position sizing is simpler: your risk is the premium paid. If maximum risk is $5,000 and one contract costs $4,600, buy one contract. No further calculation needed.

## Risk-to-Reward Ratio (RRR)

Before entering any trade, calculate the RRR using TradingView's measurement tool:

**RRR = Distance to Take Profit ÷ Distance to Stop Loss**

| RRR | Action |
|---|---|
| Below 1:1 | **Do not take the trade** |
| 1:1 | Take it |
| 1:2 | Take it quickly |
| 1:3 or better | Excellent — prioritise these |

With 80% win rate and increasing RRR, profits scale dramatically:

| RRR | 8 Wins | 2 Losses | Net (at $5K risk) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1:1 | +$40K | -$10K | +$30K |
| 1:2 | +$80K | -$10K | +$70K |
| 1:3 | +$120K | -$10K | +$110K |

The minimum 1:1 RRR is **non-negotiable**. If you cannot achieve at least 1:1 on a setup, you skip it regardless of how strong the setup looks otherwise.

## Stop Loss Placement Rules

**Stop goes below monthly support with room to breathe.** Not immediately below — give the trade room.

Typical distance: 10–25% depending on the asset's volatility. Large-cap stocks with a 10% stop on the underlying might still need the stop placed further to avoid being shaken out by daily noise that briefly pierces monthly support.

This is a case-by-case judgment for each setup. The principle: "if the market truly breaks support on the monthly timeframe, your stop should catch it — but it should not trigger on normal daily noise."

**When two support levels (horizontal and trend line) are present near each other:** Always place the stop below the **horizontal support**, not the trend line. The horizontal acts as the final line of defence. If price breaks the trend line but bounces at the horizontal, you remain in the trade. Placing the stop below the trend line risks getting stopped out just before the horizontal causes a reversal.

**Never move your stop loss against yourself.** Once placed, a stop loss is not adjusted to give a trade "more room." That is the cardinal sin. The stop was placed where it was for a reason — if the market is approaching it, the thesis may be wrong.

---

## Portfolio Heat — Maximum Concurrent Risk

| Rule | Value |
|---|---|
| Max open swing trades | ~5 at a time |
| Max risk per trade | 5% |
| Total open risk (worst case) | ~20% |

If every single open trade hit its stop simultaneously — extremely unlikely with a 60–80% win rate — you would lose approximately 20%. That is your worst-case scenario and it is recoverable.

This is why the 5% per trade rule exists alongside the 5-trade limit. Together they cap catastrophic drawdown.

## Sizing LEAP Positions vs Stock Positions

For regular stock: calculate position size using the formula (risk ÷ stop percentage). One position can consume 50% of your swing trading capital.

For LEAP options: your maximum loss is the premium paid. Size so that buying the option (or multiple contracts) does not exceed your per-trade risk. One LEAP contract on a large-cap might cost $4,000–$6,000 — that fits within the 5% rule on most accounts above $80K.

The capital efficiency of options is why Phil considers them essential: instead of one $50,000 stock position using 50% of your capital, one LEAP contract at $5,000 uses only 5%, freeing $45,000 for four more positions.

## Rules Around Earnings and News Events

Phil's framework is fundamentals-agnostic — no earnings monitoring, no news tracking. This is by design.

The system does not adjust for earnings events or macro announcements. If your analysis shows a valid setup, you take it. If the monthly chart structure is intact, the thesis is intact. Earnings can create volatility, but on the monthly timeframe, that volatility is absorbed into the candle. Phil focuses exclusively on price action and structure.

For LEAP holders: if you hold through earnings and the stock drops hard, the premium you paid remains your maximum loss. The built-in risk cap of options provides earnings protection that stock positions do not.

## Rules That Are Absolute vs. Rules That Have Discretion

**Absolute — never broken:**
- Stop loss is placed before every trade
- Stop loss is never moved against yourself after entry
- Maximum 5% risk per trade
- Minimum 1:1 RRR before entry
- Monthly candle close below support = exit (for stock positions)

**Discretion allowed:**
- How far below support to place the stop (10–25% range based on volatility)
- Whether to take partials at 1:1 or let the full position run to resistance
- Whether to enter on the breakout or wait for the retest
- Exact strike price for LEAP options (ATM is the target, nearest available is acceptable)

The rule Phil applies to the boundary between discretion and rules: "Individual losses are expected and acceptable. The strategy works because the math works." When in doubt, err toward the rule rather than the exception.

## The Workflow: All Risk Management Happens Before Entry

```
Before entry:               After entry:
1. Chart analysis           → Wait
2. Identify entry/stop/TP   → Do nothing
3. Calculate position size  → Trust the process
4. Check RRR ≥ 1:1
5. Journal and screenshot
6. Place alerts
7. Enter trade
```

Once you are in a trade, there is nothing productive to do about risk. All the control you had was exercised before you clicked buy. This is why Phil says the work is 99% analysis and preparation — the execution itself is mechanical.

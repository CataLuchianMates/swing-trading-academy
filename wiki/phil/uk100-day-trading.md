# UK100 Day Trading — London Open Breakout Strategy

## The Core Philosophy

Phil's day trading approach is built on a single edge: **session open momentum.** The first 30 minutes after a major index opens is when the largest volume and the clearest directional moves occur. Breakouts that are going to hold tend to happen during this window. After it closes, the market becomes choppy, back-and-forth, and the edge disappears.

This means the strategy requires almost no screen time. You watch the chart for approximately 30 minutes at the session open, take a maximum of two trades, and then walk away. The rest of the day is irrelevant.

Day trading in Phil's system is explicitly **secondary to swing trading**. Swing trading remains the primary income-generating activity. Day trading is being actively developed and tested — it is not yet treated as a core income source.

---

## The Instrument: UK100 (FTSE 100)

Phil trades exclusively the **UK100 (FTSE 100)** for all day trades, on both the London and US sessions. The reasons:

- The UK100 typically produces **cleaner price action** than NASDAQ or Dow Jones for this strategy
- Trading a single instrument allows you to master one chart's behaviour rather than spreading attention across multiple markets
- The breakout levels on UK100 are consistent and repeatable

During the US session, Phil monitors US 30 and NASDAQ as directional context for the broader market, but **positions are only taken on UK100**.

Why not NASDAQ or S&P? The price action on those indices around session open is often more erratic and prone to false moves. UK100 tends to produce cleaner, more decisive breakouts.

## The Time Window

| Session | Active Window |
|---|---|
| London Open | ~8:55 AM – 9:25 AM CET (approximately 30 minutes around open) |
| US Open | ~3:15 PM – 3:45 PM CET (approximately 30 minutes around open) |

Enter approximately 5 minutes before the open to identify your levels. Execute only within the 30-minute window. Once the window closes, **step away from the chart** regardless of what any open position is doing. Set stop loss and take profit, then leave.

If you miss the window — because you were busy, because the setup was unclear, because you hesitated — you do not enter. The edge is specifically the session open momentum. "After the first 30 minutes, price action typically becomes choppier and less directional. Taking positions outside the window violates the rules and removes the edge."

## The Setup: Horizontal Level Breakouts

Phil is not looking for trend line breakouts, pattern completions, or indicator signals. Only **clear horizontal level breakouts**.

**Before the session opens:**
1. Identify the key horizontal **resistance** above current price
2. Identify the key horizontal **support** below current price
3. Do not use trend lines for this. Horizontal levels only.

**When the session opens:**
- If price breaks **above resistance** → go long
- If price breaks **below support** → go short

That is the entire decision tree. No indicators, no moving averages, no complex analysis.

---

## Entry Execution

Day trading entry is fundamentally different from swing trading entry. In swing trading, Phil waits for two candle closes above the level before confirming a BBOS. In day trading, **momentum is everything** — hesitation costs the entry.

Enter as soon as the breakout is clear — ideally on the candle that breaks the level, or immediately after.

Two entry methods:
- **Manual entry** — watch the level, enter when price clearly breaks it
- **Stop order** — place a stop-buy order just above resistance (or stop-sell just below support) before the open; fills automatically when the level breaks

Stop orders are practical because they remove the need to watch the chart in real time and ensure you are filled quickly. Either method is acceptable — preference only.

---

## Stop Loss Placement

Stop loss goes **tight — just on the other side of the broken level.**

If you went long on a resistance breakout, stop goes just below that resistance level. If the breakout is false and price reverses back through the level, you want to be out immediately. The tightness of the stop is what keeps individual losses small on a strategy where false breakouts are an accepted and expected part of the process.

---

## Target: Minimum 1:3 Risk-to-Reward

Every day trade targets a **minimum 1:3 risk-to-reward ratio.** Phil sometimes holds for 1:4 or 1:5 when momentum is strong.

The math behind this requirement: if you hit 1:3 on wins and lose 1:1 on losses, you only need to be right **25% of the time** to break even. Being right 40–50% of the time at 1:3 produces strong profits even with frequent losses. The large winners compensate for multiple stop-outs.

This is why closing too early is strictly prohibited. Closing at 1:1 or 1:2 when the setup is 1:3+ fundamentally changes the math and makes the strategy unprofitable over time.

---

## Holding Discipline — When to Hold vs. When to Exit Early

### Hold Until 1:3 When

- Momentum is clearly strong: large, clean candles in your direction with no back-and-forth
- Price moved to 1:2 quickly with no sign of stalling
- The most recent 15-minute candle closed bullish (for longs) above the key level

### Early Exit (Before 1:3) Acceptable When

- Price has been oscillating around break-even for 30–40 minutes with no clear directional bias
- The momentum that triggered the trade has clearly dissipated
- Extended time + no momentum + oscillating price = the edge is gone

Phil's example from the April 20, 2026 US session: "UK100 position hovered around break-even for an extended period despite being slightly in profit at times. After 40 minutes of indecision, the correct decision was to close at a 1:2 rather than watch it tick back to the stop."

**Never close winners early out of fear.** This is especially tempting after a session with prior losses — the urge to lock in any profit is strong. Resist it. A 1:1 close after two prior losses means you finish the session red. A 1:3+ close on the same session means you finish green. The difference is holding discipline.

## Maximum Positions Per Session

**Maximum 2 positions per session.**

The second position is only taken if:
- The first position was a stop-out
- A new, clean breakout level becomes available during the still-open session window

If the first position is profitable and running, no second position is taken — let the first trade resolve.

If both positions are stop-outs, **the session is over.** No exceptions. No third trade. The session is closed, and you return for the next one with a fresh mindset.

---

## Risk Per Trade and Daily Loss Limit

**Risk no more than 2% of your day-trading capital per position.**

With a maximum of 2 positions per session and 2 sessions per day (London + US), the worst-case scenario is:
- 4 positions × 2% = **8% maximum daily drawdown**

This is uncomfortable but recoverable. A single 1:3 win in the same day recovers the drawdown.

Phil's allocation for day trading in the sessions: $100,000 specifically for day trading (separate from swing trading capital), meaning 2% = $2,000 per trade.

For smaller accounts, scale accordingly. 2% of $20,000 = $400 per trade. Do not copy Phil's dollar amounts — copy his percentage.

**Two losses = session done.** Even if a third setup appears within the time window that looks technically perfect, the rule is two losses maximum per session. Walk away.

---

## What Disqualifies a Setup

- Level is not a clean horizontal — it is a trend line, a diagonal, or an approximate cluster
- Breakout happens outside the 30-minute time window
- Price action at the level is choppy and unclear — no decisive break
- You have already taken two positions this session (regardless of outcome)
- The instrument is anything other than UK100

---

## False Breakouts Are Normal

False breakouts are an unavoidable part of session-open trading. Every breakout must be treated as real because you cannot know in advance which ones will hold.

The response to a stop-out is mechanical: close the position (the stop does this automatically), register the loss, and assess whether a second setup has appeared within the window. There is no analysis of "why" the breakout was false. There is no adjustment to the rules. The system is probabilistic — over time, the 1:3+ wins dramatically outweigh the 1:1 losses.

Phil's live session record shows the pattern: multiple 1:3, 1:4, and even 1:7 wins in prior sessions, with occasional two-loss sessions like April 20, 2026. "Looking at Phil's track record: over time, the math works out. One strong win in a week covers several losing sessions."

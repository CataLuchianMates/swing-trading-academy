# Phil's Technical Analysis Methodology

## Why Technical Analysis — Not Fundamentals

Phil does not use fundamental analysis for swing trading. The reason is simple: fundamentals cannot tell you where to enter, where to place your stop loss, or where to take profit. There is no way to translate "strong revenue growth" into "buy at $120, stop at $110, target $160." Fundamentals also play out over 10–30 years — too slow for swing trading.

Technical analysis solves all three problems: **it provides entry, stop loss, and take profit levels for every trade.** It is the only tool needed.

An additional benefit: ignoring fundamentals eliminates noise. No monitoring earnings, no tracking CEO statements, no watching headlines. You look at charts objectively and remove the psychological static that comes with news-driven decision-making.

## Chart Setup: Non-Negotiable Settings

Before any analysis, configure the chart correctly. Wrong settings lead to wrong analysis.

### Monthly Timeframe

Every analysis begins on the **monthly timeframe**. Each candle represents one full month of price action — from the first trading day to the last. This eliminates all daily noise and gives you the clearest picture of the underlying trend and structure.

Phil specifically warns against doing swing trading analysis on lower timeframes: "If an asset doesn't have 10–15 years of history, consider whether you really want to enter a swing trade on it."

### Logarithmic Scale

Always use the **logarithmic view** (the "L" button in TradingView). Never use a linear chart for swing trading analysis.

Why logarithmic? A linear chart calculates equal distances in absolute dollar terms — so a move from $10 to $20 looks the same as $100 to $110, even though the first is 100% and the second is 10%. This distorts trend lines and support levels on any asset with significant price history.

A logarithmic chart calculates equal distances in **percentage terms**. A 100% move always looks the same distance regardless of the price level. This gives you:
- Accurate trend lines that connect proportional moves
- Support and resistance zones that are genuinely meaningful
- Correct pattern identification (channels, triangles) that align with actual price behaviour

Phil's additional reasoning: "Any position can only lose 100% but can gain unlimited percentages. Using logarithmic charts embraces this asymmetry." Always set logarithmic as your TradingView default.

### Naked Chart — No Indicators

Keep the chart **completely clean**. Remove all indicators: no RSI, no MACD, no Bollinger Bands, no moving averages. Just green and red candles plus the levels you draw yourself.

Indicators clutter the chart and create false confidence. Price action on a naked monthly chart tells you everything you need to know.

### Zoom Out

Zoom out to see at least **10–20 years of history** before doing any analysis. A stock might look like it is in a downtrend over the past 6 months but is clearly in a multi-year uptrend when you see the full picture. Always start with the big picture.

Also disable **dividend adjustment** (the "ADJ" button in TradingView) — you are trading price movement, not collecting dividends, and dividend adjustment distorts price levels.

## Step 1: Identifying the Trend

Three trend types Phil recognises:

**Uptrend** — higher highs and higher lows (or higher highs with equal lows). The chart moves from bottom-left to top-right. This is the only trend Phil actively seeks setups in.

**Downtrend** — lower lows and lower highs (or lower lows with equal highs). Do not trade. Full stop.

**Sideways** — a mix: higher lows with lower highs (converging), or equal highs with equal lows (ranging), or higher highs with lower lows (expanding). Can still trade, but probability is lower than a clear uptrend.

Phil's simple rule: "Does the chart go from bottom-left to top-right? That's an uptrend." One messy candle or a temporary pullback does not change the trend — always look at the overall picture, zoomed out 10–20 years.

## Step 2: Support and Resistance Structure

After confirming the trend, identify clear **support and resistance levels** on the monthly chart.

### The Four Types (2x2 Matrix)

|            | Support (Floor)               | Resistance (Ceiling)           |
|------------|-------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| Horizontal | Horizontal Support (HS)       | Horizontal Resistance (HR)     |
| Diagonal   | Trend Line Support (TS)       | Trend Line Resistance (TR)     |

**Horizontal Support / Resistance** — connect two or more roughly equal lows (support) or highs (resistance) with a horizontal line. The touch points do not need to be at the exact same price — within a few dollars on the monthly is fine. Think in **zones, not exact prices**.

**Trend Line Support / Resistance** — connect two or more higher lows (support) or lower highs (resistance) with a diagonal line.

**The two-touch-point rule:** You always need at least two touch points to validate a level. One touch could be random. Two touches confirm the market respects the level.

**Exception:** All-time highs only need one touch point. An ATH is always resistance by definition — when price breaks above it and retests, that single previous ATH touch is sufficient to call it valid support after the break.

### What Makes a Level Invalid

A level is valid as long as price respects it. When price breaks through a level and multiple candles close below (for support) or above (for resistance) without recovering, **the level is invalidated — remove it from the chart.** Keeping invalid levels clutters your analysis.

One candle briefly piercing a level is not invalidation. Multiple candles closing through it is.

## Confluences

A **Confluence of Support (COS)** is when two independent support levels converge at the same price zone — for example, a horizontal support and a trend line support both pointing to the same area. The keyword is "independent" — two separate levels, not the same level counted twice.

Confluences dramatically increase the probability of a trade playing out. You have two independent reasons to expect a bounce. The more layers of confluence, the better the setup.

Phil prioritises COS setups over single-level setups whenever possible.

---

## Break and Retest

One of the most reliable and frequently occurring patterns in Phil's analysis.

**Bullish break and retest:** A resistance level is broken to the upside. Price comes back to retest the broken level. Previous resistance now acts as support. High probability of a bounce.

**Bearish break and retest:** A support level is broken to the downside. Price retests from below. Previous support now acts as resistance.

**All-time high break and retest** is an especially powerful version of the bullish setup — it happens extremely often with large-cap stocks in uptrends, and only requires that single ATH touch point to be valid.

Break and retest setups are even stronger when combined with confluences: broken resistance + trend line support at the same level = multiple independent reasons to enter.

## Chart Patterns

Patterns are **just a fancy way of trading support and resistance** — nothing more. Phil identifies them during Step 2 (Structure) on the monthly timeframe.

### Continuation Patterns (Monthly Timeframe)

**Symmetrical Triangle** — resistance trend line (lower highs) converging with support trend line (higher lows). Trade: buy at the support trend line.

**Ascending Triangle** — horizontal resistance with ascending support trend line. Bullish bias — primarily used for long entries.

**Descending Triangle** — horizontal support with descending resistance trend line. Bearish bias — primarily used for short entries.

**Channels** — two parallel trend lines (ascending, descending, or sideways). Always trade in the direction of the trend within the channel: buy support in ascending channels, sell resistance in descending channels. Never fight the trend inside a channel.

### Reversal Patterns (Daily Timeframe — Confirmation)

**Double Tops / Double Bottoms** — two roughly equal highs (double top = resistance) or lows (double bottom = support). These are reversal patterns, primarily used on the daily timeframe when looking for confirmation of a trend shift.

**Head and Shoulders** — left shoulder, head (fake-out beyond the level), right shoulder. Regular H&S is bearish; inverted H&S is bullish. The "head" is a liquidity grab that traps traders before the real reversal. Used primarily on the daily for confirmation, not on the monthly.

## How Technicals Confirm the Macro Thesis

Phil's rule: macro research is watchlist input only, never a trade trigger. The chart is always the final word. If a macro analyst is bullish on a sector but the monthly chart is in a clear downtrend with no confirmation signal, there is no trade.

When technicals and macro align — monthly uptrend, price at confluence support, bullish confirmation on the daily, and a macro tailwind — that is when you increase conviction, not position size beyond your rules.

---

## Common Mistakes Phil Warns About

- Trading on lower timeframes (daily, hourly) for the primary analysis — removes the edge that the monthly provides
- Using linear charts — distorts levels and patterns, especially on assets with large price histories
- Drawing levels that are unclear, subjective, or where the market trades back and forth — only draw obvious levels
- Adding too many indicators — they create false confidence and conflicting signals
- Treating a daily candle piercing monthly support as invalidation — it is not; only monthly candle closes matter
- Trying to identify the exact price of a level rather than a zone — always think in zones

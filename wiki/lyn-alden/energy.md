# Lyn Alden — Energy Sector

Energy is Lyn's most conviction-weighted sector across the 2022-2026 period. Her framework spans midstream pipelines (owned for income and stability), upstream producers (owned for commodity leverage), and oilfield services (owned as a cyclical play). The Hormuz crisis of 2026 was the most extreme validation of her thesis.

---

## The Core Energy Thesis

Lyn's energy conviction rests on several interlocking arguments that she established in 2022 and refined through 2026:

**1. A Decade of Underinvestment:**
From 2015-2021, the energy sector experienced a devastating capex bust. Oil prices collapsed in 2014-2015, and shale operators destroyed capital trying to survive. Many companies went bankrupt. Offshore drillers, deepwater producers, and oilfield services companies emerged from 2020 (the COVID demand collapse) with new owners, new management, cleaned-up balance sheets, and a fraction of prior capex commitments. The supply infrastructure for the next decade was systematically underfunded.

**2. Shale Maturity:**
US shale oil production was flatlining by 2023-2024. Existing wells deplete at 60-70% in year one; maintaining total production requires perpetual drilling acceleration. As the best sweet spots were drilled, the returns diminished. Production stopped growing meaningfully without significantly higher oil prices, which provided a floor under energy prices.

**3. Positive Carry:**
Lyn used the phrase "positive carry" for energy producers beginning in October 2023: even in a flat-to-down oil price environment, pipelines (7-8% yields), producers (high free cash flow at $70+ oil), and miners paid investors to wait. The dividend/distribution income provided time optionality — holders were being paid to be patient for the upside scenario.

**4. ESG Underinvestment / Valuation Anomaly:**
Post-2020, ESG mandates created artificial selling pressure on energy stocks from institutional funds. This drove energy to historically cheap valuations — EPD was trading at P/CF levels last seen in 2009-2011, despite a far stronger balance sheet. Lyn explicitly noted this in her October 2022 article.

**5. The Tail Risk of a Supply Crisis:**
Lyn consistently included in her energy thesis the possibility that geopolitical events could produce a sudden, severe supply shortage. She never predicted the Hormuz crisis specifically, but she built the energy overweight as partially a hedge against exactly this scenario. When it materialized, her portfolio was positioned.

## Enterprise Products Partners (EPD) — The Cornerstone

EPD is Lyn's single most frequently analyzed and praised holding. It is the largest US midstream MLP by asset value.

**Business:** EPD operates ~50,000 miles of pipeline plus storage, fractionation, and export terminals. It connects natural gas, NGL, and crude oil from production basins (Permian, Haynesville, Appalachian) to Gulf Coast export terminals and domestic consumers. The majority of its revenue is fee-based — it earns a toll regardless of the commodity price.

**Distribution record:** 25+ consecutive years of distribution growth as of 2024. No distribution cut even during COVID, the GFC, or the 2015 oil crash. This is exceptional among energy companies.

**Balance sheet:** Average debt duration of approximately 20 years, locked in at low rates. This is Lyn's favored comparison to show the difference between EPD and banks: EPD has long-dated, fixed-rate debt and fee-based variable revenue; banks have short-duration liabilities and rate-sensitive assets. When rates rise, EPD's debt stays cheap; its revenue (pipeline tolls tied to energy volumes) grows with economic activity.

**Valuation floor:** In October 2022, EPD was trading at P/CF multiples Lyn said she hadn't seen since 2009-2011. In February 2023 (barbell article), EPD was yielding 7.5% and trading at well under 10x distributable cash flow. In January 2024, Lyn published a full deep dive reconfirming that EPD remained one of the "most attractive risk-adjusted income investments on the market."

**Position sizing:** EPD is a cornerstone of both the defensive and income ends of Lyn's barbell. She has described it as the fixed-income alternative for a fiscal dominance era — more secure in real terms than long-duration Treasuries, higher yield, with growing distributions.

## Energy Transfer (ET) — The Higher-Yield Pipeline

ET is EPD's more aggressive sibling — a large midstream MLP with approximately 120,000 miles of pipeline (the largest US network). Lyn began covering it in June 2022 when she established a position.

In her November 2022 Energy Transfer update article, she showed ET yielding 8.5% at entry. The same article was where she first formally presented the domestic liquidity formula chart — illustrating how ET's price had been suppressed not by fundamentals but by the general liquidity withdrawal.

ET carries more debt and more history of distribution management than EPD (it cut its distribution in 2020, unlike EPD). For this reason, Lyn sized it smaller than EPD — as a supplementary income holding rather than a cornerstone. But its cheap valuation relative to its infrastructure asset base and recovering distribution was compelling.

She also owns AMLP (Alerian MLP ETF) as a diversified midstream basket, capturing the sector return without single-name risk.

## Upstream Producers: XOM, CVX, PBR, SU

**XOM (ExxonMobil):** Lyn's primary large-cap integrated oil holding. She published a deep dive in February 2024 reconfirming the thesis. XOM benefits from Hormuz through both higher crude prices and its LNG assets. The Pioneer acquisition (completed late 2023) massively expanded Permian Basin exposure. By 2026, XOM was consistently profitable at $65+ oil and generating strong free cash flow for buybacks.

**CVX (Chevron):** Also featured in a July 2024 deep dive. Chevron's Venezuela operations (Chevron is the only US oil major allowed to operate there despite sanctions) gave it unique exposure to potential production increase if political stabilization occurred. Also deep water Permian and Gulf of Mexico exposure. CVX was yielding 4-5% with 35+ years of consecutive dividend growth.

**PBR (Petrobras):** Brazil's state-controlled oil company, trading at single-digit P/E and double-digit dividend yields through much of 2022-2024. Lyn was long-term bullish on PBR throughout despite political risk from Lula's election, because the absolute valuation was so cheap that she viewed it as priced for distress that wasn't materializing. Brazil's offshore presalt fields (massive deepwater reserves with low production costs) make PBR one of the lowest-cost major oil producers globally.

**SU (Suncor):** Canadian oil sands. Lyn added exposure in 2023 (mentioned in SVB trend change article) for several reasons: oil sands are extremely long-lived assets (50-100 year lives vs 5-10 for shale wells), Suncor owns its own downstream refining and retail (reducing commodity price exposure), and Canadian oil sands production was relatively insulated from geopolitical supply disruptions because it's North American. Suncor specifically had repurchased enormous amounts of stock — reducing the share count dramatically — which amplified per-share metrics.

**CNQ (Canadian Natural Resources):** Also held in the Canadian oil sands complex for similar reasons to Suncor.

## Oilfield Services: VAL, TDW, SLB, HAL

Lyn entered oilfield services and offshore drilling in October 2023, viewing the sector as one of the most deeply discounted in the market.

**VAL (Valaris):** The world's largest fleet of offshore drilling rigs. Emerged from bankruptcy in 2021 with a clean balance sheet. Lyn's thesis from her March 2024 write-up: Valaris's fleet was held at prices well below replacement cost, shipbuilders had no capacity to build significant new rigs quickly, and offshore drilling would be a key source of new oil supply as shale matured. Day rates were rising. VAL had operational leverage — modest improvements in day rates and utilization drove large improvements in earnings.

After a rough 2023-2024 (stock fell despite the thesis remaining intact), VAL surged in late 2025 into early 2026 and was then acquired by Transocean in an all-stock deal. In her February 2026 article, Lyn sold VAL into the acquisition premium. "Valaris has reached my target upside potential... I no longer view it as having asymmetric risk/return aspects." The three oilfield services positions (SLB, HAL, VAL) were up 36% to 90% in less than five months from her September 2025 re-entry point.

**TDW (Tidewater):** Offshore support vessels — the boats that service offshore drilling platforms. Similar thesis to VAL: deepwater offshore activity recovering, vessel supply constrained, replacement costs high.

**SLB (Schlumberger) and HAL (Halliburton):** Large-cap oilfield services with global exposure. After selling VAL at the acquisition premium, Lyn shifted SLB and HAL to a "hold" given the 36-43% surges. She remained long-term constructive on both.

## The Hormuz Crisis Impact

February-March 2026: The Strait of Hormuz was effectively closed by Iran following US/Israeli strikes. ~20% of global oil and LNG production (roughly 20 million barrels/day of crude equivalent) transits through the Strait. The immediate impact:

- Three tankers struck within days of conflict beginning
- 200+ tankers anchored awaiting transit signals
- Maersk (world's largest container shipper) suspended all Hormuz transit
- Insurance coverage cancelled for ships passing through
- Egypt, South Korea, Slovenia began fuel rationing
- IEA head: Europe gets 75% of its jet fuel from Middle East sources
- US drawing from Strategic Petroleum Reserve

Energy prices spiked sharply. Lyn's energy portfolio was well positioned for exactly this scenario — EPD (protected by being North American infrastructure, not dependent on Hormuz), XOM/CVX (benefit from higher prices on their non-Hormuz production), and the upstream positions generally.

By April 2026, six weeks into Hormuz crisis, the impact was lingering: failed ceasefire attempts, Trump's Navy instituting a toll collection standoff (blocking ships paying Iran's yuan-denominated tolls), ongoing supply disruption. Lyn noted that even if the Strait fully reopened, the insurance market premium for Hormuz transit would remain elevated for years, structurally increasing the cost of that supply route.

# Lyn Alden: Energy

## Framework: Two Types of Commodities

Lyn divides commodity exposure into two categories based on inventory characteristics:

**Category 1 — High stockpile-to-flow commodities** (gold, silver, uranium, platinum): Existing above-ground inventory is large relative to annual new supply. These maintain monetary value but producers have limited pricing power because existing inventory can always be brought to market. Her preferred exposure: direct commodity ownership via physical or ETF/trust.

**Category 2 — Low stockpile-to-flow commodities** (oil, natural gas, copper, fertilizer, grains): Inventory is small relative to annual consumption. Supply disruptions quickly translate to price spikes. Her preferred exposure: the *producers, transporters, and service companies* rather than the commodity itself.

Energy falls entirely in Category 2. She prefers companies with strong balance sheets and low production breakeven points, so they can survive weak oil price periods while being positioned for upside when prices spike.

## Large Integrated Producers

**Exxon Mobil (XOM)** and **Chevron (CVX)** are Lyn's core producer holdings. She entered both during the 2020 energy trough when the sector was deeply out of favor.

Her XOM/CVX thesis: these companies survived the 2015/2016 oil crash and the 2020 Covid lockdowns — the two biggest stress events in modern energy history. They emerged with better balance sheets, reduced costs, and disciplined capital allocation. They have breakeven oil prices well below $50/barrel, meaning they generate free cash flow even in weak energy environments.

By January 2026, she noted XOM was "trading near its highs, paying out growing dividends" but at "somewhat richly priced" valuations. She maintained existing positions but was not looking to add significantly at those levels.

Chevron is notable for its Venezuela exposure. When the US conducted the January 2026 Caracas operation capturing Maduro, CVX was already uniquely positioned as one of the few US companies authorized to operate in Venezuela despite sanctions. Lyn viewed this as a modest long-term positive for CVX's production growth outlook.

## Offshore Drilling: Valaris (VAL) — A Full Cycle Story

Valaris is the most compelling individual energy trade Lyn described across the 62 articles. She initiated a "tiny position" in October 2023, building a thesis around:

- Largest offshore drilling fleet in the world, held at below-replacement-cost book values
- Emerged from 2020 bankruptcy restructuring with clean balance sheet and new management
- Offshore drilling in long-term structural undersupply (major shipbuilders cannot quickly build new rigs)
- Day rates rising sharply from multi-year lows as shale matured and offshore returned to prominence
- Clean balance sheet enabling large free cash flow to shareholders as day rates improved

The position had an "extremely rough year" in 2024-2025 as oil prices stayed weak and oilfield services broadly underperformed. Lyn re-examined the thesis during the September 2025 drawdown, noted that VAL (along with SLB and HAL) had not made a new low since April 2025, and recommended "topping up a small position at these inexpensive levels."

Within five months of that September 2025 bottom call, VAL was up 90%.

**February 2026**: Transocean (RIG) announced a merger with Valaris in an all-stock deal, with RIG controlling 53% and VAL 47% of the combined entity. The market liked the deal, VAL surged to all-time highs, and Lyn sold her entire position: "Valaris has reached my target upside potential and I no longer view it as having asymmetric risk/return aspects."

She replaced VAL in the newsletter portfolio and No Limits portfolio with ADBE (Adobe) and increased GUNR weighting. She would be open to re-entering Valaris (or the combined RIG/VAL entity) at a future discount, but exited fully on the merger news.

## Oilfield Services: SLB and HAL

After Valaris's exit, SLB (Schlumberger) and HAL (Halliburton) remained in her watchlist category. They had both returned 36-43% from the September 2025 bottoms by February 2026. She shifted these two from buy to neutral/hold at that point — they had recovered significantly from cheap levels but weren't yet at clear exit valuations.

Her ongoing view: oilfield services have narrow economic moats but benefit from structural offshore drilling revival over the 2025-2030 period. The VAL merger thesis plays out by reducing duplicate overhead across combined fleets.

## Midstream: Enterprise Products Partners (EPD)

EPD is arguably Lyn's most consistent energy recommendation across the entire article corpus. She characterizes it as "my preferred choice" in midstream repeatedly.

Key attributes:
- ~7% yield throughout 2025-2026
- 25 consecutive years of distribution growth (gold standard in MLPs)
- Transports crude oil, natural gas, NGLs, and refined products
- Makes money from volumes, not oil prices — reduces commodity price sensitivity
- Partnership structure (K-1 tax form, more complex for international investors and some retirement accounts) but tax-advantaged for domestic taxable accounts

The knock: partnership structure limits audience. But for domestic investors in taxable accounts, EPD's 7% yield plus volume-driven growth makes it one of the highest-quality income instruments in her portfolios.

**ONEOK (OKE)**: Added to her watchlist in January 2026 after its valuation came off temporarily elevated levels. 5.5% dividend yield, 25 consecutive years of growth, corporation (not partnership), one of the largest midstream asset footprints in the country. She viewed OKE as a slightly less preferred alternative to EPD for investors who can't hold K-1 instruments.

## The Hormuz Crisis (March-May 2026)

The single biggest macro event in the 62-article corpus for energy markets. Timeline:

**March 1, 2026**: US and Israel launched strikes on Iran "with a specific intention toward regime change and killing Iran's supreme leader." Khamenei was reported killed.

**Strait of Hormuz**: Iran announced closure. Approximately 20% of global oil and LNG production passes through the Strait. Iran offered passage to "non-US-allied" ships but required extensive IRGC vetting and, eventually, toll payment in yuan. In practice, throughput collapsed — only Iran-aligned ships passed.

**March 15 (2 weeks in)**: Worse than Lyn's initial base case. Ships still not passing in meaningful numbers. Three tankers had been struck. Maersk suspended operations. Dubai physical oil swaps trading near $140 while futures were around $100.

**March 29 (4 weeks in)**: Polymarket odds of oil reaching $150/barrel: 14% by end of March, 33% by end of June. Lyn described this as "potentially a Covid-19 level shock if it persists for months." Multiple countries rationing fuel: South Korea (license plate-based driving restrictions), Egypt (mandatory 9pm business closing hours), Slovenia (first EU fuel rationing).

**April 12 (6 weeks in)**: Ceasefire attempted and broken within the first day. US announced Navy would begin blocking the Strait as well (retaliating against Iran's tolling system). Oil and gasoline prices continued rising. US CPI for March came in hot, constraining Fed from easing.

**April 25 (8 weeks in)**: Ceasefire discussions with "little tangible progress." Mild demand destruction occurring. US drew down Strategic Petroleum Reserve. Europe's jet fuel (75% sourced from Middle East) essentially running at near-zero supply from regional refiners.

**May 10 (11 weeks in)**: Crisis ongoing but energy prices slightly eased from peak levels. Combination of demand destruction and SPR releases softened aggregate prices below the worst-case scenarios feared at week 4. No major humanitarian crisis yet but situation remained unresolved.

Lyn's investment takeaway: she held substantial energy exposure throughout and viewed the Hormuz closure as exactly the tail risk scenario she had been positioned for. She explicitly cited this as "part of why I have a significant amount of energy producers in the model portfolios — there are many tail risk scenarios, including primarily this one, that can drive energy prices higher." 

She did not add new energy positions during the crisis (cautioning against "chasing at elevated prices") but maintained all existing positions and let cash accumulate for optionality.

## Gold/Oil Ratio Implication for Energy

Lyn highlighted that the gold-to-oil ratio reached record highs in December 2025. Her inference: if the relationship between gold and oil ultimately normalizes from historical extremes, oil-linked assets look very cheap relative to precious metals. This is not a guarantee of oil outperformance but suggests substantial embedded value in energy producers even if oil price recovery is modest.

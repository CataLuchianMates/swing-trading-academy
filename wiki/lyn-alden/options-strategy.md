# Lyn Alden — Options Strategy

Lyn uses options in a disciplined, income-oriented way that integrates directly with her macro views and equity framework. She does not speculate with options. Every options position she discusses serves one of three purposes: (1) generating income while waiting to buy at lower prices, (2) capturing premium on positions she's already happy to own, or (3) monetizing known exit points.

---

## Cash-Secured Puts — The Primary Tool

Cash-secured puts are Lyn's most frequently used options strategy. The mechanics:

**Sell a put option** on a stock you're willing to own at a price you find attractive. The option obligates you to buy 100 shares at the strike price if the stock falls below it by expiration. In exchange, you receive the premium upfront.

**Required capital:** You must hold enough cash to cover the potential purchase (the "cash-secured" part). This prevents leverage — you're not writing puts you can't honor.

**Outcomes:**
- Stock stays above strike: Put expires worthless, you keep the premium. This is the income-generating outcome.
- Stock falls below strike: You're assigned — forced to buy 100 shares at the strike. But this is a price you chose deliberately, so the outcome is owning a stock you wanted to own, at a price you chose, with the premium reducing your effective cost basis.

Lyn's framing: "It's like being paid to leave a limit order." If she wants to buy a stock at $X, she sells a put at $X rather than just entering a limit buy order — she gets paid the premium for the commitment.

**Date:** March 2025 (referenced in her article)
**Underlying:** MicroStrategy (MSTR), then trading at approximately $300
**Strike:** $250
**Expiration:** June 2025 (approximately 3 months out)
**Premium received:** Approximately $30/share (~10-12% of the $250 strike)

**Analysis:**
- If MSTR stays above $250 through June 2025: Lyn keeps $30/share premium = ~12% return in 3 months (~48% annualized if repeatable)
- If MSTR falls below $250: She buys at $250 minus $30 premium = effective buy-in of $220/share
- At $220, she's buying MSTR at a discount to the ~$250 strike, and with MSTR holding >330,000 BTC at the time, she knows the NAV support level

This is a high-volatility stock with an unusual structure (it's primarily a Bitcoin holding company), which is why the premium was so large. Lyn specifically chose a strike that placed the effective buy-in price at a meaningful premium to the estimated NAV of MSTR's Bitcoin holdings — providing additional downside protection.

**What this illustrates about her framework:** She was willing to be assigned at $220 because that was below NAV levels at 2024-2025 Bitcoin prices. The put was structured so that assignment was actually a good outcome, not just a fallback.

## GDX Covered Calls — Exit Via Assignment

Lyn's February 2026 article mentioned that her GDX (VanEck Gold Miners ETF) position was "automatically exited with a major gain due to covered calls being exercised amid soaring prices."

This illustrates the covered call strategy:

**Covered call mechanics:** If you own 100 shares, you can sell a call option giving someone else the right to buy those shares from you at the strike price. In exchange, you receive premium. If the stock rises above the strike, your shares get called away (you sell them at the strike to the call buyer). If it stays below the strike, you keep shares + premium.

**Lyn's GDX outcome:** Gold and gold miners surged dramatically in late 2025-early 2026. She had been selling covered calls against GDX to generate additional income. When gold surged, the calls were exercised — she was "called out" of her GDX position at her covered call strike prices, selling into the strength. The result: a "major gain" on GDX plus all the premiums collected over the period.

**The tradeoff:** Covered calls cap upside. If GDX had continued rising another 50% past her strike, she wouldn't have participated in that move. But her actual outcome was a major gain, and she is neutral on gold at current prices, so being exited via covered calls was actually her preferred outcome.

## When She Doesn't Use Options

Lyn is explicit that her options usage is narrow. She does not:

1. **Buy puts as hedges:** Buying options costs premium. Lyn prefers to be structurally positioned for her base case rather than paying ongoing insurance costs. If her base case is right, the insurance was wasted money. If she's wrong, the loss was already happening in the underlying positions anyway.

2. **Buy calls for leveraged upside:** Speculative call buying is not part of her framework. She prefers owning the underlying asset directly if she wants upside exposure.

3. **Sell naked puts:** All her put selling is cash-secured. She never puts herself in a position where assignment would force her to use capital she doesn't have.

4. **Trade short-dated options:** Her options are consistently 2-6 months out — long enough for the fundamental thesis to play out, short enough that time decay is meaningful.

## The Philosophy Behind the Income-First Approach

Lyn's options strategy reflects her broader portfolio philosophy: generate income from every position rather than concentrating on capital gains alone.

In a fiscal dominance era, she argues, the goal is to generate enough real income to maintain purchasing power, with capital gains as the bonus rather than the primary objective. This is why:

- EPD at 7.5% yield and dividend growth is a "better bond" than a Treasury
- A 12% annualized put premium on MSTR beats the nominal yield on any investment-grade bond
- Covered calls on a fully-valued position convert unrealized gain into realized premium income

In options terms, this means she is consistently a premium seller (selling optionality to others) rather than a premium buyer. Premium sellers are short volatility — they profit when volatility is elevated at entry (collecting high premium) and normalizes over time. Lyn's macro framing helps her time these entries: when fear is elevated (Hormuz crisis, 2023 SVB moment, 2022 FTX), premiums spike and selling puts becomes particularly attractive.

## Position Sizing in Options

Lyn's cash-secured put sizing is implicitly determined by her willingness to own the underlying. If she'd be comfortable putting 3% of her portfolio into a stock at the strike price, she might write one or two put contracts representing that capital commitment. She never writes puts that, if assigned, would create a position too large for her risk tolerance.

She explicitly warns: "For risk management purposes, I wouldn't go all-in on any of them, and would limit overall bank exposure to a reasonable level" (from her USB deep dive). The same logic applies to options — concentration risk from a put position that gets assigned is the same risk as if she'd bought the stock directly.

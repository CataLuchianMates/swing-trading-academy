# Lyn Alden — USD and Liquidity Thesis

*Last updated: May 2026. Based on 62 premium newsletter articles (January 2024 – May 2026).*

---

## The Primary Indicator: Global M2

The single most important macro indicator in Lyn Alden's framework is dollar-denominated global M2 — the total broad money supply of the world measured in US dollars. The correlation between global M2 and bitcoin was documented quantitatively in a September 2024 co-published paper with Sam Callahan (83% correlation). But global M2 is also the primary driver of all risk assets, not just bitcoin. When global M2 is expanding, risk assets broadly rise; when it contracts or stalls, risk assets fall.

The key insight is that global M2 is not controlled by the Fed alone. It is affected by: Fed balance sheet changes (adds or removes base money), TGA drawdowns or refills (Treasury cash account flowing out to or from the banking system), RRP drain or fill (reverse repo facility recycling excess bank reserves), bank lending growth or contraction in the US, and equivalent monetary actions in China, Japan, Europe, and other major economies.

## The Domestic Liquidity Trifecta

For the US specifically, Lyn tracks what she calls the domestic liquidity trifecta:

**1. Fed Balance Sheet** — When the Fed expands (QE or balance sheet growth), it creates new bank reserves and adds base liquidity. When the Fed contracts (QT), it destroys reserves and removes base liquidity. The Fed ended QT in December 2025 after bank reserves fell into the "tight" zone (below 12% of system assets). It then shifted to adding $40B/month in Treasury purchases through April 2026, and announced a baseline of $20-25B/month indefinitely thereafter.

**2. Treasury General Account (TGA)** — The Treasury's cash account at the Fed. When Treasury borrows money and accumulates cash in the TGA, it sucks reserves from the banking system (negative for liquidity). When the Treasury draws down the TGA (debt ceiling standoffs, spending exceeding borrowing), reserves flow back to banks (positive for liquidity). In summer 2025, the TGA was drawn down to $370B (positive). In October 2025, a government shutdown caused an overshoot refill to $958B (negative). By December 2025, this overfill combined with ongoing QT created the acute repo market strains that forced the Fed's hand.

**3. Overnight Reverse Repo (RRP)** — From 2021 to 2024, the Fed's overnight reverse repo facility absorbed excess bank reserves (negative for liquidity when rising). As the RRP drained from ~$2.4T (late 2022) to near-zero (2024-2025), those reserves flowed back into the banking system (a persistent pro-liquidity tailwind). By mid-2025, the RRP was essentially depleted, removing this tailwind from the system. No more easy liquidity contributions from RRP drain after that point.

## The Q4 2025 Liquidity Crisis (And How Lyn Predicted It)

This is one of the clearest examples of Lyn's forecasting precision across the 62 articles. In August 2025, she explicitly wrote that domestic liquidity would enter "somewhat tight territory" in Q4 2025 as the TGA refilled toward $850B and QT continued — and that this would force the Fed to end QT either "in late 2025 or early 2026." She then tracked the timeline in real time:

- September 2025: SOFR spikes above IORB for the first time in years. Repo facility begins being used persistently. This is the signal.
- October 2025: TGA overfills to $958B. Bank cash at 12.6%, hitting the "ample" threshold floor.
- October 29, 2025: Fed announces QT ends in December.
- December 10, 2025: Fed ends QT and announces balance sheet expansion at $40B/month through tax season.
- January 2026: Repo usage disappears. SOFR-IORB spread begins normalizing.

The entire sequence played out within weeks of Lyn's August 2025 prediction. The mechanism was almost identical to September 2019 when the same TGA refill + QT combination caused repo market seizures and forced the Fed to restart balance sheet growth.

## SOFR-IORB Spread as Liquidity Stress Signal

Lyn uses the spread between SOFR (Secured Overnight Financing Rate) and IORB (Interest on Reserve Balances) as a real-time liquidity stress indicator. When SOFR is consistently above IORB, it means banks are paying more to borrow cash overnight in the market than they earn holding reserves at the Fed — a structural shortage signal, not just end-of-quarter window dressing. This spread was elevated from September 2025 through approximately January 2026, then normalized as the Fed's new balance sheet purchases added reserves. By April 2026, during the Hormuz crisis, the spread briefly spiked again then normalized, suggesting adequate domestic liquidity despite the external energy shock.

## The "Gradual Print" Baseline (2026 Onward)

From December 2025 onward, Lyn uses the phrase "gradual print" to describe the new Fed operating regime. The math: US nominal GDP grows at roughly $1.5T per year, the Fed wants to maintain its balance sheet at ~20% of GDP, so the baseline balance sheet growth rate is ~$300B/year, or $25B/month. This is not QE in the traditional stimulus sense — it's a structural necessity to keep bank reserves ample as the economy grows. Whether the next Fed chair is named Kevin Warsh (nominated February 2026) or anyone else, Lyn's base case is that this rate of balance sheet expansion continues indefinitely, because any attempt to meaningfully shrink the balance sheet would trigger a repeat of the September 2019 or Q4 2025 repo crises.

The projections: Fed balance sheet was $6,556B at end of 2025. Adding $40B/month for 5 months (Dec-Apr) and then $25B/month for 8 months (May-Dec) brings the total to approximately $7T by end of 2026. Lyn treats this as the minimum baseline, with the actual number likely higher if any form of economic stimulus becomes necessary.

## SLR (Supplementary Leverage Ratio) Reform

In June 2025, US banking regulators proposed a 1.5% reduction in bank SLR requirements, taking the bank SLR from 5% down to 3.5-4.5%. The SLR counts Treasury securities as part of the leverage calculation, which creates an artificial disincentive for banks to hold Treasuries (since it crowds out their capacity for other assets). A reduction would allow banks to hold more Treasuries without regulatory penalty.

Lyn's interpretation: SLR reform is pro-liquidity and pro-Treasury because it creates structural demand for government bonds from the banking sector. Banks currently hold over $4 trillion in Treasuries; SLR reform could increase this capacity. It also means the Fed doesn't need to own as many Treasuries directly (reducing the Fed's balance sheet requirement around the margins). Combined with the described ATI (T-bill issuance) mechanic, SLR reform is one of the tools Lyn identified that Kevin Warsh might use to modestly reduce the Fed balance sheet without causing liquidity problems.

## Dollar Cycle Thesis

The dollar cycle is a central organizing principle for Lyn's asset allocation calls. The primary relationship: a rising dollar is negative for global liquidity and emerging markets; a falling dollar is positive.

**2022:** Dollar spikes to 115 DXY on Fed rate hike cycle. EM currencies collapse. Bitcoin falls 75%. EM equities crater.

**2023-2024:** Dollar moderates from 115 to 102-104 range. EM assets begin stabilizing.

**2025:** Dollar falls from 110 (early 2025) to 97 (mid-2025) to below 95 (late 2025-early 2026). Lyn's base case is a 3-5 year dollar weakening cycle similar to 2003-2007, driven by: large US fiscal deficits, Fed balance sheet expansion, capital inflows slowing as US equity valuations peak relative to EM, and deliberate Treasury policy to ease financial conditions.

As of February 2026, the dollar briefly touched its lowest level since late 2021. In March 2026, the Iran war briefly pushed it back above 100 (flight to safety demand), but the structural trend remained downward in Lyn's view.

**USD/CNY specifically:** Lyn expected the top to be in for the dollar versus the Chinese yuan by late 2025, arguing that the USD/CNY ratio closely tracked the Fed's tightening cycle. With the Fed now easing and the Chinese economy stabilizing, the ratio was expected to gradually decline.

## Japan as a Global Liquidity Variable

From the December 2025 article onward, Japan became a significant variable in Lyn's global liquidity framework. Japan's central bank had suppressed rates near zero for decades through yield curve control (YCC). When the Bank of Japan began normalizing rates in 2024-2025, Japanese 10-year yields went "rather vertical" — rising to multi-decade highs by late 2025 and continuing to fresh highs through Q1 2026.

The implications are two-fold. Positive: Japanese banks now earn real interest income on their portfolios, which improves their financial health (SMFG and other major Japanese banks were cited as performing well). Negative: rising Japanese rates cause global carry trade unwinding (yen was the world's main funding currency for leveraged positions), and as yen strengthens and Japanese rates rise, global capital repatriates toward Japan and away from other assets. This is one reason Lyn was cautious about bitcoin and global risk assets in Q4 2025 — the Fed-induced liquidity tightness and the Bank of Japan tightening were a "one-two punch" against liquidity-sensitive assets.

## Private Credit and Shadow Banking: Liquidity Spillover Risk

One of the most comprehensive analyses Lyn provides across multiple reports (October 2025, March 2026) addresses whether the growing private credit industry represents a systemic risk to the banking sector comparable to 2008's subprime mortgage crisis. Her conclusion: no, for reasons that are structural and quantitative.

Banks have $1.9T in lending exposure to non-deposit financial institutions (NDFIs) as of March 2026. As a share of total bank assets ($24.5T), this is 7-8%. Even in a catastrophic scenario where 50% of all NDFI loans default, the loss would be roughly $950B — large but absorbable given $2.6T in aggregate bank equity capital. The key contrast with 2008: subprime mortgages totaled $10.8T against only $12.2T in total bank assets. NDFI exposure today is simply not in that ballpark.

More importantly, banks hold 32% of assets in cash and Treasuries (nominally risk-free) — nearly a record high versus only 13% in 2007. The banking system entered this period of elevated private credit risk in fundamentally better shape than it entered the GFC.

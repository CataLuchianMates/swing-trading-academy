## The Dollar's Structural Role

The US dollar is the world's primary unit of account for cross-border debt. This creates a structural dynamic that no other currency experiences:

- Trillions in dollar-denominated liabilities exist **outside** the United States
- When the dollar strengthens, those liabilities appreciate relative to foreign cash flows → EM squeeze
- When the dollar weakens, those liabilities shrink relative to foreign cash flows → global liquidity expansion

This is why DXY is Lyn's #1 global liquidity indicator. A falling DXY is more stimulative for global growth than any single central bank action. [Emerging Markets Breakout, 2026-05-10]

**Historical dollar cycles:**
- Strong dollar (1980–1985, 1995–2001, 2011–2022): US growth/tech dominates; EM/commodities underperform
- Weak dollar (1971–1980, 2002–2010): EM/commodities/gold outperform; US growth stagnates
- Current: Dollar peaked ~2022 and is in early-stage weakening trend as of 2025–2026

## Fed Balance Sheet — "The Gradual Print"

### QT Cycle and Its End

The Fed began quantitative tightening in 2022 to fight inflation. Lyn tracked its terminal approach through the SOFR-IORB spread and repo usage:

1. **Sept 2025**: Fed's repo facility used persistently — not just quarter-end spikes. SOFR-IORB spread elevated. Banks competing for reserves.
2. **Oct 14, 2025**: Powell publicly acknowledged liquidity constraints.
3. **Oct 29, 2025**: QT officially ended.
4. **Dec 10, 2025**: FOMC announced resumption of balance sheet growth — $40B/month T-bill purchases initially. "Completely separate from monetary policy — just keeping ample reserves."

> "Banks are near the low end of their ample reserve range, which is why the Fed has shifted toward balance sheet growth." [The Boom in AI Agents, 2026-02-01]

**Baseline ongoing need:** ~$25B/month in balance sheet expansion to maintain ample reserves as the banking system grows. This is structural, not cyclical — Lyn calls it **"the gradual print."**

## SLR — Supplementary Leverage Ratio

The Supplementary Leverage Ratio is a bank capital regulation that requires large banks to hold capital against **all** assets, including US Treasuries (which are otherwise risk-free). This creates a perverse incentive: holding more Treasuries forces banks to hold more capital, reducing their ability to lend.

**Why this matters for liquidity:**
- If SLR is loosened, banks can hold more Treasuries without capital penalty
- This makes banks natural buyers of Treasury supply, reducing pressure on the Fed to absorb it
- It also frees up capital for lending → pro-liquidity overall

**Timeline:**
- SLR was temporarily relaxed in 2020 (COVID crisis), then reinstated in 2021
- By June 2025, regulators announced a 1.5 percentage point reduction — from ~5% down to ~3.5–4.5% for large banks. Lyn called this the "first tangible update" on an issue she had been tracking.
- This was one of Warsh's preferred tools for shrinking the Fed balance sheet at the margin

[Fed Dilemmas and SLR Updates, 2025-06-22]

## Treasury Dynamics

### Yellen vs. Bessent — T-Bill Issuance

An underappreciated liquidity variable: **what percentage of federal debt is issued as short-term T-bills vs. long-duration bonds.**

- Pre-COVID normal: ~15% T-bills
- Yellen (2023–2025): ~22% T-bills — pro-liquidity because it limits long-duration supply
- Bessent (2025–2026): Ironically continued Yellen's 22% T-bill approach despite criticizing it previously

More T-bills = more frequent refinancing = government sensitivity to rate changes, but also less upward pressure on long-term rates. Lyn interprets Bessent's continuation of this approach as evidence that reducing long-term yields is the priority over normalization. [Emerging Markets Breakout, 2026-05-10]

### TGA (Treasury General Account)

The TGA is the federal government's cash account at the Fed. When Treasury builds cash (raises TGA), it drains liquidity from the banking system. When it spends/draws down, it injects liquidity.

After the "One Big Beautiful Bill" (July 2025) raised the debt ceiling by $4–5T, Treasury could begin refilling the TGA. Lyn tracked this as a liquidity drain headwind in August 2025 ($850B refill target). The TGA level rising above normal in early 2026 was temporarily offsetting the Fed's balance sheet expansion. [Updates on Tariffs, Liquidity, and Strategy, 2025-08-03; The Boom in AI Agents, 2026-02-01]

## Liquidity Transmission to Asset Prices

Lyn's core empirical observation: **global M2 (aggregate money supply across major economies) has a ~0.9 correlation with bitcoin on 12-month timeframes.** This relationship holds because:
1. New money flows first into the most liquid, globally-accessible risk assets
2. Bitcoin is 24/7, globally accessible, and has no credit risk
3. Gold, then EM equities, then US equities follow in sequence as liquidity expands

The transmission sequence:
- Fed/Treasury actions → bank reserve levels → interbank lending → broad money → asset prices

[A Bank and Tech Barbell, 2025-05-25]

## Repo Market as Canary

Lyn watches the Fed's repo facility (daily) and FIMA repo facility as early warning systems for liquidity stress:

- **Normal**: Zero or near-zero usage of standing repo
- **Mild stress**: $5–20B/day usage (seen in Feb 2026 briefly)
- **Crisis onset**: Sustained heavy usage → Fed forced to act

During the Hormuz Crisis (March–May 2026), global stress rose but the Fed's facilities absorbed it with no acute domestic crisis. UAE and other Gulf nations requested dollar swap lines, but the FIMA repo facility already covered most of that need. [Hormuz, Liquidity, and Upcoming Investments, 2026-04-12; Fiscal Re-Acceleration, 2026-04-25]

## Fed Dilemma — Trapped Between Inflation and Fiscal

The Fed faces a structural dilemma that Lyn has tracked across multiple reports:

- **Can't cut aggressively**: Inflation remains above 2% target; energy price surges (Hormuz) make it worse
- **Can't raise or hold tight indefinitely**: Large fiscal deficits mean the government's interest expense compounds; tight money doesn't reduce spending, it just makes it more expensive and shifts who bears the cost
- **Can't run QT to completion**: Banking system hits ample reserve floor, repo stress emerges

The result: the Fed is effectively **trapped in a middle path** — mild balance sheet growth, moderate rates, and slow financial repression. This is the "gradual print" regime and it's Lyn's base case for multi-year duration.

> "The Fed is not going to take actions that result in treasury security dysfunction, repo illiquidity, or a material slowdown in bank lending and deposit growth." [The Boom in AI Agents, 2026-02-01]

## Macro View

**The Strait of Hormuz**

As we wrap up the 8th week of the closure of the Strait of Hormuz, it remains the largest variable in the global macroeconomic situation.

Since my prior report, military activity has gone down substantially amid ceasefire discussions, but with little tangible progress between US and Iran.

Demand destruction is taking hold, where many poorer countries experience energy curfews, mild-to-moderate energy rationing, and so forth. Fertilizer and food prices are up. Consumers and businesses in the US and other wealthy nations are generally avoiding shortages, but are paying higher prices, which eats into potential spending elsewhere in the economy and contributes to record-low consumer sentiment.

The longer the issue drags on, the more impactful it is likely to become.

Since the start of April, the US began drawing down some of its strategic petroleum reserve to offset the issue. It’s a small total number so far, but large on a weekly change basis:

![SPR Stock](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-25-spr-stock2.png)

![SPR Change](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-25-spr-change2.png)

China, the US, and Japan all have rather sizable reserves. On the other hand, Europe’s is pretty small relative to their economic and population size, even though they still do import most of theirs like Japan does.

![Petroleum Reserves](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-25-international-petroleum-reserves.png)

![Petroleum Reserves](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-25-china-vs-us-reserves.png)

Another big issue is jet fuel. A barrel of oil is ultimately refined into a number of different types of fuel, and one of them is kerosene, used for air travel. That particular type of fuel is running into more shortages than other types at this early stage, resulting in many airlines cancelling unprofitable flight routes.

We often hear the number that about 20% of the world’s oil and other liquid fuels passes through the Strait of Hormuz. However, that’s an aggregate number, and there are many nuances within it. The head of the International Energy Agency, for example, has pointed out that Europe gets 75% of its jet fuel from the Middle East:

> Middle East refineries provide Europe with around 75% of Europe’s jet fuel but production from those facilities “is basically now almost zero,” he added.
>
> –[CNBC, April 23rd 2026](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/23/europe-jet-fuel-shortage-airlines-cut-flights.html)

Additionally, jet fuel demand rises in the summer months, meaning that the supply-demand mismatch has the potential to sharply worsen if the supply-side Hormuz situation remains unresolved by then.

**Liquidity Check-Up**

US domestic liquidity and global liquidity conditions remain quite resilient despite the ongoing energy crisis.

Back in December 2025, when bank reserve levels grew tight and domestic liquidity shortages were showing up, the Fed shifted from balance sheet reduction to gradual balance sheet expansion, to raise the aggregate level of reserves for banks on a structural basis. And specifically, the Fed determined that $20-$25 billion in monthly reserve creation was needed indefinitely, but purposely erred higher to $40 billion per month until tax day. This was to provide an extra liquidity buffer so that even when the April tax week liquidity suck occurs, banks would still have plenty of liquidity.

And indeed, from Powell’s mid-December speech into mid-April, they averaged ::drumroll:: $41.5 billion in balance sheet growth per month over the span of four months.

![Fed Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-25-fed-balance-sheet.png)

These were Fed chairman Powell’s remarks on the subject:

> As detailed in a statement released today by the Federal Reserve Bank of New York, reserve management purchases will amount to $40 billion in the first month and may remain elevated for a few months to alleviate expected near-term pressures in money markets. Thereafter, we expect the size of reserve management purchases to decline, though the actual pace will depend on market conditions.
>
> […]
>
> So, you know, we announced that we’re resuming reserve management purchases. That is completely separate from monetary policy. It’s just we need to keep an ample supply of reserves out there. Why so big? The answer to that is, you know, if you look ahead, you will see that April 15th is coming up, and our framework is such that we want to have ample reserves even at times when reserves are at a low level temporarily. So that’s what happens on Tax Day. People pay a lot of money to the government, reserves drop sharply and temporarily. So this seasonal buildup that we’ll see in the next few months was going to happen anyway. It was going to happen because April 15th is April 15th. There’s also a secular ongoing growth of the balance sheet. We have to keep reserves, call it, constant as a—as it relates to the banking system or to the whole economy. And that alone calls for us to increase about $20 or $25 billion per month.
>
> -Jerome Powell, December 10th 2025 Press Conference

Powell’s term as Fed chairman ends on May 15th, 2026. However, his term as a Fed governor goes until January 31, 2028 unless he elects to resign early (as outgoing chairmen often do) or is removed for cause.

Kevin Warsh, who was previously on the Fed board, has been nominated by President Trump to be the next Fed chairman. He’s currently in the process of going through the Senate confirmation.

Warsh has advocated for a smaller Fed balance sheet, but he’s unlikely to achieve that at scale, and if he does, it’ll mainly be in a liquidity-neutral way. I outlined the reasons and scenarios for that in my February 2026 public newsletter:

> **Below-Baseline Scenarios**
>
> President Trump has announced that his nominee for the next Fed chairman (a term that starts in May 2026) will be Kevin Warsh. The U.S. Senate would have to confirm it at that time to make it official.
>
> Warsh is on record saying he is in favor of a smaller Fed balance sheet, which is in contrast to the Fed’s existing forecasts. Therefore, it needs to be asked: could a shake-up in leadership at the Fed change their balance sheet composition?
>
> The answer is likely “not much” but with some caveats.
>
> -The chairman is powerful, but he’s only one of 12 votes on the Federal Open Market Committee, so he doesn’t unilaterally set policy.
>
> -The Fed and other federal agencies could further ease bank regulations, allowing them to hold more treasuries without negatively impacting their regulatory leverage ratios. That means the Fed can own fewer treasuries.
>
> -The Fed could take some steps to discourage excess reserve accumulation at banks, thereby distributing the reserves that are in the system a bit more evenly. This could let them shrink their balance sheet by perhaps a few hundred billion dollars.
>
> -The Treasury Department could increase its average debt duration and shrink its average cash account size (known as the Treasury General Account). A smaller cash account size would give more liquidity back to the banking system, enabling a smaller Fed balance sheet by perhaps a few hundred billion dollars. However, the government generally doesn’t want higher long-term interest rates, so they’re sensitive about how much supply of long-duration debt they issue.
>
> In theory, if all of those things were done, the Fed’s balance sheet could go down by somewhere around a trillion dollars. However, the chances of that happening are low.
>
> And importantly, all of those measures just move liquidity around. Banks buying more treasuries on fractional reserve is functionally similar to the Fed buying them with new reserves. And moving reserves around and reducing the Treasury General Account basically result in efficiency gains: doing more with less.
>
> At the end of the day, the Fed is going to optimize for 1) maintaining bank lending and broad money creation at a moderate pace, 2) ensuring the treasury security market remains liquid, and 3) ensuring that the repo market remains liquid and that any liquidity stresses that do pop up don’t remain acute for very long.
>
> -Lyn Alden, February 8th 2026 newsletter

Current usage of the Fed’s standing repo facility remains muted, and the SOFR-IORB spread is fine as well, which together suggest that domestic liquidity conditions are fine at the moment.

Looking globally, the dollar index remains choppy, and there aren’t really any signs of an acute global liquidity shortage.

![DXY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-25-dxy.png)

Last week, multiple nations including the UAE that were hard-hit by this war on Iran [asked for swap lines](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/22/iran-war-treasury-uae-scott-bessent-currency-swaps.html) with the United States, and many people on social media took that as a sign of acute dollar shortage liquidity problems.

Under normal conditions, only five major central banks have standing swap lines with the US (Bank of England, European Central Bank, Bank of Japan, Swiss National Bank, and Bank of Canada), but others are occasionally given swap lines during severely adverse conditions (such as Covid, where up to 14 countries had swap lines temporarily).

The reasons I don’t consider these recent swap lines headlines to be as serious as many took them to be are twofold. First, the UAE and most other nations that reportedly asked for swap lines have high levels of foreign-exchange reserves. They could trim those reserves to backstop their currencies. Second, the Fed already has a standing “Foreign and International Monetary Authorities (FIMA) repo facility”, which lets foreign central banks pledge their treasuries as collateral for dollars, rather than sell them. They put this in place during Covid and it reduces the need for swap lines in general:

> The Federal Reserve established a repurchase agreement facility for foreign and international monetary authorities (FIMA Repo Facility). By creating a backstop source of temporary dollar liquidity for FIMA account holders, the facility can help address pressures in global dollar funding markets that could otherwise affect financial market conditions in the United States. Its role as a liquidity backstop also helps to support the smooth functioning of financial markets more generally.
>
> The FIMA Repo Facility allows FIMA account holders, which consist of central banks and other international monetary authorities with accounts at the Federal Reserve Bank of New York, to enter into repurchase agreements with the Federal Reserve. In these transactions, approved FIMA account holders temporarily exchange their U.S. Treasury securities held with the Federal Reserve for U.S. dollars, which can then be made available to institutions in their jurisdictions. This facility provides, at a backstop rate, an alternative temporary source of U.S. dollars for foreign official holders of Treasury securities other than sales of the securities in the open market. A temporary FIMA Repo Facility was established March 31, 2020, and the facility was made a standing facility on July 28, 2021.
>
> –[Federal Reserve](https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/fima-repo-facility.htm)

These types of swap lines and repo facilities are not provided to the world out of benevolence. Rather, they are provided to the world so that international entities don’t have to rapidly sell treasuries and cause market disruption to our bond market whenever they have temporary extra liquidity needs. They’re standing facilities that exist primarily for this purpose.

As I’ve said before, certain smaller developing countries could certainly be facing acute or semi-acute dollar shortages at the moment, and likely are. However, those are local problems rather than ones that rise up to a global macro level, as would be the case when larger, wealthier nations run into shortages.

## Fiscal Re-Acceleration

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act of July 2025 extended a number of tax cuts and provided some additional cuts and exemptions. As a result, tax return payments are larger than average here during tax season, and those who have payable taxes generally owe a bit less. This is both a minor fiscal boost and a minor liquidity boost, and it’s kicking in here in 2026.

Unfortunately, it’s basically offset for most people by sharply higher fuel costs at the moment.

One of the biggest components of my analysis over the past 6-7 years has been about the growing impact of fiscal dominance in the United States. Nothing stops this (fiscal) train.

The tangible takeaway from it is that I would consistently predict 1) large fiscal deficits despite verbal attempts by policymakers to reduce them and 2) that those large fiscal deficits would be greatly beneficial to asset prices despite other bearish concerns along the way.

Now, that doesn’t mean that valuations don’t matter and that risks aren’t real. I still avoid bubbly valuations where possible, and acknowledge temporary risks as they emerge. But basically, when a developed country is in a state of fiscal dominance, it starts to resemble an emerging market in some limited ways, or “emerging market lite”. When an emerging market has a recession, it often has a high level of currency debasement such that its stocks and real estate do well in local currency terms even as they stagnate relative to harder unit measures like dollars or gold.

In the US, the dollar is our currency, so the harder measure is basically just gold. And that’s exactly what the US has been like during the past several years: stocks and real estate go up in dollars, but stagnate relative to gold:

![SPX to Gold Ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-25-spx-to-gold.png)

-In 2019, fiscal dominance began, but was still mild, and was largely driven by aging demographics, financialization of the economy, and the fact that ever-higher debt levels were no longer offset by structurally falling interest rates as they had been for nearly four decades prior.

-In 2020 and 2021, super-stimulus occurred and so fiscal activities reached extreme levels, on top of those structural forces.

-In 2022, there was a fiscal contraction, like a hangover from all that stimulus. A temporary slowing of the fiscal train.

-In 2023 and 2024, there was a re-acceleration in fiscal deficits as public interest expense in particular ballooned to enormous levels.

-In 2025, the fiscal situation just kind of went flat, neither accelerating or decelerating, as new taxes (tariffs) pulled it down but those various structural forces kept it high.

Here in 2026, the fiscal situation is back toward mildly accelerating again. This is because, as mentioned earlier, we have the One Big Beautiful Bill Act kicking in with mild-to-moderate stimulatory effects, while the structural backdrop for fiscal dominance remains in effect as well. Some substantial tariffs still remain in effect as a downward force, but those are partially held in check by the US Supreme Court now.

The overall blend points toward a slightly looser fiscal context than in 2025, meaning a bit more stimulatory. This is up against a backdrop where lower-income and middle-income consumers and certain businesses are struggling with higher energy costs, which then leak into higher fertilizer, food, and transport costs if persistent enough.

Thus, we continue to have a somewhat stagflationary two-speed or “K-shaped” economy. Those on the receiving side of fiscal deficits, and those with higher incomes and a lot of existing assets, are generally doing well. Those who are on the tighter side of monetary policy (such as younger families looking to buy a home), those with low/medium incomes, and those without substantial assets, are generally in a more stagflationary malaise.

In this environment, I continue to have a more constructive outlook on US banks than most.

-They are gradually re-absorbing their unrealized losses from 2023, despite ongoing sensationalism around the topic:

![Lyn Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-25-lyn-tweet.png)

-Their private credit exposures, while real, are limited. As senior creditors, they’re not first in line for losses like the direct investors are. Realistic loss scenarios for banks from private credit are in the hundreds of billions of dollars, which is manageable compared to their $2.6 trillion in aggregate equity capital and hundreds of billions in aggregate annual profits.

-Ongoing US fiscal deficits continue to provide for a “run it hot” economy in nominal terms which is decent for banks, even if the result is stagflationary for many people.

-Their lending activities continue at a comfortable pace, despite various negative headwinds and headlines.

![Bank Loans](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-25-bank-loans-.png)

I continue to view regional and super-regional banks as decent places to have capital for the value side and/ or the income side of a portfolio.

## Final Thoughts: DeFi Trouble

Those who have followed my work for a while know that I’m structurally bearish on most cryptocurrencies outside of bitcoin and stablecoins, and so I’ve maintained no positions in any of them other than small positions for hands-on research and testing purposes.

This bearish/cautious take began with my [Ethereum article](https://www.lynalden.com/ethereum-analysis/) (Jan 2021), and only increased from there with [A Blockchain Centralization Dilemma](https://www.lynalden.com/proof-of-stake/) (Nov 2021), [Digital Alchemy](https://www.lynalden.com/digital-alchemy/) (May 2022), [The Problems with DeFi and Crypto](https://www.lynalden.com/defi-problems/) (Dec 2022), and [Why Most Cryptocurrencies Won’t Accrue Value](https://www.lynalden.com/why-most-cryptocurrencies-wont-accrue-value/) (Dec 2025). And along the way I’ve provided more tactical updates within the premium reports.

On April 18th, North Korean hackers exploited a DeFi platform within the Ethereum ecosystem, resulting in the largest hack of the year so far. What made even bigger news was that a number of large DeFi platforms and second-layer blockchains built on top of Ethereum made the decision to freeze the funds, thus challenging their own concept of being decentralized in the first place.

![Arbitrum Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-25-arbitrum-tweet.png)

As I’ve written about before, these systems boil down to a small multi-signature quorum at the end of the day. In other words, like a small digital board of directors that runs a given protocol. The most decentralized systems like the Bitcoin base layer and the Lightning network don’t have that type of centralization, but most smart contract blockchains and platforms do.

The reason that so many of them took centralized action this time (while letting prior hacks go unaddressed), is due to the high level of rehypothecation involved. Zach Rynes humorously described the problem in a tweet last week:

![DeFi Humor Post](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-25-defi-humor-post.png)

For the more plain-language breakdown, I warned about this nearly three years ago in a handful of reports:

> **Liquid Staking Derivatives 101**
>
> To run an Ethereum 2.0 validator, you need 32 ETH. If ETH is $3k per coin for example, that would be $96k to run a validator. Most people don’t have that amount. Only wealthy people can run Ethereum validators.
>
> So, third parties can stake ETH for normal customers in smaller amounts. Exchanges like Coinbase and Kraken, for example, can take a customer’s ETH with their permission and stake it along with other peoples’ ETH. They run many full validators, and their customers basically have an off-chain claim to a partial validator. The problem with this is that large centralized entities end up controlling a lot of the staked ETH in a custodial fashion, which means they have a lot of influence over which transactions are added to the blockchain.
>
> Smart contracts also popped up to do this, with liquid staking derivatives. The biggest one is called Lido. You can deposit your ETH coin into Lido’s smart contract, and receive a stETH coin in return, which represents your claim on that ETH. This is an extremely attractive solution from a profit point of view because it lets users earn double the interest on their ETH.
>
> Theoretically, staked ETH is supposed to have an opportunity cost compared to using it to earn yield in a DeFi protocol. Users give up DeFi yield, in order to stake their ETH and earn a yield from validation. However, with a liquid staking derivative stETH, a user can have their cake and eat it to. They can deposit their ETH into Lido, receive stETH as their claim on it, and earn the staking yield. Then they can take that stETH coin (which is equal in value to one ETH), and deposit it into a DeFi protocol to earn additional yield on that stETH.
>
> Basically, with this liquid staking protocol solution, users get liquidity similarly to unstaked ETH, while actually staking. And liquidity is a major network effect; a stETH token is a lot more desirable than the derivative coin of a much smaller staking provider, which begets more people staking with Lido and using stETH. Technically anyone can create a new liquid staking protocol, but in practice, it concentrates towards one. Lido has about 90% of the liquid staking market share despite the existence of multiple competitors.
>
> ![ETH 2 Liquid Staking Balances](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-1-eth2-liquid-staking-balances.png)
>
> *Source: Dune Analytics, @eliasimos*
>
> More concerningly, Lido now has nearly 30% of all staked ETH and climbing.
>
> Additionally, processing transactions on a smart contract blockchain offers a lot more arbitrage opportunities than processing transactions on something simple like Bitcoin. Miners/stakers can run complex software analysis to find arbitrage opportunities in terms of how to order their transactions on smart contract blockchains, and this naturally comes with economies of scale. This is called miner extracted value or “MEV”. The combination of liquid staking providers with MEV represents a powerful set of network effects that are likely to quickly centralize the transaction processing of the system post-Merge, unless something is changed.
>
> Bitcoin miners don’t have this issue anywhere near the same degree. Mining has some economies of scale, but it starts to top out pretty quickly. In other words, it’s more cost effective to have 5% of the Bitcoin mining market than only 0.5% of the market due to various cost synergies and economies of scale, but there’s no significant benefit to a miner as they keep going higher than that, and so the ability to reach higher and higher is constrained.
>
> Liquid staking, however, has an actual self-reinforcing network effect rather than just an economy of scale from its MEV, and this can trend towards controlling the majority of the market unless big players deliberately go against their own economic interests and avoid using it.
>
> The risks of this are pretty apparent. The blockchain becomes more centralized, and a large number of staked ETH ends up in a single smart contract that, if it is exploited/hacked in some way, would result in billions of dollars worth of lost funds.
>
> -Lyn Alden, May 1st 2022 report
>
> One of the problems with DeFi is that people use it for recursive leverage. For example, they deposit ETH into a liquidity pool and get stablecoins in return, collateralized by that ETH. Then they take those stablecoins, deposit them into a liquidity pool, and get some other token, collateralized by those stablecoins, and then deposit that other token, and so on. There are various arbitrage opportunities to get high yields from layers of rehypothecation, and so there’s a lot of rehypothecation in the ecosystem in general. The same sort of recursive thing is happening with stETH arbitrage; some big players could really blow up if the stETH/ETH spread gets too wide for a persistent period of time.
>
> I would personally avoid having any significant deposits in crypto yield platforms going forward in this bearish environment.
>
> -Lyn Alden, June 12th 2022 report

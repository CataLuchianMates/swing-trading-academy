## Macro View

**The Strait of Hormuz**

Back during the 2020/2021 reports, I had a recurring Covid-19 update within the macro section to discuss the various waves of virus variants and the associated lockdowns that would come with them, or the ebbing of those waves and the easing of those lockdowns. The reason, of course, was because these things affected the global macro picture and certain investment outcomes.

I find myself doing the same thing recently with the Strait of Hormuz. It has become this ongoing background saga to keep track of. A significant percentage of the world’s oil, LNG, fertilizer, and helium remain disrupted as we enter the 11th week of the conflict. And to complicate matters, both the US and Iran are at varying times blocking the Strait.

The good news is that many oil experts feared worse outcomes than this if the blockage remained in effect going into May. A combination of mild demand destruction and releasing of oil from reserve stockpiles has softened the blow for aggregate price levels, and no major humanitarian crises have occurred as a result of truly severe energy or food crises. Energy, food, and fertilizer prices are broadly elevated, and are indeed negatively affecting people, but by most measures it hasn’t yet gotten as bad as it did in spring 2022 shortly after Russia invaded Ukraine.

The bad news is that the problem remains ongoing. Existing reserves only have so much capacity, and the risk for a 2022-style or worse outcome remains a significant possibility. Shortages are cumulative, and if it goes on long enough, it will risk another major price spike until more severe demand destruction occurs, likely from developing countries and from airliners more broadly. As we enter the summer months, air travel picks up seasonally, and electricity demand (i.e. natural gas demand) picks up for hot areas of the world for air conditioning, which will heavily pressure the demand side of the imbalance.

**US Economic Conditions**

Estimates for Q2 GDP growth in the United States are solid, after somewhat weak numbers over the past couple of quarters.

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-5-10-gdpnow.png)

The economy continues to operate at two speeds:

-The headline numbers are driven heavily by the fiscal deficits and by AI capex spending. Parts of the economy that are on the receiving side of those mega-trends are in great shape, and so are those who own substantial assets in general.

-However, we also see record-low consumer sentiment and many stagnating economic measures among the bottom three quarters of the population. Those who are on the wrong side of higher fuel prices and substantial mortgage rates, continue to face affordability problems.

The job market in particular is interesting, because on one hand total nonfarm jobs have been flat for a full year since April 2025, and yet the unemployment rate and weekly jobless claims remain low. Businesses currently aren’t hiring much, but also aren’t firing much, and instead are trying to do more with less via AI.

My base case is that the US will have somewhat of a “Japanification” in the years ahead, where things never quite boom as much as the bulls think, nor are there catastrophic job losses or credit losses like the bears think. Rather, credit cycles and hiring/firing cycles will likely be more muted, as large fiscal deficits continue to constantly stimulate parts of the economy, and AI gives companies a major productivity boost if they use it well.

**Liquidity Check-Up**

Since their shift in late 2025, the Fed continues to gradually provide new base money to the banking system. At the moment, there are no major signs of liquidity stress in the form of usage of the Fed’s repo facility or highly elevated SOFR-IORB spreads.

![Repo Usage](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-5-10-repo-annotated.png)

![SOFR Rates](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-5-10-sofr.png)

The Treasury Department [recently released](https://home.treasury.gov/policy-issues/financing-the-government/quarterly-refunding/most-recent-quarterly-refunding-documents) their quarterly documents for upcoming treasury security issuance expectations. The summary is that they plan to continue their current course of operations, which is a rather T-bill heavy issuance pattern.

Prior to Covid, the Treasury was funding about 15% of its debt with T-bills and the rest with longer duration T-notes and T-bonds. That’s a historically normal ratio. During Covid, Treasury Secretary Steve Mnuchin relied on a spike of T-bill issuance to fund the early rounds of stimulus, which is typical for a crisis.

Janet Yellen took over as Treasury Secretary in early 2021, and by the second half of her term in 2023 and 2024 as economic conditions had mostly normalized, she was still relying on above-normal T-bill issuance (about 22% of total debt) to fund the government’s fiscal deficits. Scott Bessent criticized this approach at the time.

A higher T-bill issuance pattern is generally a pro-liquidity move, because it puts less long-duration debt into the financial system and generally puts downward pressure on long-term rates by limiting supply of long-term debt. However, the “cost” of that approach is that the government becomes more sensitive to changes in interest rates (i.e. they haven’t locked in their debt; it’s like relying on an adjustable-rate mortgage rather than a fixed-rate mortgage) and they require a bigger cash account to fund that frequent turnover of T-bills that are constantly maturing and being refinanced.

Now that Scott Bessent has been Treasury Secretary for over 15 months, he’s ironically continuing the Yellen playbook without change, i.e. relying on 22% T-bill issuance, which is high. The recent Treasury funding updates showed that they plan to continue this approach for a while longer.

Bessent is on record about how one of his priorities is to get long-duration rates down, and so issuing more long-duration debt would somewhat contrast with that approach. Ideally, Bessent would like to normalize the amount of T-bill issuance *and* get long-term rates down due to disinflationary economic policy (i.e. plenty of oil production, no manufacturing supply bottlenecks, and lower deficits) but the ongoing high fiscal deficits (as I have long been forecasting) and now the war on Iran are disrupting that hope.

In interviews, I’ve recently been asked many times if incoming Fed chair Kevin Warsh will be able to shrink the Fed balance sheet like he is on record saying he wants to. My stance has been that he likely won’t be able to shrink it by much (if at all), even though there are indeed some levers to pull to make it happen around the margins. Ironically, one of the levers to pull would be for Bessent’s Treasury Department to issue fewer T-bills and a higher ratio of longer-duration T-notes and T-bonds, and therefore reduce their cash account at the Fed by a few hundred billion dollars. And that so far is not happening. I outlined this in my February newsletter:

> ## Below-Baseline Scenarios
>
> President Trump has announced that his nominee for the next Fed chairman (a term that starts in May 2026) will be Kevin Warsh. The U.S. Senate would have to confirm it at that time to make it official.
>
> Warsh is on record saying he is in favor of a smaller Fed balance sheet, which is in contrast to the Fed’s existing forecasts. Therefore, it needs to be asked: could a shake-up in leadership at the Fed change their balance sheet composition?
>
> The answer is likely “not much” but with some caveats.
>
> -The chairman is powerful, but he’s only one of 12 votes on the Federal Open Market Committee, so he doesn’t unilaterally set policy.
>
> -The Fed and other federal agencies could further ease bank regulations, allowing them to hold more treasuries without negatively impacting their regulatory leverage ratios. That means the Fed can own fewer treasuries.
>
> -The Fed could take some steps to discourage excess reserve accumulation at banks, thereby distributing the reserves that are in the system a bit more evenly. This could let them shrink their balance sheet by perhaps a few hundred billion dollars.
>
> -The Treasury Department could increase its average debt duration and shrink its average cash account size (known as the Treasury General Account). A smaller cash account size would give more liquidity back to the banking system, enabling a smaller Fed balance sheet by perhaps a few hundred billion dollars. However, the government generally doesn’t want higher long-term interest rates, so they’re sensitive about how much supply of long-duration debt they issue.
>
> In theory, if all of those things were done, the Fed’s balance sheet could go down by somewhere around a trillion dollars. However, the chances of that happening are low.
>
> And importantly, all of those measures just move liquidity around. Banks buying more treasuries on fractional reserve is functionally similar to the Fed buying them with new reserves. And moving reserves around and reducing the Treasury General Account basically result in efficiency gains: doing more with less.
>
> At the end of the day, the Fed is going to optimize for 1) maintaining bank lending and broad money creation at a moderate pace, 2) ensuring the treasury security market remains liquid, and 3) ensuring that the repo market remains liquid and that any liquidity stresses that do pop up don’t remain acute for very long.
>
> -February 8th 2026 public newsletter

Overall, it’s easier to talk than to take action. I continue to expect that the combo of the Treasury and the Fed will keep liquidity conditions at workable levels. If one of them gets a bit more hawkish, the other is likely to turn a bit more dovish to compensate. It’ll be a delicate balancing act that will ultimately keep the treasury security market liquid and long-term interest rates at moderate levels.

Part of the “fiscal dominance” playbook involves ongoing large fiscal deficits, mild-to-moderate financial repression, and a revolving set of excuses (regardless of administration) as to why fiscal and monetary policy can’t be fully normalized.

In other words, nothing stops this train.

## Emerging Markets’ Breakout

I’m writing this report, and the next few months of reports, from Egypt. Long-term readers are familiar with the fact that it’s my second home and I live part of each year here.

It’s interesting (and potentially unfortunate) timing, because Egypt had an energy curfew in effect throughout April due to a tripling in the price of their monthly natural gas import bill. Shops and restaurants had to close early each day to reduce electricity consumption (and by extension natural gas consumption), and even street lights would shut off, plunging large swaths of urban landscape into darkness when normally there’d be plenty of economic activity. Here in May, that situation has eased, although there are still some electricity-saving actions taking place such as reduced lighting and increased usage of remote work.

Egypt often has brown-outs in the summer, since air conditioning usage ramps up and takes a ton of electricity, and public funding hasn’t prioritized nuclear electricity production as quickly as it has construction of the country’s [new capital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_New_Capital). So, if the Hormuz blockage extends into summer months, Egypt would be at risk of further electricity reductions.

Anyway, given the context, it seems a suitable time for an emerging markets update. And I’ll go back a bit and see which emerging forecasts have gone as expected versus which ones have not.

While I’ve long carried an emerging markets position as a structural part of my various portfolios, it was in mid-2024 that I started seeing catalysts for their potential outperformance in the years ahead from a more tactical perspective:

> ## Deep Dive: Rotation Analysis
>
> The United States runs structural trade deficits with the rest of the world in aggregate. And, being the global reserve currency with the biggest and deepest capital markets, the rest of the world takes their surpluses and invests them into U.S. financial assets including stocks, bonds, real estate, and private equity.
>
> Over the past 15 years in particular, this has built up to rather imbalanced levels. Countries like Japan and China own a very large amount of U.S. financial assets, as do many others.
>
> There is a giant engine of almost automatic capital that keeps flowing into large-cap U.S. stocks in particular. Foreign capital keeps flowing in, and domestic employee 401(k) paychecks keep flowing in. And a lot of it is passively weighted by market capitalization, and so much of it automatically flows toward the top.
>
> Effectively the way this works is that we buy depreciating goods from abroad and we sell portions of our appreciating capital ownership to them in return. But it feels nice while we do it, at lease for those of us that own assets, because it’s really good for asset prices.
>
> However, China has been reducing its Treasury holdings, and Japan has been using some of its assets to intervene in its currency weakness. In addition, Japan raised its interest rate to 0.25%, which doesn’t seem like much but it’s their highest level in over 15 years.
>
> ![Japan Interest Rate](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-4-japan-interest-rate.png)
>
> Combined with Japan’s structural current account surplus, large amounts of capital allocated to foreign markets, and high levels of productivity, relatively minor hawkish moves from them can be rather supportive of their weak currency. There’s been a significant drop in the USDJPY exchange rate, both as U.S. economic data softens and as Japan gradually takes more measures to support its currency from getting too weak.
>
> ![USDJPY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-4-usdjpy.png)
>
> Right now, all of those attributes have pushed a lot of capital to one side of the boat. Foreign investors have a very high percentage of their allocations in U.S. assets, which props up our equity valuations and our currency (which in some ways is beneficial, and other ways is harmful), which then feeds on itself.
>
> Many emerging markets have dollar-denominated debt, and so a strong dollar squeezes their financial systems and economies rather hard. As more capital flows into the United States, it can keep the dollar strong and thus keep downward economic and financial pressure on these dollar-indebted countries. That pressure increasingly makes global pools of capital decide to leave those markets to shift toward U.S. markets, which weakens their currencies and strengthens the dollar further.
>
> However, the United States is not invincible. It’s running massive fiscal deficits, which by late 2025 the Federal Reserve will likely go back to assisting with for liquidity purposes. It’s also running a structural trade deficit, partly because of its artificially strong dollar, which has increasingly become a domestic political issue. With interest rates as high as they are and with deficits as large as they are, there’s a growing gap between those who already own assets and those who are effectively locked out of owning assets.
>
> When enough shifts happen, this dynamic that feeds on itself in one direction can instead turn and feed on itself in the other direction. If capital inflows start turning into outflows, it can weaken asset prices and the dollar together. When the dollar weakens, dollar-indebted emerging markets tend to get a fundamental boost, which attracts more capital to them. As they get more capital, it helps support their currency. And that tends to attract more capital.
>
> Many investors have a concern that these large creditor Asian nations could spark a rotation in capital. As they seek to support their own currencies and/or markets, they can stop investing in the United States around the margins, or even repatriate some of their capital.
>
> **The Potential Catalyst**
>
> Global macroeconomic systems are very complex, with more variables than any one framework or model can account for. And so, the way I approach it in terms of managing risk and discovering opportunities, is to identify a base case path, and then keep modifying it or branching from it as new data and updates come in.
>
> Two months ago on Twitter, there was a thread involving financial professionals Michael Arouet and Patrick Zweifel about what could spark a rotation in capital from U.S. to foreign equity markets, like what has happened in the past.
>
> ![Developed vs Developing Markets](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-4-developed-vs-developing.png)
>
> In response, I provided a thought process:
>
> ![Lyn Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-4-lyn-tweet.png)
>
> I then provided more analysis about this topic in my mid-July public newsletter. The focus of the newsletter was how, when the U.S. economy softens and the Fed cuts rates, it might not be as stimulating for the domestic economy as people think.
>
> After four decades of lower-lows and lower-highs in terms of mortgage rates in the United States, this was the first cycle where we got a higher-high in mortgage rates. I think there’s a strong probability that we’ll also get a higher-low in mortgage rates, meaning that they’ll go down from peak levels, but won’t get as low as they did in the prior cycle.
>
> ![Mortgage Rates](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-4-mortgage-rates.png)
>
> Back in April 2024, the New York Fed [released](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/annual_reports) their annual report which reviewed their activities for 2023 and projected how things might go in the future. In the report, they projected that by the end of 2025 they’d likely go back to structural accumulation of Treasury securities but would continue to decrease their holdings of mortgage backed securities:
>
> ![UST vs MBS](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-28-projected-soma-ust-vs-mbs.png)
>
> The Fed phrases this projection in terms of maintaining adequate bank reserves, while also trying to normalize their balance sheet back toward Treasury securities. I’d phrase this a bit more bluntly as an anticipatory projection on their part that they’ll need to quietly support the structural fiscal deficits going forward, which is what my math points to as well by around that same timeframe.
>
> Meanwhile, if we don’t get a lower-low in mortgages rates, there is a limit to how much spending power homeowner consumers can unlock by mortgage refinancing. Other parts of the lending market, such as small business lending, would benefit positively from rate cuts, but in the troubling context of a weakening consumer and weakening job market.
>
> Some of the biggest beneficiaries of rate cuts might actually be emerging market governments and companies. I don’t necessarily mean right away as we deal with market volatility, but as interest rates on the dollar ease, there are a lot of dollar-indebted emerging markets out there that look like coiled springs. It looks similarly in that regard to the early 2000s period, and emerging markets enjoyed a very strong 2003-2007 cycle from an easing dollar.
>
> During strong dollar periods of the 1960s, 1990s, and 2010s where there was also a general trend of disinflation domestically and internationally, a lot of assets tended to move in lockstep. U.S. growth stocks in particular tended to do well. In the 1960s they were called the “[Nifty Fifty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nifty_Fifty)“. In the 1990s they were called the Dotcom Bubble. Lately they’ve been called the FAANG stocks and now the Magnificent Seven. In addition to doing well fundamentally, they often received very high valuations that eventually became excessive. Meanwhile, value-type stocks, foreign stocks, gold, energy, and commodities tended to not do particularly well, both in terms of fundamentals and in terms of low valuations on those fundamentals.
>
> During weak dollar periods of the 1970s and 2000s, that trend reversed. The dollar weakened, which eased debt burdens globally. Inflation increased, energy and commodities did well, gold did well, and value-type stocks tended to do pretty well. The growth stocks from the prior decades tended to still do well fundamentally, but their stock prices would often stagnate for a while as their valuations normalized.
>
> As a basic example, Coca Cola (KO) reached a price/earnings ratio of 56x in the late 1990s selling sugar water alongside the tech bubble. As a blue-chip stable growth stock, it got caught up in the frenzy. Over the next decade and a half, their net income continued to increase substantially, but their valuation fell by more than half down to reality, resulting in a stock price that took that full period to recover. A lot of similar non-tech U.S. growth stocks had a similar pattern, including Walmart (WMT).
>
> ![KO Price/Earnings](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-4-ko.png)
>
> Meanwhile, here in the 2020s, a lot of people are asking if the Nvidia’s (NVDA) of the world are a bubble or not, like Cisco (CSCO) in the late 1990s. Meanwhile, I’m instead scratching my head looking at Costco (COST), which is a great company like Coca Cola was in the 1990s, but is trading at over 50x earnings.
>
> ![COST FAST Graph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-4-cost-fastgraph.png)
>
> If there’s a catalyst for the start of that kind of rotation, then I’d say that the United States cutting interest rates in response to a gradually weakening consumer, at a time of near-record structural twin deficits as a percentage of GDP, is a strong contender.
>
> Admittedly, I had a similar thought back in 2019, which was the last time that the United States began a rate-cutting cycle. That ended up being incorrect. The U.S. economy was softening in 2019, and the Fed performed moderate rate cuts in the middle of the year. By September, the Fed had to end quantitative tightening and go back to balance sheet growth due to financial plumbing issues partially related to the large U.S. deficit. And indeed the dollar index began softening from that point. I thought that might be the start of a significant rotation.
>
> But, as a curveball, the pandemic happened in 2020. On January 26th, 2020 I sent out an extra note between normal premium reports to alert members about a riskier-than-normal setup with a combination of overbought stocks and worsening virus news out of China. In the months that followed, economies around the world performed lockdowns, trillions of currency units were printed, and so the likelihood of a rotation got thrown out the window for a few years. Europe had an energy crisis (even before the war in Ukraine, which the war then made worse), and then squeezed out poorer countries by buying as much liquified natural gas as they could and outbidding them. Altogether, a variety of occurrences gave the world another five years of this trend continuing, with the U.S. trade deficit widening further, more capital flowing into the U.S., and the biggest stocks going from $1 trillion to $3 trillion companies.
>
> However, in those five years, China became the largest auto exporter in the world. Russia invaded Ukraine, got cut off from the dollar system, and financially unified more with China. China substantially trimmed their U.S. Treasury exposure. More members joined BRICS and the Shanghai Cooperation Organization. A lot of central banks of BRICS-type nations bought gold at an accelerated pace to diversity their reserves a bit, and China helped broker some degree of peace and communication between Iran and Saudi Arabia. Political polarization in the United States continued to rise. And U.S. oil production had been growing dramatically from 2012 to 2019, but here in 2024 it’s nearly flat from its 2019 peak, and with faster well depletion rates now.
>
> The question is, will events occur that result in another repeat of that cycle, with everything pushed further than before, and with pieces further along on the chessboard? It’s possible. But I think there’s a higher chance this time that a rotation will take place. I think the extremes to which capital is stuffed into U.S. financial markets are hitting levels that are hard to push for another full cycle, but we’ll see.
>
> To be clear, I’m not saying to rotate out of all the top U.S. stocks and put everything elsewhere. But I am saying that I’d certainly want some style-factor and geographic diversification in my equities outside of just the Magnificent Seven, and that a set of catalysts for such a rotation seems to be there. I’ll be monitoring how this plays out to see what developments may come.
>
> -August 4th 2024 premium report

Since that report, the US indeed continued to run massive fiscal deficits, the Fed shifted back toward balance sheet expansion in late 2025 (despite ongoing above-target inflation) to address liquidity issues and therefore assist with deficit monetization, and US mortgage refinancing volumes continue to be quite low despite moderate rate cuts by the Fed.

However, AI continued to take off, and so large-cap US stocks with direct or indirect exposure to AI have continued to perform well. Fortunately, emerging markets have plenty of AI-related tech as well.

Here was another report section from 2024:

> ## Deep Dive: Emerging Markets
>
> In my August 4th report, I focused on the possibility of some asset rotations in the years ahead. If you haven’t read that report, I suggest doing so.
>
> For many years, there have been a lot of investors and analysts looking for a major asset rotation too early, so we have to be mindful of timing. The way that I currently phrase this is that it’s the first significant catalyst for a rotation since 2019, but it’s not assured. We have to look for follow-through.
>
> -There are periods of time where growth stocks outperform value stocks, and vice versa. And recent years have been quite volatile on that front:
>
> ![Growth vs Value](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-growth-value.png)
>
> -There are periods of time where developed markets outperform emerging markets, and vice versa:
>
> ![Emerging vs Developed](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-emerging-developed.png)
>
> -There are periods of time where equities in general outperform gold, and vice versa:
>
> ![SPX vs Gold](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-spx-gold.png)
>
> -There are periods of time where the dollar strengthens vs other currencies, and periods of time where it weakens:
>
> ![Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-dollar-index.png)
>
> And they tend to approximately rotate together. Decades where American growth stocks do well and the dollar is strong tend to be the decades where value stocks, emerging market stocks, and gold/commodities collectively underperform. And then when an asset rotation occurs, American growth stocks (which at that point are quite expensive along with a strong dollar) tend to stagnate and the dollar weakens, while all that other stuff has a period of outperformance for a while.
>
> And a lot of it is tied to capital flows and the strength of the dollar. Emerging markets are responsible for the majority of marginal economic growth and commodity demand growth in the world, since they collectively have a larger population and have far less per-capita commodity usage as a starting point compared to developed markets. Emerging markets also have a lot of dollar-denominated debt, which is lent to them from lenders across the world that want to minimize local currency risk when lending globally.
>
> So when the dollar strengthens relative to other currencies, it hurts most emerging markets, because their dollar-denominated liabilities begin appreciating relative to their assets and cashflows. Their economies get impaired, reducing their commodity demand and growth prospects. That hurts commodity prices, and many emerging markets are themselves commodity producers, so that further hurts them too. Global investors then pull capital out of emerging markets and stick them into U.S. capital markets for safety, which further strengthens the dollar and hurts emerging market currencies. The dollar gets very strong, and U.S. equity valuations become very high. It’s a vicious cycle for emerging markets.
>
> But eventually that runs out of steam. In 1971 it was because the United States defaulted on its gold peg, having created a lot of dollars while its gold reserves dwindled. In 1985 it was because the United States initiated the Plaza Accord to weaken the dollar in an attempt to defend its troubled export industries. In 2001 it was because the U.S. encountered a recession and cut interest rates, at a time when capital from around the world was heavily stuffed into its equity market at very high valuations.
>
> When we use Brazil as a quintessential example, we can see how much their dollar-denominated GDP suffered during the three times where the dollar index spiked significantly:
>
> ![Brazil GDP Annotated](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-brazil-gdp-annotated.png)
>
> We can also see this in more tangible metrics. In two of those three dollar spikes, Brazil’s nationwide oil consumption went down for many years, indicating severe economic distress:
>
> ![Brazil Oil Annotated](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-brazil-oil-annotated.png)
>
> A lot of investors have been early when looking for the latest asset rotation, including myself. The previous window for a rotation was back in 2019. The U.S. economy began slowing, the Fed cut interest rates, and they hit the domestic banking liquidity floor with the September 2019 repo spike, and went back to balance sheet growth. This could have led to a rotation.
>
> However, the pandemic hit in 2020, global economies and markets were locked down and scrambled, developing countries generally had fewer tools to handle this than the United States, Europe’s energy crisis was exacerbated by the Russian invasion of Ukraine, and the big winners of the pulled-forward trend of remote work were the American software companies. And then as the Fed began to fight back against inflation in 2022, it sharply strengthened the dollar index.
>
> The Fed has indicated that they will begin cutting interest rates here in September 2024. And we do see significant weakening of the U.S. economy, or at least it has become a two-speed economy with significant deterioration in the economic prospects of people in the lower half of the income spectrum that don’t own a lot of assets. The dollar index has rolled over from its highs, and might have plenty of room to decline if indeed we get a substantial rate-cutting cycle.
>
> As the Fed lowers interest rates and as the dollar eases a bit, it gives emerging markets a lot of breathing room. And as they start to perform better, they can attract more global capital, and experience a multi-year virtuous cycle and period of growth. Capital flows contribute to fundamentals, which then entice more capital flows.
>
> So this is a period of time to be locked in and focused to see if we do get follow through with a rotation, or not. Here in late 2024, the catalyst is a slowing U.S. economy and expectations of rate cuts. In 2025, an end to the Fed’s quantitative tightening could be a second catalyst to provide more follow-through. I don’t expect this story to unfold in one month or one quarter; it’s a process to watch over multiple quarters to see if we stay on course or not.
>
> **Potential Rotation: Latin American Equities and Gold**
>
> If we look at the S&P 500 to Latin American equities on a total return basis (i.e. inclusive of reinvested dividends), Latin American equities reached a low against the S&P 500 back in December 2021, and haven’t made a lower-low since then. Instead it has now been almost three years of sideways choppy relative performance between the two:
>
> ![ILF vs SPY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-ilf-spy.png)
>
> If we look at Brazil again, as the largest economy in this mix, they’ve got 4.5% inflation but a whopping 10.5% interest rate, giving them some of the highest real rates in the world currently. Their central bank has been in currency-protection mode for years, holding the economy down like a coiled spring, and as the Fed eases their monetary policy it gives Brazil a lot of room to change policy.
>
> Similarly, the S&P 500 peaked against gold on a total return basis in December 2021 and has mildly underperformed since:
>
> ![SPX vs Gold Total Returns](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-spx-gold-total-returns.png)
>
> **Delayed Rotation: Chinese Equities**
>
> Broader emerging markets haven’t shown signs of a rotation yet, since Chinese equities are a big weighting in the index and they’ve been a self-imposed limiter in recent years.
>
> China’s government cracked down on its own tech companies a few years ago, bringing them to extremely cheap valuations, and resulting in the first net capital exodus from China in decades. Their housing sector continues to deleverage, and they’ve been reluctant to stimulate fiscally, opting to protect their bond market over their equity market.
>
> Whether one looks at earnings multiple or books values, a lot of the Chinese tech, media, and ecommerce stocks are extraordinarily cheap, as global investors don’t want to touch them with a ten foot pole:
>
> ![China Tech Stocks](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-china-tech.png)
>
> Isolating JD.com (JD) in particular, compared to a few years ago it’s now profitable, cheap, paying a dividend, and rapidly buying back shares, and yet with a near record low stock price:
>
> ![JD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-jd-fastgraph.png)
>
> Investors don’t really want it at any price, because they are afraid of getting zeroed out of a position much like how American investors got effectively zeroed out of otherwise high-quality Russian stocks. So when I analyze JD, I am extremely bullish over the next 3-5 years as a 90% base case, but then 10% concerned about the investment effectively getting zeroed out as the company continues to perform fine enough, due to sanctions or confiscation. And there’s a lot of global capital looking at it the same way, along with many other similar stocks, and deciding to invest minimally or not at all.
>
> I think the bottom in Chinese stock indices is likely already in, and there are myriad catalysts that could propel them higher in the years ahead, including thawing tensions between the United States and China, or significant stimulus from the Chinese government. However, there’s no sign as of yet that Chinese stocks have bottomed vs American stocks, as the more relevant comparison. So that’s still in a wait-and-see mode.
>
> -September 1st 2024 premium report

Since that report, value stocks and Chinese equities continued to languish (no rotation toward them), but gold and Latin American equities outperformed the S&P 500 (a significant rotation toward them), and South Korea (thanks to their memory stocks) has been a breakout outperformer as well. South Korea is within the MSCI emerging market index.

> ## Investment Updates: Emerging Markets
>
> Since emerging markets were basically defined as such in the late 1980s, there have been two major emerging market cycles. When this line is rising, it means emerging markets are outperforming the S&P 500, and when it’s falling it means the S&P 500 is outperforming:
>
> ![SPX vs EM](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-spx-em.png)
>
> There were cycles before then, but they were less defined. And they were inversely correlated with dollar strength:
>
> ![SPX vs EM vx DXY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-spx-em-dxy.png)
>
> There are two main camps of thought going forward.
>
> One camp is that there aren’t really going to be meaningful cycles going forward; that it’s structurally different now. U.S. tech stocks will continue to dominate, global capital will continue to gravitate toward the United States, and emerging markets are structurally uninvestable in the broad sense.
>
> The other camp is that we’re approaching a sentiment bottom. Emerging markets are considered uninvestable right at the bottom, and they’re super cheap. With China starting to stimulate, and the U.S. starting to cut rates, if there’s a time for a reversal, it’s right about now.
>
> I’m more in the second camp, but am not betting the farm on it. The way I view it is that we can identify catalysts, but we then need to monitor for follow-through. What 2001 and 2024 have in common is that global capital is stuffed disproportionately into U.S. markets, and the Fed is starting a cutting cycle.
>
> There was a Fed cutting cycle in 2007, but global capital was not stuffed into the U.S. at the time. Quite the contrary; that was a top of an emerging markets cycle and capital was stuffed into the BRICS (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa). That was the cycle when the BRICS were named in the first place.
>
> There was also a Fed cutting cycle in 2019. Global capital was stuffed into the U.S. at the time, but not quite as much as now, and then the whole 2020-2022 period was disrupted by a global pandemic and lockdowns and extreme fiscal and monetary actions.
>
> So, 2024 might or might not be the start of a reversal, but there are conditions present for it. Perhaps another disruption will occur and prevent any sort of reversal, such as a war that sends oil to $200/barrel. Maybe major emerging market leaders will shoot themselves in the foot with war, anti-market political actions, or other types of own-goals.
>
> And so I’m in the phase of watching for follow-through or the lack thereof, and am treating countries separately even though they are partially all linked to the dollar and global capital flows.
>
> **Examples of Emerging Market Stocks and ETFs**
>
> Chinese stocks had an almost 50% rally from their low in mid-September to their high, and then they gave up about half of those gains:
>
> ![MCHI 1 Year](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-mchi-1-year.png)
>
> This was due to a combination of re-positioning and short-covering. I plan to watch to see if that new base starts to hold, to see if we get a higher-low, in other words.
>
> When we zoom out, even that huge 50% spike was a mere blip:
>
> ![MCHI Full](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-mchi-full.png)
>
> An example of a Chinese stock that I like is JD.com (JD). It’s a major e-commerce stock, it’s trading at under 11x earnings, and it has more cash than debt. But it’s broadly impaired by the fact that China is viewed as uninvestable. Investors are worried about economic conditions, but then also worried about the geopolitical tail risk of war-related impairments. In other words, even if JD does okay, a foreign investor’s claim of ownership on JD could be taken from them, either because China takes it, or because the investor’s home country sanctions them from it.
>
> ![JD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-jd-fastgraph.png)
>
> This is where an investor can use something akin to [expected value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expected_value) to build a probability-weighted outcome.
>
> For a simple example, JD’s ADR is currently around $40. An investor might say that over the next three years, due to a combination of Chinese stimulus, a global rate-cutting cycle, and rock-bottom valuations, that the stock has an 80% chance to double in price to $80. However, she also acknowledges that there’s a 20% chance that, perhaps due to a conflict in the South China Sea, the value of her investment will go to zero.
>
> The probability-weighted price in three years would therefore be .8\*80 + .2\*0 = $64. That’s a 60% total gain, or 17% annualized over three years. And of course that’s a very simple model. An investor would generally have a bigger range of outcomes than that. There’s a chance it triples, a chance it doubles, a chance it goes up 50%, a chance it stays flat, a chance it gets cut in half, a chance it goes to zero, and so forth. The big variable in that calculation are the odds that the ownership of the asset is zeroed-out for any reason. One investor might assign 5% odds over three years, and another investor might assign 50% odds.
>
> Similar analysis can be done on Alibaba (BABA), Tencent (TCEHY), and so forth.
>
> If we move over to Brazil, I continue to find their banking system interesting as a very small value slice. Their biggest bank had an explosive run of more than 8x price appreciation during the BRICS boom of 2002-2007, and then has chopped along for over a decade and a half since then:
>
> ![ITUB YChart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-itub-ychart.png)
>
> If any point in the second half of the 2020s we get a proper dollar downcycle and an emerging market upcycle, the move that something like ITUB could make is quite large.
>
> ![ITUB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-itub-fastgraph.png)
>
> And then there are emerging market proxies in developed markets. I’ve highlighted Japan’s heavy equipment maker Komatsu (KMTUY) as an example of this before. With a stock like that, you generally get emerging market correlation with a developed market jurisdiction. The reason for the correlation is that emerging markets tend to be more physical economies than developed markets, and so marginal growth there tends to translate into more commodity usage, more development, etc. They’re starting from a lower base of installed physical capital.
>
> ![Komatsu vs EEM](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-komatsu-vs-eem.png)

Some people will dismiss the latest emerging markets rally as driven mainly by Taiwanese and Korean chip stocks. But that’s like dismissing the American stock rally by saying it’s only our chip and AI stocks. At any given time, markets tend to be driven by their best performers.

In my report from a month ago, I discussed why memory stocks were more likely to break up out of their multi-month consolidation than break down from it:

> Micron (MU) is one of the three companies that collectively make almost all of the world’s random access memory, and the only one that’s based in the United States. As AI usage has shifted from information-based chatbots toward activity-based agents (and more broadly, toward a greater need for persistence of memory), the demand for memory has soared alongside graphics processors.
>
> The prices of memory stocks similarly surged throughout 2025, although they’ve taken a breather here in 2026.
>
> ![MU YChart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-12-mu-ychart.png)
>
> ![MU FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-12-mu-fastgraph.png)
>
> -Bears point out that memory is a highly cyclical business. As the three main memory makers perform a lot of capex to increase supply and meet demand, they will eventually oversupply the market and crash memory prices just like they have in past cycles.
>
> -Bulls argue that we’re still in early innings for how much memory the world will need over the next 3-5 years. Additionally, there are supply constraints that limit how quickly new supply can reach the market, and thus prices are likely to remain elevated for quite a while. With the boom in AI agents, this isn’t a normal cycle.
>
> While there are risks, I generally side with the bulls here, and view memory stocks as more likely to break to the upside out of their current consolidation than to the downside. Additionally, the high volatility is interesting in and of itself for rebalancing.
>
> As such, I’ll be selling Micron June 18 cash-secured puts at a strike price of $380. This’ll pay a decent premium during the two months until expiry, and if the stock price drops it means I’ll be entering at a cost basis in the ballpark of $350 (the current price is $420).
>
> However, I do recommend conservative position sizing with a stock that moves this sharply.
>
> -April 12th 2026 premium report

Taiwan now has a bigger allocation in the MSCI emerging markets index than all of China (despite a roughly 60x larger population in China). And South Korea has a bigger allocation than all of India (despite a roughly 27x larger population in India).

While bears would say that it’s a bad sign that the Taiwanese and South Korean chip stocks are responsible for most of the emerging markets’ rally, as a longer-term bull I would flip that around and say that the ongoing underperformance of other large areas of emerging markets represent a “deep bench” of future growth.

In other words, by owning the emerging markets index, an investor gets exposure to some of the best chipmakers in the world, and then also has exposure to things like consolidating Indian banks and cheap Chinese internet companies for a future wave of growth once the chip stock really has run its course. And Latin American stocks, while they’ve been great performers in recent years, are still rather cheap and likely have a lot more to run.

## Final Thoughts: Bitcoin’s Positive Momentum

Bitcoin jumped to over $82k last week, which is the highest price since the start of February. It then corrected a bit, touching down near $79k before climbing back above $80k for the time being.

![BTCUSD](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-5-10-btc.png)

I continue to operate with the base case that the bottom is in. I don’t expect explosive upside in the months ahead (although that would be nice), but rather expect a long and uncertain grind upward from here, likely with frustrating periods of consolidation along the way.

Most valuation measures have fully reset, and if/when the price climbs higher, there likely won’t be as much profit-taking by longer-term holders as there was during 2024/2025, at least until new all-time highs are achieved.

# When It Rains It Pours

It was a tough back half of the week for US stocks, as the combination of recession fears and mixed Big Tech earnings left investors fleeing to safety.  On the week, we saw sharp moves lower for the S&P 500 -2%, NDX -3%, and RTY -6.7%.

The moves were classic ‘risk off’ / ‘flight to safety’ oriented.  On Thursday and Friday, we saw economically sensitive Cyclicals underperform Defensives by 734bps, the worst 2-day move ever recorded (dating back to 2007).  The only other comparable days were during actual recessions (March 2020 Covid Crash and 2008 GFC). And yet, Thursday-Friday were worse than any 2-day drop that took place during those recessions.

The headlines were dominated by renewed concerns around economic growth following weaker ISM Manufacturing & NFP prints.  As [mentioned on Friday](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/friday-morning-notes-82), the Macro evolution playbook officially flipped to bad economic data = bad for stocks.

With economic growth slowing and inflation in the rear view mirror, the market now seems to be extremely sensitive to data turning negative and what potential lagged effects the higher for longer environment has had on jobs, growth, and the economy moving forward.  This is why we have officially entered a bad news is bad news environment.

The catalyst for late last week’s sell off began with recessionary-like ISM data (released at 10 AM on Thurs), which ignited the sharp market reversal on Thursday.  The big concern was the ISM jobs subindex, which plummeted to 43.4 from 49.3.  This is the weakest employment reading since June 2020, and is even lower than the 45.4 recorded in September 2008 (the month Lehman collapsed).  The only times we’ve seen this number lower over the last 25+ years was (1) The Dot Com Bubble Bust; and (2) the 2008 Great Financial Crisis.

This was followed up [on Friday](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/friday-morning-notes-82) with a weak Employment Report, headlined by the Unemployment Rate hitting 4.3% (vs expectations of 4.1%).  This officially triggered the Sahm Rule, a recessionary indicator with a perfect (11/11) track record dating back to 1950.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!xDMt!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2938b191-58d6-45f6-a3d6-786a2981d8cd_910x707.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!xDMt!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2938b191-58d6-45f6-a3d6-786a2981d8cd_910x707.jpeg)

The Sahm Rule is a recessionary indicator that triggers when the 3-month moving average of the Unemployment Rate rises by at least 0.5 percentage points from its 12-month low.  With the Unemployment Rate rising to 4.3% in July, the Sahm Rule has now been triggered.

As mentioned, over the last 65 years, there has not been a single occurrence where this indicator has provided a false positive signal.  Each of the last 11 Sahm triggers have coincided with a recession.  In 10/11 cases, we were already in a recession when the Sahm indicator triggered (in the other 1959, we entered a recession 5 months after the Sahm trigger).

With these negative/recessionary data points to close last week, the soft landing vibes that permeated throughout 2024 have now morphed into recessionary worries.  Global macro risk off moves ensued with equities moving lower, the yield curve steepening, the VIX spiking, credit spreads widening, and more rates cuts being priced in.

The markets are now pricing a Fed Funds Rate of 3.20% twelve months out (equating to 200bps of cuts over the next year), which is recessionary pricing given the Fed cuts around 200bps in the first 12 months of a recession on average.

On the week, Bond Proxies (+5%) and Defensives (+2.7%) were the biggest outperformers (indicating a big risk off / flight to safety market sentiment).

Bitcoin sensitives (-14%), Memes (-9.5%), Most Short (-7.8%), Regional Banks (-7.7%), Biotech (-6%), 12M winners (-6%), High Beta Software (-6%), and Non-Profitable Tech (-6%) were the worst performers on the week.  Again, the heavy selling of these groups indicates a huge risk off sentiment in the market.

The feeling of last week’s selling was orderly (yet still painful), but started to transform to a more panicky flavor on Friday.  Volatility spiked on Friday with the VIX finishing the day +26% to 23.39.  All of this is coming on the heels of August liquidity (quickly deteriorating), which is known to be the largest month of the year for outflows.

Speaking of Bitcoin/Crypto, things turned ugly here this weekend with Bitcoin falling as much as -11% on Sunday and Ethereum falling as much as -21%.  High Beta stocks often trade along Crypto to some degree, so this is obviously a negative sign as we head into this week.

That is not the only headline we’re seeing this weekend, though, as Japan’s Nikkei 225 (Japan’s main stock market index) is down over -5% on Monday, and on pace for its worst 2-day drop in history.  This is an even larger drop than the Black Monday crash of 1987.

In 3 weeks, the Nikkei 225 has now wiped out all of its YTD gains and entered Bear Market territory.  Circuit breakers are being activated on Monday in some Asian markets, with South Korea suspending all sell orders due to market volatility. Such panicky global equity moves are an obvious cause for concern and often spill over into the US equity markets.

US Equity Futures are indicating a major gap down Monday morning (as of this publishing), which is not surprising given the global equity/crypto moves.  Even after the aggressive selling in US stocks we saw on Thursday/Friday, many positioning indicators don’t yet show full capitulation or a washout.  According to GS Prime, CTAs are still moderately net long the US market heading into this week, and their selling is likely to get more aggressive if downside continues.

On earnings – We came into Q2 with a high bar to clear, and the majority of SPX companies (369/500 have reported) have failed to significantly clear that bar so far.  We are tracking for the lowest proportion of revenue beats in 10+ quarters, and the lowest proportion of EPS beats in 7 quarters.

Earnings revision breadth has officially rolled over into negative territory for the 1st time since last Fall.  During that time, the SPX P/E multiple declined from 20x to 17x.

**Key Takeaways**

* We saw classic ‘risk off / flight to safety’ moves to end last week following recessionary-like ISM/NFP prints
* Bad = bad again, as the fear is now that the Fed is behind the curve
* Q2 Earnings have failed to impress so far given the huge YTD rally and elevated expectation coming in
* We are seeing risk off moves in Crypto this weekend and Asian markets on Monday.  US Equity Futures are indicating a big gap down for Monday morning.
* The selling may not be over yet based on positioning data and lack of capitulation signals
* I’m 100% cash in my trading account heading into this week

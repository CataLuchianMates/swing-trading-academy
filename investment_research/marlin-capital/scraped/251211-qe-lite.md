# QE Lite

#### **Yesterday’s Fed Meeting**

As expected, the Fed cut rates by 25 bps yesterday (to 3.50-3.75%), and signaled that additional easing will face a higher bar at their next meeting on January 28th.

In my mind, the big news of the day was this:

> “The Fed will start buying $40 billion of Treasury Bills, starting Dec 12”

The Fed’s announcement to purchase $40B per month in Treasury bills starting December 12th is being explicitly framed as “Reserve Management Purchases” (RMPs), aimed at maintaining ample reserve balances in the banking system.

Technically, this is not considered Quantitative Easing (QE), which involves broader asset purchases (including longer-dated securities) to stimulate economic activity and lower long-term yields.

But for me, it’s close enough. I would call this “QE Lite” or “Diet QE” due to the balance sheet expansion and likely spillover effects on liquidity.

In my view, this announcement is **undeniably** a very bullish tailwind for US equities. History confirms this.

**Previous Instances of QE Lite**

Looking back, the Fed has very rarely conducted targeted T-bill purchases explicitly for reserve management. The most comparable recent case was in October 2019, following a September repo market spike that strained liquidity. There are no other modern precedents out there.

**October 11, 2019 Announcement:**

In October 2019, the FOMC directed the New York Fed’s trading desk to buy ~$60B per month in T-bills (starting October 15th) to rebuild reserves after non-reserve liabilities grew and pushed reserves below “ample” levels. This was in response to the prior month’s funding stress, not economic stimulus. Purchases totaled ~$350B before shifting to full QE in March 2020 during the Covid crash.

At the time of the announcement (10/11/2019), the S&P 500 was within ~1.5% of ATHs.

This setup mirrors the current 2025 environment, where the SPX is also hovering just below ATHs at the time of the RMP announcement.

Back in 2019, the T-bill buying helped amplify the SPX uptrend (until the March 2020 Covid Crash began and they shifted to massive QE).

**ORCL Earnings**

The Nasdaq and Big Tech/Mag 7 are underperforming today after Oracle’s Q2 results fell short of expectations post-market yesterday. Q2 total revenue, operating income, and FCF all came in below expectations.

At the same time, Oracle raised its FY26 CapEx guide to be $15B higher than previously forecasted, driven by the accelerated pace of data center buildouts. Management also reiterated their FY26 total revenue outlook of $67B, even after booking an additional $68B of RPO this quarter (raising some questions about the monetization profiles of these contracts).

While there is certainly some disappointment from ORCL shareholders today, my most important takeaway from this report is that overall AI demand remains incredibly strong. For ORCL specifically, the disappointment stems from materially raising CapEx guidance while still missing financials targets (and not raising rev guidance). But for the AI trade as a whole, a Mega Cap hyperscaler like ORCL vowing to invest even more into AI is undeniably a positive.

Overall, ORCL’s decline is company specific and does not raise any concerns about the broader AI trade for me. In fact, ORCL’s CapEx guide raise should be viewed as a big positive for the AI Infrastructure trade, particularly Small and Mid Caps (which are outperforming and breaking out today).

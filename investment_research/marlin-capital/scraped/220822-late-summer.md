# Late Summer 

Equities have rallied big over the past two months led by:

**1.       Financial conditions easing (post July Fed Meeting)**

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!3y_c!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa7179ded-08f8-474e-82e6-08d8c7881fe3_409x406.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!3y_c!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa7179ded-08f8-474e-82e6-08d8c7881fe3_409x406.png)

As I highlighted in [“Making Sense of a Wild Summer”](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/making-sense-of-a-wild-summer), the 20 day easing of Financial Conditions post-July FOMC is among the largest of such events in the last decade (even larger than the late 2018 Powell Pivot).

**2.       Aggressive short covering among funds**

Over the last 20 days, the pace of hedge fund short covering in US single stocks places this recent rally among the top 5 institutional short squeezes of the last decade.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!mjEK!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F91a8db25-f0b2-473c-9e9c-9455e5f4b483_1254x706.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!mjEK!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F91a8db25-f0b2-473c-9e9c-9455e5f4b483_1254x706.png)

A bucket of the 50 most shorted stocks in the Russell 3000 (GSXUMSAL) bounced 45% off the June lows, compared to 20% for the Russell 3000 Index, 24% for the QQQ, and 18% for the S&P 500.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!ZOwx!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb70c07b7-c8ae-4456-a8bc-3c1d0e145918_869x543.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!ZOwx!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb70c07b7-c8ae-4456-a8bc-3c1d0e145918_869x543.png)

It has been a big squeeze off the lows, but now what?

The elephant in the room (inflation) is not simply going to retreat to 2%, especially with financial conditions easing so starkly.  Fed speaker Bullard reminded everyone last week that a 75 bp hike is still on the table for September.  We may have already seen peak inflation, but 8.5% CPI is still nothing to celebrate.  The market is currently pricing in a 50/50 chance of a 75 bp hike in September.

Reviewing last week’s Fed minutes, I thought it was **“significant”** that the only risk the committee categorized as **“significant”** was the idea that “elevated inflation could become entrenched”:

> “Participants judged that a significant risk facing the Committee was that elevated inflation could become entrenched if the public began to question the Committee’s resolve to adjust the stance of policy sufficiently.  If this risk materialized, it would complicate the task of returning inflation to 2% and could raise substantially the economic costs of doing so.”

Importantly, I believe the Fed is attempting to send a message that they will be running “restrictive policy for longer” – longer than the market is accustomed to and certainly longer than the market is willing to price.  Fed Funds Futures has started to catch on “a bit”, rising from an implied peak of 3.12% on 7/28 to the 3.7% current implied peak.  **But further out - starting in June 2023 - the market is pricing in -69 bps of Fed easing.  This is where the market is doubting/questioning the Fed’s resolve.** As the FOMC pointed out, this is **the #1** significant risk and will only serve to complicate matters.

So, how will the Fed handle this risk moving forward?  We will get a more definitive answer to that question with Fed Chair Jerome Powell set to speak at Jackson Hole in the latter half of this upcoming week.  Former Fed President Bill Dudley issued his Op-Ed on what he believes the Fed Chair must do:

> “To convince markets of the Fed’s resolve, Powell will have to be forceful.  Many see his warnings as mere rhetoric, designed to keep inflation expectations down.  They think that once the economy slows, unemployment rises, and inflation falls, the Fed will start cutting interest rates long before the 2% target has been achieved.  Powell will need to find a better way to persuade them that he has no intention of behaving like Arthur Burns (the Fed Chair who relented prematurely in the 1970s), lest he later be forced to act like Paul Volcker – who had to correct Burns’s mistake by putting the economy through two recessions.”

As I pointed out in [“Making Sense of a Wild Summer”](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/making-sense-of-a-wild-summer), the lesson from the Fed’s 1970 cycle was that they backed off tightening too soon, and then had to pivot back for a 2nd round of aggressive tightening.  The inconsistent monetary policy and uncertainty regarding inflation and economic growth led to a lost decade for equities.  This is precisely why my biggest long-term risk for US stocks (and the economy) is a premature Federal Reserve pivot, which could lead to a sustained period of “entrenched inflation” like the 70s.  The 2022 Bear Market is tracking the early 1970s Bear Market closely so far.

So, where does that leave us now?

Equities have rallied big off the lows, but the two main drivers of this rally (1.) Financial conditions easing; and (2.) aggressive short covering; are not sustainable for long term Bear Market lows.  There are already signs of both these drivers subsiding, and the rally beginning to fizzle out.

First off, the recent move in High Yield Bonds (HYG) is a “Risk Off” indicator.  The recent topping action should be viewed as a warning sign from the Bond market to all risk assets (equities).  A further move lower in HYG in the coming weeks is likely to be followed by a move lower in equities.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!f5EB!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1fac27ac-f9b2-4e8a-87be-ee3516b7d06e_1034x652.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!f5EB!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1fac27ac-f9b2-4e8a-87be-ee3516b7d06e_1034x652.png)

The US 2 YR Treasury Note is beginning to climb its way back towards highs of the year.  This will continue to be a key indicator to watch moving forward.  A breakout to new highs in the 2 YR (> 3.46%) is likely to resolve itself with new lows in the S&P 500.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Yo52!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa0b84f4b-8dfe-4c9b-9be8-788d3de9c1bc_1011x636.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Yo52!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa0b84f4b-8dfe-4c9b-9be8-788d3de9c1bc_1011x636.png)

Additionally, both the SPY and QQQ failed to break their 200 day moving averages on this rally, an indicator I use to assess long term trends.  As we head into next week, both indices have finally closed **below** their 8 EMAs for the first time in August.  Moreover, the QQQ gave its first Bearish MACD Cross since June 9th.  The QQQ gave a Bullish MACD Cross on June 24th, leading to a trend of higher prices for the index lasting nearly 2 months (before Friday's Bearish Crossover).  Now, we have the first signs of the uptrend beginning to fizzle out, with the first Bearish MACD cross since the rally began.

One thing is for certain – the Fed must tighten Financial Conditions much more than they already have to achieve their 2% Inflation goal.  Once again, this is a toxic recipe for equities and stock valuations.

Equity valuations have traded in line with real 10Y yields this year, with higher real interest rates leading to lower P/E multiples for the S&P 500. We know that is highly unlikely for inflation to return to 2% target with Real 10 year rates still in deeply negative range. There is still much tightening to be done here, meaning equity valuations could continue to be under pressure.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!4U5n!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4f82b6af-9a1b-4f57-84b0-6f28950fb5c2_463x271.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!4U5n!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4f82b6af-9a1b-4f57-84b0-6f28950fb5c2_463x271.png)

The Fed just allowed financial conditions to ease for nearly 2 months, but they must correct this error to tame inflation.  September is likely to be a month of steadily tightening financial conditions.  Speaking of September, it is the worst month of the year historically for US stocks.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!ukNk!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5163f6c4-cddb-4477-b54f-9d48feb1ab2b_973x711.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!ukNk!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5163f6c4-cddb-4477-b54f-9d48feb1ab2b_973x711.png)

In my view, we are likely to continue to have these tightening/loosening FCI episodes, correlating with risk off/risk on price action, until inflation is under control.  The Fed does see the risk of overtightening – as highlighted in the Fed minutes.  Therefore, it is logical that they will periodically allow financial conditions to ease in an attempt to avoid something “breaking” from the sheer pace of tightening.  But big picture -  it is going to take more restrictive monetary policy (for longer) than the market is currently pricing to tame inflation.  And perhaps the bigger risk for stocks (and the economy) is that Fed Funds Futures pricing is correct, and the Fed squanders their chance to tame inflation before it becomes entrenched in the US economy for the next 5-10 years. In any case, I do not believe the S&P 500 has seen its bottom for this cycle.

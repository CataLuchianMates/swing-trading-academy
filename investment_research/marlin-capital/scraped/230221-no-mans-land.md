# No Man's Land

The US economy is proving to be a lot more resilient than many expected just a few months ago.

If there was a collective takeaway from last week’s data set – notably retail sales, homebuilder sentiment, and jobless claims – it is consistent with that overarching narrative: as we pass the point of peak Fed tightening, the economic data has generally been stronger than anticipated.

However, last week introduced an element of complexity to the equation – the inflation data (namely PPI) was stronger than expected, and so too was the hawkish Fed commentary that followed.

[January CPI data](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/tuesday-morning-notes-214) was broadly consistent with the disinflation narrative, though PPI delivered an unexpected acceleration.  For January, CPI came in at 6.4% YoY versus 6.2% expected and 0.5% MoM, matching the consensus projection.  On the other hand, PPI printed 0.7% for January versus 0.4% forecasted.  This is a major acceleration from the -0.5% print for December.

Retail sales were strikingly strong for January, up 6.4% YoY and 3% MoM.  When adjusted for inflation, though, the YoY reading was flat (0% growth).

Jobless claims continue to print sub-200k, illustrating the strength of the US labor market.  We have seen a myriad of layoff announcements from big tech companies like Amazon, Meta, Microsoft, etc; but it is still yet to translate to the economic data coming through.

Fed speak clearly highlighted the need for the committee to do more, as both Mester and Bullard forced the market into a hawkish re-pricing of future monetary policy. This is demonstrated in the diagram below. Yet, even in the latest monetary pricing, investors continue to question the Fed’s resolve and plan to keep rates above 5% for the duration of 2023.

Long story short: the US economy is still impressively strong, and the Fed’s job is far from finished.

Historically, the most accurate indicator of a future recession is the inverted yield curve.  Currently, the US 2/10 YR Yield Curve is the most inverted it has been since 1981.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!r1Tb!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F430ba46a-1822-4334-9cb8-164f7f7a1ac9_932x386.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!r1Tb!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F430ba46a-1822-4334-9cb8-164f7f7a1ac9_932x386.png)

Could this time really be different? After all, this has been anything but a normal economic cycle.

Anything is possible, but I prefer to work in terms of probabilities.  The Fed’s dramatic shift toward restrictive monetary policy began just 11 months ago and is still working its way through the economy.

In historical context, the recent bullish performance of equities after the inversion of the yield curve is far from unprecedented.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!9odO!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9f96a55d-6286-4ea8-a344-7478b6f84324_999x523.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!9odO!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9f96a55d-6286-4ea8-a344-7478b6f84324_999x523.png)

In other words, equities have shown the ability to perform well while the yield curve is inverted and the economy still stable.  During this time, it is also common to believe a soft landing is on the way.  After all, the economic data is yet to meaningfully deteriorate.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!xo9K!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa854c112-8b59-4c1f-8b4b-a3d3769ccf90_2048x1302.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!xo9K!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa854c112-8b59-4c1f-8b4b-a3d3769ccf90_2048x1302.png)

While the underlying economic data has held up stronger than expected, corporate profits have actually been weaker than anticipated.  With 84% of S&P 500 companies having already reported Q4 results, GAAAP earnings came in at just $38.38 per share, which annualizes to $154.  The number is only slightly better using trailing twelve months (TTM) annualized, which would equate to GAAP earnings of $172.  Keep in mind that [the current consensus estimate](https://www.yardeni.com/pub/yriearningsforecast.pdf) for 2023 EPS is $223, 30% higher than TTM EPS annualized.

On the whole, analysts believe the worst of the EPS decline is already over (or taking place right now in Q1 ’23).  In fact, consensus projects Q2 ’23 to have the highest Q/Q EPS growth (31%) in recent memory.  This is a growth number that has only been reached during major expansionary phases of the economic cycle (2010, 2021).

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!pwlJ!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1e13c6d2-815d-4301-9536-d9d0d744a7b2_497x352.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!pwlJ!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1e13c6d2-815d-4301-9536-d9d0d744a7b2_497x352.png)

Long story short: consensus is projecting a “no landing/soft landing” scenario and a major economic expansion phase beginning this April. Economic expansions typically last multiple years, so it is safe to say consensus is not projecting any type of recession in the near future.

Moreover, the price action in equities has deviated from the historical recession path. With the recent rally, stocks are more closely following the path of “no recession”.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!3uDN!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F41ba58ba-2aa2-4750-ada6-1c9e61073208_904x456.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!3uDN!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F41ba58ba-2aa2-4750-ada6-1c9e61073208_904x456.png)

In other words, the market is now pricing in a “no landing/soft landing” Bear Market scenario.

**Technicals**

The current rally off October lows reached +20% in the S&P 500 (on 2/2/23) and +22% in the Nasdaq Composite (on 2/2/23).  Much more than any type of fundamental change that has taken place, the price action has led many to believe that the market has already bottomed.  After all, the market looks ahead, and could be discounting something that we are not seeing.

As I have discussed in several [reports this year](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/green-candles), the rally off October lows is indeed different technically than any that took place in 2022.  Not only is breadth stronger, it has also lasted longer in terms of duration (17 weeks low to high) and we saw a bullish golden cross, which did not occur during any bear market rally last year.

Historically though, such a bear market rally is not unprecedented.  In the early 2000s Dot Com Bust, the Nasdaq underwent a 51% rally before peaking and eventually making new lows.  The same rally was 25% in the S&P 500 and lasted 17 weeks (low to high), which, coincidentally (or not) is the same amount of time the current rally persisted (to the current peak on 2/2/23).  Yet, in 2002, both indices would later crater to new lows.

Famed market predictor Michael Burry actually alluded to this specific 2001-02 rally in a (since deleted) tweet on 1/24/23.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!mSVM!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2b326ef7-34c7-44df-bb96-39c4a6fc9035_1204x1170.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!mSVM!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2b326ef7-34c7-44df-bb96-39c4a6fc9035_1204x1170.png)

The essence of Michael Burry’s tweet is that we are currently in a similar type of bear market rally to the one we saw back then.  This is particularly interesting to me because, as I highlighted in [“Light At the End of the Tunnel”](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/light-at-the-end-of-the-tunnel), not all stocks bottom at the same time.  In the Dot Com Bust, many individual stocks bottomed before the indices did.

As you can see in the table below, certain stocks had already bottomed by September 2001 (when this aforementioned Bear Market rally began).

While the indices would go on to make new lows after the 17 week rally, there were a number of individual stocks that did not.  Let’s take AMZN, for example.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!WPT7!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa105485c-f8e5-446f-a8f6-f54411ec3980_1037x653.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!WPT7!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa105485c-f8e5-446f-a8f6-f54411ec3980_1037x653.png)

As I pointed out in [“We’re a Long Way from Kansas Now”,](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/were-a-long-way-from-kansas-now) this is where stocks will ultimately go their separate ways based off individual fundamentals.  In the report, I wrote:

> “All unprofitable companies were severely punished during the 2000-01 crash, but then, they each went their separate ways based off future profitability.  Companies that remained unprofitable continued to suffer over the next few years.  However, unprofitable companies that became profitable by 2004 rallied sharply, eventually catching up to companies that had been profitable all along.”

Yet, as I noted in [“Light at the end of the tunnel”,](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/light-at-the-end-of-the-tunnel) it was not all smooth sailing for these market leaders despite bottoming before the indices.  As you can see in the AMZN chart above, it was a relatively choppy journey with several 30%+ drawdowns along the way.

To compare current to history, this is where I believe we are in the current market environment.  While I continue to believe the indices have (at least) 1 more wave down to new lows, history tells us that some stocks have likely already hit their bear market lows.  Still, I believe it is prudent to wait for the next wave down in the indices for long term investors looking to scale in to their preferred names.  This is because (1) it is only clear in hindsight which names bottomed before the indices; and (2) even for the names that have hit their bear market lows, the next wave down in the indices is likely to provide a better price to buy (as seen with AMZN above).

As an active trader, I follow the technicals and the trend.  A big picture macro thesis is not something that can be traded on a day-to-day or week-to-week basis.  More importantly, the technicals will tell you when to get bearish or bullish and should serve as a confirmation of any larger thesis.  Further, trading the technicals is how I choose to manage risk, which is the most critical component for sustained success in the trading game.

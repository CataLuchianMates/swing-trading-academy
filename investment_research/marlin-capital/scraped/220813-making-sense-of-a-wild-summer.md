# Making Sense of a Wild Summer

What happened in the market the past month? Here is my best explanation:

1. The market perceived a dovish pivot by Powell and the Fed at the July meeting.  With this pivot, the market began to price in Federal Reserve easing in 2023, igniting a major rally in equities.
2. The squeeze in equities forced systematic long/short funds to de-risk their strategies, many of which were centered around fundamental short positions.  This exacerbated the momentum, magnitude, and speed of this move.

3. The peak inflation narrative took center stage under the weight of buying CTAs (~$100B since late July).
4. Earnings were not the negative catalyst that many were expecting them to be.
5. July CPI came in lower than expected (8.5% vs 8.7% est.), and increased 0% M/M.  The market’s confidence in a 2023 Federal Reserve pivot grew.
6. The market continued to rally because we are now past the point of peak Fed hawkishness and peak inflation.
7. Stubborn Hedge Fund Bears finally threw in the towel and capitulated, with [this week’s de-grossing activity](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/thursday-morning-notes-811) ranking among the top 10 in the last decade.

**So, what’s next?**

First off, there are some signs of this rally getting a little “long in the tooth”.  As mentioned above, the most stubborn bears are throwing in the towel and have already begun to capitulate on their shorts.

The 5-day upside move in the “GS Most Shorted Stocks” bucket was one of the largest we have seen in the past 5 years.

When highly shorted stocks lead a new rally, it is best to be cautious because short covering is much different than real buying.

As we head towards August Options Expiration next week, the real question becomes “how much longer/higher can this rally go?”

In my view, with the absence of any potentially immediate negative catalysts over the next 1-2 weeks, the most likely scenario is a grind higher with random intraday rug pulls to reset sentiment and put/call ratio when things start to get too extreme.  An object in motion tends to stay in motion, and there is no evidence of this recent uptrend ending just yet.  With so much momentum, it might take some type of catalyst to ignite a reversal.

**Macro Picture**

In the meantime, financial conditions have eased considerably following the “risk-on” post-FOMC interpretation from markets. Over the last 20 days, financial conditions have eased at one of the fastest paces on record.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!EOkY!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd164eda4-cc20-4dc9-b79e-578c29ffc1d0_956x716.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!EOkY!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd164eda4-cc20-4dc9-b79e-578c29ffc1d0_956x716.png)

The USD has gotten hammered, credit spreads have tightened, and commodities have rallied (Gasoline +7.2% WTD, WTI Crude +2.8%, Industrial Metals +4.1%, Lumber +21.8%). All of this will help to undermine the Fed’s fight against inflation.

To me, the big picture scenario that I am most concerned about from here is inflation flatlining at 4-6% in early/mid 2023, instead of smoothly sailing back down to 2%. What if inflation declines to 5%, and then spikes back up to 7%?

This could result in a replay of the dreaded 1970s style “Secular Inflationary Bear” scenario for equities, where the economy is characterized by a prolonged period of high inflation and low growth (Stagflation).  In the 70s, a series of premature rate cuts led to inflation lingering, and the Fed having to reverse course shortly thereafter.  The market followed suit, rallying >30% and making new highs after the original dovish pivot, only to reverse course and break lows when the Fed was forced to start tightening again (to deal with the inflation that never went away).  The Dow Jones broke 1970s lows almost 5 years later (in 1975), in what was a lost decade for US stocks.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!cejL!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd28dfcf7-f0f1-48b3-9408-2a01dee7c8f7_634x439.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!cejL!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd28dfcf7-f0f1-48b3-9408-2a01dee7c8f7_634x439.png)

The point of this story is that it is critical for inflation to be extinguished before a meaningful long-term low can really be in place for equities.  In my view, a prolonged period of high inflation and low growth, with unpredictable monthly CPI spikes, would likely correlate with a Secular Bear Market in equities, similar to the 70s.  If inflation is not fully extinguished and persists in 2023, the market will continue to be at the mercy of CPI (i.e. CPI spikes from 5% to 7%, market likely falls sharply 10-20%).

The 1970s analog warns about the dangers of the Federal Reserve cutting rates too soon, and makes the argument for more restrictive monetary policy for longer (i.e., pause at 4%, but not straight to rate cuts).  Federal Reserve members Evans and Kashkari appeared to agree with these views in their comments this week, with both members saying that they do NOT expect the committee to cut rates at all in 2023, let alone in Q1 2023 like the market has priced.

In my view, I expect the Fed to proceed with more restrictive policy for longer, meaning a likely pause once rates reach ~4% in 2023.  With the Unemployment Rate currently at 3.5%, the Fed is nowhere near close to cutting rates.  I am unsure what the catalyst will be (potentially Jackson Hole 8/25-8/27), but the market will eventually need to price out the early 2023 rate cuts.  This should lead to a reversal in equities and some retracing of this recent rally, although this is unlikely to be the catalyst to bring new lows.  It will be critical to watch how both the indices and stocks react once the market comes to grips with the ”restrictive policy for longer” regime we are likely to see.  Even in a 2023 pause (rather than cut) scenario for the Fed, the biggest risk for equities remains that a second bout of Fed tightening will be needed to fully cure inflation.

**Strategy**

I believe the most likely scenario is for the market to grind higher over the next 1-2 weeks, with the QQQ reaching its 200-day MA ($342), and perhaps overshooting it by a bit.  With that said, and as mentioned above, we are at the point in the rally where downside risks are elevated, and it is best to be cautious.   I will continue to focus on short term trades for now, as I believe we are in the 9th inning of this rally and do not want to get caught in the eventual rug pull.  Additionally, it would not be surprising to see the market front run Jackson Hole (8/25-8/27) with selling, as Powell is aware that he cannot allow financial conditions to continue to ease with inflation at 8.5%.  The Fed notched a big win this week with inflation finally peaking, but the game is far from over.

For individual stocks, it will be very important to watch how they each react to the next market pullback.  As I talked about in “[Light At The End Of The Tunnel”](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/light-at-the-end-of-the-tunnel), I believe many individual stocks are likely in the process of bottoming.  Most of them remain in the “Stage 1” basing period I discussed in the report.  As a trader, this is not my ideal time to buy.  In this regard, I am in agreement with legendary trader Mark Minervini in waiting for the new trend to fully develop before entering for a swing trade.

As an investor, I expect that each of my IRA stocks have begun the bottoming process and entered Stage 1.  With that said, I do expect each of them to sell off in the next major FCI tightening/risk-off episode.  This is where I will look to accumulate shares and deploy much of the large cash position (~40%) in my IRA.

If there are any long-term positions you are unsure about or no longer have a high conviction on, now is the time to unload after a massive “everything bounce”.  Even in the scenario that the lows of this Bear Market are in, as some now believe, the next major FCI tightening/risk-off episode is likely to result in a painful bout of backtesting this rally off the June lows.  In March 2020, crisis policy from the Fed allowed stocks to stage a V-shaped bottom (and ultimately enter a massive bubble).  This time around, the Fed’s job is still far from over, and they will need to strategically turn the FCI tightening levers up (causing risk-off episodes) to achieve their goal.

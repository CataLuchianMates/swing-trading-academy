# Red Flags Emerge

Last week was an especially challenging one for US stocks culminated by (1) a large momentum stock drawdown in high flyers, (2) WMT disappointing on earnings; and (3) poor economic data.

Goldman’s 12M Winners (Long Momentum Basket) traded down -8.6% on the week as retail investor favorites like PLTR, HIMS, RKLB, etc. got crushed. Speaking of retail investors, the euphoria we have seen in that cohort of the market should be viewed as an obvious red flag. 2 weeks ago, we witnessed the largest weekly retail investor buying on record.

To expand on this, retail investors/traders are often viewed as a contrarian signal because their behavior tends to reflect emotional extremes (either excessive optimism or pessimism), which can signal market tops or bottoms. The idea is that when the masses all pile into 1 side of a trade, it is often a signal that the trend is nearing exhaustion.

Legendary investor Howard Marks has written in detail about this market psychology dynamic:

> “The majority of investors—especially nonprofessionals—are heavily influenced by psychology, and their involvement in the market is often governed by emotion rather than reason. When they’re wildly optimistic and pouring money into stocks, it’s usually a sign the market is overheated. Conversely, when they’re despairing and selling, it’s often a signal that the worst is over.
>
> “When the crowd is euphoric, it’s time to be cautious; when it’s terrified, it’s time to be aggressive. The behavior of the majority tells you more about the market’s temperature than its direction.”

Historically, retail traders get most excited near market highs (i.e. the Dot Com Bubble or 2021-22 Meme Stock Mania) and most pessimistic/fearful near lows (2008 GFC).

Based on elevated risk appetite and speculation (retail euphoria) in the market as well as some of the parabolic moves we’ve seen with potential climax/blowoff tops, there are definitely some parallels from now to that time. [During that time](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/were-not-in-kansas-anymore?utm_source=publication-search), we had parabolic/climax tops in more speculative names (SPACs, ARKK, etc) before the indices topped.

While there are some parallels that can be drawn from now to back then, the main difference to me in 2025 is the lack of Macro catalyst. In late 2021 and early 2022, it became clear the Fed was way behind on inflation and were going to have to raise rates very aggressively.

Marlin Capital readers who have been with me since the beginning will remember my [bearish market calls during that time](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/were-not-in-kansas-anymore?utm_source=publication-search). Market psychology (retail euphoria) and technicals (climax tops, etc.) both played a big part in my bearish calls at the time, but the Fed’s aggressive pivot is what ultimately gave me the confidence that we were heading for a Bear Market. This time around, I do not see the same type of catalyst for a period of sustained selling (i.e. a Bear Market).

With that said, it is typical for the S&P 500 to see a -10% drawdown/correction in most years. From 1928-2021, the SPX saw at least one -10% correction in 63% of years (i.e., most years see a -10%+ correction).

We could certainly be heading for one here in early 2025 if NVDA earnings fail to impress this week (more on that below).

In my mind, there are some definite red flags that have emerged here.

1. Retail euphoria
2. Speculative melt ups/parabolic tops (HIMS, RKLB, TEM, GRAL, OKLO, etc.)
3. Walmart earnings pointing to a weaker consumer, which is a red flag for the economy and consumer based stocks.

In addition to the 3 main red flags I have noted above, we are simply seeing a much more difficult environment for new long trades with volatility, false breakouts, big gap downs, etc. We are also seeing some of the top earnings gappers (i.e. stocks that announced the biggest earnings beats) fail and break down, even after announcing great results. When stocks are getting sold off on good news, it is a bearish signal as it means the best has already been discounted.

While retail traders have been piling into stocks/crowded names, we have seen broad signs of de-risking from the Hedge Fund community. During last week’s sell off, Hedge Funds de-risked aggressively in Tech. The de-grossing over the past 2 weeks was the largest since July and ranks in the top 4 “risk off” events for Hedge Funds in the past 5 years (99th percentile selling).

From a flows perspective, this is obviously a bearish setup. We have Hedge Funds and institutions de-risking aggressively while Retail demand is now starting to fade after record buying over the last month.

We also have mechanical beginning of year flows fading (retirement, 529, etc.). January and February are typically the 2 strongest months of the year for yearly asset allocations before a seasonal lull in March as Tax Day approaches.

In summary, I believe some caution is definitely warranted here based off the red flags I provided (and my recent long trades are telling me as much).

The next major catalyst for the market is NVDA earnings this week (on Wednesday AH). NVDA is the 2nd largest weighting in the SPX (6.6%) and the options market is implying a 9% move on a $3.4T market cap (~$300B+ move in the name). Needless to say, this is a huge event for the market and the AI trade in general.

The S&P 500 is sitting right at its 50-day moving average and heading into NVDA earnings in a precarious position technically.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Lh2i!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3cc62477-df04-448d-98dc-1331d006fb6b_1191x885.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Lh2i!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3cc62477-df04-448d-98dc-1331d006fb6b_1191x885.jpeg)

There is no question that the market is vulnerable here if NVDA results fail to impress, and expectations are certainly high going in. With that said, I wouldn’t take an overly bearish stance heading into NVDA results as Nvidia is the AI king and I have never doubted Jensen’s ability to execute (and I’m not about to start now). It would also be no surprise to see Jensen calm markets with a blowout earnings report as NVDA so often does.

Overall, I see caution warranted here in the short term, especially on new long trades. If NVDA earnings this week fail to impress, I do see the writing on the wall for a -10% correction in the indices. This type of volatility is ordinary (happens in 63% of years) and I would view as a buying opportunity (absent a significant deterioration in my economic outlook).

With that said, we could just as easily see Jensen calm markets and provide a big catalyst for Tech buying (especially in funds that just de-risked).

In my view, a lot hinges on NVDA earnings, and I do not see any particular edge in getting aggressive either way prior to the numbers being released Wednesday AH. Stay tuned for my report post-NVDA earnings later this week as I dive into what I see coming next.

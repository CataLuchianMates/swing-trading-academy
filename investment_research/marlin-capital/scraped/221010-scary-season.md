# Scary Season

2022 has been a brutal year for stocks, with US equities on track for their worst year since 2008.

I believe equities have room to fall even further, as prices are still not fully reflecting the combination of higher rates and recessionary risks.

**Macro Update**

**Valuations remain elevated**

Higher real interest rates typically lead to lower equity valuations.  The surge higher in real yields suggests that there is more valuation compression ahead for the indices.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Cf0Z!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F304d2c53-1200-4ad4-bef2-96cd6e09fe01_447x457.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Cf0Z!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F304d2c53-1200-4ad4-bef2-96cd6e09fe01_447x457.png)

The chart overlay above is suggesting that market wide valuations remain elevated when accounting for the move higher in real interest rates.  Based on real yields, the Forward P/E Ratio for both the S&P 500 and Nasdaq 100 should be closer to 14x.  Such a decline in the Nasdaq 100 would be upwards of 30% from the current multiple.

The S&P 500’s LTM EV/Sales multiple remains elevated above the 10-year average.  Further, previous Bear Markets (excluding the Covid crash) each bottomed below the 30 year average of 1.6x.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!YStM!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F91996d7f-1b65-4819-8a34-73fb238b833a_607x422.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!YStM!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F91996d7f-1b65-4819-8a34-73fb238b833a_607x422.png)

**Earnings estimates are at risk**

Recessionary risks are rising quickly, but 2023 earnings estimates have remained stable.  Consensus estimates are calling for 2023 EPS of $241 for the S&P 500, representing 7% growth YoY.

Leading indicators for EPS are suggesting a 2023 earnings recession is more likely instead.  Corporate and CEO sentiment has plummeted, pointing to further downside risk in earnings.

Earnings breadth is deteriorating in both stocks and industry groups.  71% of industry groups are seeing estimate downgrades from the previous quarter.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!DQ9v!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fcf110053-15b9-47ed-8492-735a6513f4e9_1319x823.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!DQ9v!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fcf110053-15b9-47ed-8492-735a6513f4e9_1319x823.png)

Many companies are already struggling to meet lofty 2022 estimates.  Negative earnings pre-announcements tracked at a decade high in August, according to BofA.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!OUhF!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb40fa785-76fe-461b-8225-66e0ffb0c573_470x379.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!OUhF!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb40fa785-76fe-461b-8225-66e0ffb0c573_470x379.png)

Companies’ mentions of “weak demand” has already jumped prior recession levels (2020 and 2008).

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!uSTV!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F46b5ebdd-3a42-4d3f-a0f4-123a1807465f_465x425.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!uSTV!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F46b5ebdd-3a42-4d3f-a0f4-123a1807465f_465x425.png)

This is a problematic scenario for earnings, because the Federal Reserve must further crush demand to bring inflation back to target range.  It should also be noted that monetary policy works with a lag, and it can take over 1 year for the economy to feel the full effects.  On this note (and the above indicators), I believe that 2023 earnings estimates are vulnerable to future downside revisions.  **The market cannot bottom until the earnings shoe drops.**

**The Fed is still tightening aggressively**

Historically, equities do not bottom until after the tightening cycle is complete.  In each of the previous 7 Bear Markets dating back to 1973, the S&P 500 did not bottom until after the Fed began cutting rates.  On average, the index bottomed 11 months after the first rate cuts.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!1zCV!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6a3d26c8-70ff-49ec-a1ed-94cc4bf63964_1101x731.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!1zCV!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6a3d26c8-70ff-49ec-a1ed-94cc4bf63964_1101x731.png)

[As I have noted in the past](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/bear-market-bottom-checklist), there is some question on when the first rate cuts will come during this cycle.  BofA has forecasted the first rate cuts to come in Q3 2023, while many Federal Reserve members have continued to claim that there will be no rate cuts in 2023.   In any case, my view is that rate cuts will not become a realistic proposition until the second half of 2023, at the earliest.

In 2022, each time financial conditions have eased (and equities have rallied), the Fed has pulled them tighter again (leading to new lows for equities).  The recent bout of tightening was the 3rd sharpest 15-day tightening event since 2000, behind only the 2008 GFC and March 2020 Covid Crash.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Li-d!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Facb1c549-647b-44ee-9cd8-d6e14a016fa8_740x536.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Li-d!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Facb1c549-647b-44ee-9cd8-d6e14a016fa8_740x536.png)

I would expect this cycle to continue until inflation is under control, as “financial tightening” is the Fed’s only method of solving inflation.

Undoubtedly, the most important “Fed Pivot Indicator” to watch is the Unemployment Rate.  After all, the Fed has a dual mandate to (1) keep inflation low and (2) to make sure a maximum number of Americans are employed.  Inflation is continuing to run near 40 year highs, while Unemployment is continuing to run near 20 year lows.  This leaves the Fed no reason to pivot away from their hawkish stance **until** unemployment spikes significantly OR inflation retreats back to target **first**.

Historically, labor market weakness [precedes equity market bottoms](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/inflation-versus-unemployment-research).  There is still no weakness to be found in the labor market (yet), so there is no reason to look for a Fed pivot.  Fed Chair Jerome Powell has actively talked about the Fed’s intent to “soften” the labor market, as this is a necessary ingredient to curing inflation.  In my view, the Unemployment Rate must spike to above 4.5% (at a very minimum) before the Fed begins to pivot.  This is also in line with SF Fed President Daly’s [recent commentary](https://www.reuters.com/markets/us/feds-daly-need-further-rate-hikes-beat-inflation-2022-10-04/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CThere's%20a%20lot%20of%20room,higher%20levels%20some%20have%20forecast.).

Labor market weakness promises to be another upcoming headwind for the equity markets.

**Technical Update**

In my view, we are in the 3rd Wave down of this Bear Market.  This does not necessarily need to be the final leg down of the Bear Market, but as I highlighted in [“The Worst Third”](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/the-worst-third), the magnitude of declines tend to get worse the deeper (in duration) the Bear gets.  In simplest terms, **the worst declines in Bear Markets are historically at the end.**

The VIX is nearing its highs of the year, as some signs of fear have finally shown up in the market.  As a result, I would expect more volatility in both directions.  Counter-trend bounces in the first 2 Wave Downs typically failed near the 21 EMA (purple line below) and lasted 1-5 days.  Last week’s bounce fits that bill, with the indices failing at the 21 EMA thus far.

My targets remain the same on this 3rd Wave down.  For the S&P 500, my target is $2900-$3300.

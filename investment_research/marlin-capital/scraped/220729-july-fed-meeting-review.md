# July Fed Meeting Review

Yesterday, the Federal Reserve voted unanimously to raise the target range for the Federal Funds Rate by 75 bps to 2.25-2.50%.  According to Jerome Powell’s assessment, the policy rate is now near “neutral” after this aggressive frontloading cycle.  This has led many investors to conclude that we have passed the point of “Peak Fed Hawkishness”, and most expect the rate of increases to significantly slow from here.

In fact, the market believes that the Federal Reserve is not too far from pivoting all together.  The Fed funds futures market predicts that this tightening cycle will conclude by the end of 2022, with the federal funds rate near 3.3%.  This means that the market is forecasting an additional 80-90 bps of hikes for the remainder of 2022 (and this entire tightening cycle).  After yesterday’s FOMC meeting, markets responded by shaving about 10 bps off the future funds rate, in what was a dovish interpretation of events.

Fed Chair Jerome Powell stopped short of providing any type of detailed guidance for September or any other upcoming meetings, instead saying the committee will rely heavily on the upcoming data to determine the appropriate path of action.  The market interpreted this as bullish, because recent data shows a “softening economy”, as the committee so eloquently put it.  The reasoning for the market’s bullish interpretation is clear – softening economic data should force the Fed to pivot away from their hawkish stance.

To me, the market’s reaction to deteriorating economic data in this instance almost seems like a reflex – that is, we have become very accustomed to the Federal Reserve “rescuing” the economy at the very first sign of any growth slowdown.  There is a recency bias of investors to believe that “bad economic data is good”, because it will force the Fed to not only cut rates, but also potentially enact another wide scale Quantitative Easing program.

Speaking of rallying off of bad economic data, Thursday’s (unofficial) US Real GDP reading came in at -0.9%.  This marks the second consecutive quarter of declining GDP in the US, which would meet the criteria for the technical definition of a Recession.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Qe2M!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F350df125-af39-45f4-9f7c-a0e50a1eae51_904x496.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Qe2M!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F350df125-af39-45f4-9f7c-a0e50a1eae51_904x496.png)

Today’s Q2 GDP number remains unofficial and subject to revision in the coming weeks.  Even if the final Q2 GDP number remains negative, something tells me this will still not be classified as an official recession yet, perhaps because of the White House’s attempts to change the definition of what a recession is.  In Wednesday’s press conference, Fed Chair Powell said he does not believe the US is currently in a Recession, pointing to the strong labor market as the number 1 reason why.

For investors, Powell’s answer should be seen as hawkish, in my opinion.  The old Jerome Powell, leader of the “bad is good” market regime, likely would have been cutting rates after 1 quarter of negative GDP growth.  The new Jerome Powell, who has his hands firmly tied with 9.1% Inflation, is refusing to classify two consecutive quarters of negative GDP as a recession, and instead highlighting the strength of the economy.

So the question is: If two consecutive negative GDP prints can’t force the Fed to consider pivoting, what will?  In my view, there are only two scenarios that will cause the Fed to pivot.

1.    A dramatic decline in Inflation, for multiple consecutive months

2.    A surge in Unemployment above 5-6% minimum

Number 1 is obvious.  The Federal Reserve is hiking so aggressively to fight inflation.  The market believes that the Fed will ultimately win the fight against Inflation (at the expense of growth, albeit), as 1YR forward Inflation expectations clearly show below.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!L4ag!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4ecf17de-ba30-4658-96ff-b749a8a4047a_1213x645.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!L4ag!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4ecf17de-ba30-4658-96ff-b749a8a4047a_1213x645.png)

If correct, July 2023 CPI is priced to print at around 2.9%, which would basically constitute a drop to the Fed’s target inflation range.

The second scenario in which I envision a Federal Reserve pivot, is if we see a significant spike in the Unemployment Rate.  The Fed’s dual mandate only includes achieving “maximum employment” and “stable prices”.  I have discussed the relationship between [Unemployment and CPI here](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/inflation-versus-unemployment-research), and why I believe it is necessary to see a surge in joblessness in order for Inflation to return to the Fed’s target range.  Fed Chair Powell alluded to this fact in Wednesday’s Press Conference:

> “In all likelihood we will need softening of the labor market conditions [for inflation to return to target], and that will be necessary.”

As I have pointed out before, history paints a clear picture of how this story ends – with a Recession AND a spike in the Unemployment Rate above 6%.  None of the [previous 4 inflationary recessions that I studied](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/inflation-versus-unemployment-research) can be classified as “mild” or a “soft landing”.  This is due in large part to the fact that the Federal Reserve has always acted very aggressively in periods of high inflation.  As you can see in the graph below, the Fed has always raised the Federal Funds Rate ABOVE the inflation rate in order to achieve price stability.  Why then, does the market believe a peak Federal Funds Rate of 3.4% will be enough to bring inflation down from 9.1% to 2%.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!gt9M!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F846de2b4-7d4b-4c09-86e9-27f8491926bb_927x520.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!gt9M!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F846de2b4-7d4b-4c09-86e9-27f8491926bb_927x520.png)

Looking at the full collection of data, I believe many investors agree that CPI peaked last month at 9.1%.  Despite this potential fact, we remain a long way from the Fed’s 2% inflation target.  The question is, how sticky will inflation be from here? With headline CPI of 9.1% and a tight labor market (3.6% unemployment), Non-Farm Payrolls consistently printing over 400k+ job additions per month, the Atlanta Fed Wage Growth Tracker at all-time highs of 6.7%, and recent consumer nominal spending data showing a more resilient consumer than expected, I believe inflation will prove to be more persistent than the market is currently prepared for.

To make matters worse, the financial market’s “dovish interpretation” of the July FOMC meeting will only make the Central Bank’s job more challenging.  Following yesterday’s meeting, financial conditions eased, equities and commodities surged higher, the dollar weakened, and we saw a sharp drop in real yields.  A weaker dollar is inflationary, so are higher commodity prices, and the drop in real yields indicates that the market is reacting to a more dovish Fed than anticipated.

For me, the issue is that the market seems to be pricing in the Best Case Scenario – a very mild recession that will allow the Fed to start cutting rates 5 months from now, and a smooth return to 2-3% CPI within the next 12 months.  The market is certainly NOT pricing in a major surge in unemployment or anything in the system “breaking” (i.e. major credit event), as we have often seen following aggressive tightening cycles.  Additionally, the market is certainly NOT pricing in any type of bumpy path ahead for inflation, as that would assume the Fed will not be in a position to cut rates so soon/smoothly.

If the current market view is correct, this post-FOMC rally makes a lot of sense.  After all, a smooth sail back to the Fed’s 2-3% Inflation target, with only a mild recession along the way, is a much better outcome than most everyone was expecting.  In the market view, we are nearing the end of this tightening cycle, and the Fed will be cutting rates for the entirety of 2023 (yet, through this ‘23 monetary easing, inflation will smoothly sail right on back to target??).

There are a lot of things that can go wrong with the current rosy market view of how this cycle will end.  I do not view such a perfectly manicured landing as likely.  In my opinion, a mild recession will not be enough to bring inflation back down to target, nor will a peak 3.4% Federal Funds Rate that is immediately followed by significant monetary policy easing in 2023.

With all that said, the market currently has its rose-colored glasses on, and we have already gotten through various potential negative catalysts this week (FOMC, Big Tech Earnings).  For the indices, the short and medium term trends are pointing UP.  The market is in rally mode, while the next FOMC decision is not scheduled until September.  Things should get a lot quieter after this week, which could help this rally to continue for awhile longer.

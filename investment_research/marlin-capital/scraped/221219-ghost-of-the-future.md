# Ghost of the Future

The key takeaways from the final major news cycle of 2022 are clear: the trend in US Inflation is indisputably pointing down, and the Fed intends to dial things back, leaving expectations for the terminal rate just below 5% come June.

With that, the principal Macro narrative has shifted from “Inflation” to “Recession”.  When (not if) the Recession theme starts to take on more weight, equities are certainly NOT priced for a hard landing.

Look no further than forward earnings estimates for the S&P 500.  While forward earnings estimates have fallen considerably from peak levels, the consensus is still calling for 4% EPS growth in 2023.

Since WWII, the shallowest earnings recession was -4.6% (in 1980).  Even in the inflationary mid-1970s, the earnings recession was -14.8% from its peak.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!GfIH!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F03f17e02-23aa-4a90-b344-1b75616136ac_600x326.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!GfIH!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F03f17e02-23aa-4a90-b344-1b75616136ac_600x326.png)

Equities also tend to bottom when EPS cuts stop.  In other words, the rate of change in EPS cuts typically slows before stocks bottom.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!sv9O!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2f772f50-9cea-423f-a420-2e47173d8cd5_680x355.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!sv9O!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2f772f50-9cea-423f-a420-2e47173d8cd5_680x355.png)

In my view, the velocity of EPS cuts is likely to accelerate over the coming months (not decelerate), making it very difficult for stocks to bottom.

There is currently a large disparity between different analysts’ earnings estimates for 2023.  In fact, the negative divergence between Morgan Stanley’s LEI (Leading Economic Indicators) - based Earnings Model and consensus is the largest since August 2008.  August 2008 was not a good time to buy stocks, as the SPX dropped another 47% before bottoming 7 months later.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!oI6q!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe1e9cfff-ac17-4472-a081-a43b027c6895_431x252.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!oI6q!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe1e9cfff-ac17-4472-a081-a43b027c6895_431x252.png)

It should also be noted that Morgan Stanley, led by Chief Investment Strategist Mike Wilson, has been the most accurate of the sell-side banks this year.  Many sell-side banks follow the “herd mentality”, but to their credit, Morgan Stanley continues to lead the herd this year (which is why I often share excerpts of Mike Wilson’s work in the daily morning notes).

Speaking of LEIs (leading economic indicators), stocks typically follow their lead.  In other words, equities have historically bottomed after leading economic indicators have bottomed.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!D3CK!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F255ed213-8d83-4626-b06d-59f618904dd2_745x402.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!D3CK!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F255ed213-8d83-4626-b06d-59f618904dd2_745x402.png)

All signs point to the economy deteriorating further in 2023.  One such example is the labor market.  The Federal Reserve [currently projects](https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/fomcprojtabl20221214.htm) the unemployment rate to rise to a peak of 4.6% (from 3.7% currently) by the end of 2024.  However, the Fed has a long history of underestimating the eventual pain that monetary tightening brings to the labor market.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!NXX-!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbda64328-63be-4f16-89df-09a1ce0f760c_575x289.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!NXX-!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbda64328-63be-4f16-89df-09a1ce0f760c_575x289.png)

The economy and labor market have a direct affect on equity prices.  Investors tend to rotate towards cash when the outlook for the economy deteriorates and unemployment rises.

When the “hard landing” theme starts to take more weight, households will likely reallocate from stocks to cash.  This has not yet occurred in 2022.  In fact, US households have been net buyers of equities YTD, pumping another $100B into stocks this year.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!xmaj!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F126e3225-79c2-4889-9c9d-32a64c3203a7_836x314.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!xmaj!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F126e3225-79c2-4889-9c9d-32a64c3203a7_836x314.png)

According to Goldman Sachs, Households currently allocate 43% of their financial assets to equities, which is just off the peak levels seen in early 2022 (46%).

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!F2VZ!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fca509677-8d7a-46c4-a966-9c2988c20f9e_423x332.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!F2VZ!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fca509677-8d7a-46c4-a966-9c2988c20f9e_423x332.png)

The average Household equity allocation since 1952 is just 28%.  In the previous 2 major Recessionary Bear Markets (2000 and 2008), household equity allocations dipped below 33% before hitting a trough.

While we have heard much about extreme “Bearish Investor Sentiment” in financial news media this year, the aforementioned facts do not quite line up with that narrative.  This is likely due to the fact that the **consumer** has felt little to no pain from the monetary tightening cycle thus far.  I expect this to change in 2023, forcing investors to shed some equity exposure in favor of cash.

**Technical Update**

A couple weeks ago (12/4), [I wrote](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/an-early-gift): “the technicals are suggesting this rally will likely peak and fail in the next 1-2 weeks.”  In my view, that peak was likely achieved last week with a false breakout/double top near $4100.

On the weekly chart, the S&P 500 failed to move above its 40-wk moving average, and closed with an ugly reversal candle.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!M-vj!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbbcfbbe4-5409-430b-930a-adee33c0f3e1_1032x653.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!M-vj!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbbcfbbe4-5409-430b-930a-adee33c0f3e1_1032x653.png)

With that, I believe we have started the 4th wave down of this Bear Market.  As you can see above, the previous 3 waves down lasted 8, 12, and 9 weeks in duration.  If we continue to follow this pattern, it would suggest the SPX continues this wave down until February.  However, these down waves are not without counter-trend bounces, even on the weekly time frame.

On a shorter time frame (30 minute), we have broken the month-long trading range to the downside, with the SPX losing the key $3900 support level.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Bei_!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8c36ec0d-80aa-41ab-9a16-069c7db9e7e1_1363x651.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Bei_!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8c36ec0d-80aa-41ab-9a16-069c7db9e7e1_1363x651.png)

We head into this week short-term oversold after a roughly 250 point (-6%) drop in the index following the December FOMC meeting.  On a technical basis, I am looking for an oversold bounce back to the $3900 level early this week.  In my view, any oversold bounce could top out below $3975 (so this a potential stop loss level for those looking to short).

A break below $3820 could set us on the path to filling the $3750 Nov. CPI gap level.  In my view, the technicals are suggesting that this gap will be fully filled before the end of the year.

**Summary**

* Peak Inflation and Peak Fed Tightening are **both** likely behind us.
* A Recession lies ahead in 2023. Leading economic indicators point to more downside for equities.
* Forward EPS cuts have started to come, but are still priced for nothing more than a modest growth slowdown in 2023.
* Purposefully or not, the Fed always underestimates the pain monetary tightening brings to the economy and labor market. This time is no different.
* Households have been Net Buyers of equities YTD (+$100B), while institutions have de-risked sharply. Historically, households rotate towards cash when the economy and labor market deteriorate.
* The technical pattern suggests that we are 2 weeks into the 4th wave down of this Bear Market. The previous 3 down waves lasted an average of 9 weeks, suggesting this 4th wave down will persist into February 2023.

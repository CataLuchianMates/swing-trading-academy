# The Big Unwind

First off, a farewell to my [Small Cap 6 Portfolio](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/6-small-caps-with-massive-upside).

I exited these positions in full last week, locking in +99.4% gains overall in just over 7 months (+226.7% annualized gain).

To ultimately have long term success in the market, you must make sure you get paid when you are right. One of the GOATs in the investing world, Stanley Druckenmiller, has always stressed the importance of maximizing gains when you are right and minimizing losses when you are wrong. In a 2018 Bloomberg interview, he elaborated on this concept:

> "It’s not whether you’re right or wrong that’s important, but how much money you make when you’re right and how much you lose when you’re wrong."
>
> Stanley Druckenmiller

This concept is known as asymmetric risk/reward investing. Ensuring that the upside of your correct call far outweighs the downside of your incorrect call is how you put yourself in a position to succeed as a trader/investor. Here’s how it played out in my Small Cap 6 Portfolio:

Even though my win rate was only 50% in this portfolio (3/6), I still ended up doubling my money overall in just over 7 months.

This is also why I always emphasize locking in profits progressively. When you have a multi-bagger (especially in a few months time like we got with SEZL, INOD, and WGS), the most important thing is getting paid for it.   
  
When I worked on the trading floor, I had a mentor that would always say “lock in profits, otherwise it’s **not** your money”.   
  
Until you sell, you have a **paper gain**. These are simply numbers on a screen. The way you protect big gains is by locking in profits progressively. For me, in a longer term portfolio where my strategy was to target potential multi-bagger returns, I look to lock in profits at key levels and century marks (+100%, +200%, +300%, etc). At its peak, SEZL was a 6x. INOD was a 4x. WGS was a 2.5x. You need to be ringing the register hard when you achieve gains like this (let alone in a few months time).  
  
So for me, the decision to close the Small Cap 6 Portfolio was made much easier by the fact that I was locking in profits in my top 3 names and only holding a runner. I certainly wouldn’t blame you if you kept a runner in the 3 big winners (~10-15%, SEZL, INOD, and WGS). But for me personally, closing out the whole portfolio for +99% profits in 7 months is how I am handling it for myself. I can only speak to how I am handling it for myself and that is what I cover here at Marlin Capital.

This is where I adjust my strategy systematically. Market conditions, particularly for Small Caps and more speculative names, have deteriorated meaningfully. They lost key support levels and Bull market moving averages that held the whole ride up. Systemically, the R2K has lost the uptrend.   
  
For that reason, I no longer view a buy and hold portfolio of Small Caps as the ideal portfolio for our current market environment. If we bottom and start a new uptrend soon, I will systematically adjust for those conditions. That would entail a weekly close back above the 40 week and daily closes above the 200 SMA.

Anyone who has experienced **real extreme fear** in the market (like when the Nasdaq was falling -13% in a day during the March 2020 Covid Crash and you couldn’t pay people to buy stocks, or at the end of the 2022 Bear Market when people were throwing in the towel and no one wanted META at $88 or NVDA at $12), knows this isn’t really it.  
  
While sentiment indicators are useful to monitor (especially in the short term), I view Positioning > Sentiment. Positioning is the ultimate sentiment indicator, in my opinion, because it tells you how investors are actually feeling. Benjamin Graham called the market a voting machine (in the short term). People vote with their money. During real times of extreme fear, there is a genuine reluctance (for most every investor and most all funds) to buy.  
  
Positioning does not reflect extreme fear, in my view (although it is much cleaner now). Starting with households - Retail traders net bought equities 28 days in a row from 1/10 to 2/20.

What I have not seen to go along with the falling GDP estimate, though, is negative earnings revisions. It is rare to get a deep market correction/Bear Market without an economic downturn and earnings deterioration. I am not seeing future earnings projections deteriorate (and I have gone through 300+ earnings). On the whole, we are seeing numbers revised up more than down (both for next quarter and the FY).  
  
Overall, while I see similarities to the 2022 market top, particularly in the price action, the Macro environment is still a much different picture now. This is not to say we do not have some concerns now with tariffs and figuring out how cutting government spending/jobs will affect GDP. However, I am not viewing these as major bearish catalysts (i.e. Bear Market catalysts).   
  
For me, I do not think the current “growth scare” has played a major role in this selloff (yet). I view this mini correction in the SPX as a straight positioning unwind with funds selling heavily and retail turning to sellers this week. Hedge funds took down leverage and consensus long trades got washed out.   
  
Positioning is much cleaner now, as long short HF gross exposure has gone from a historically high 94th percentile on 1/28/25 to current 69th percentile after the HEAVY selling we saw in February.

Goldman’s US Equity Sentiment Indicator (which is actually a positioning indicator), combines 9 measures of positioning across institutional, retail, and foreign investors. We saw it tick to all time highs in January and is now \*a month later) no longer in stretched territory.

Notice how big the overall positioning unwind has been - we have gone from ATH positioning to just above neutral in a month (hence the name of this report, “The Big Unwind”).

With that said, I think we could still have a couple more innings of this unwind (especially with the “growth scare” headlines just now starting to dominate the news cycle following the revised GDP est).

My base case remains a -10% correction in the SPX. In general, I think there is a lack of demand to take us back higher. We are seeing the lack of demand with Hedge Funds de-risking heavily, while retail buying has become exhausted. We also have tax day coming up (possible retail outflows) and the first half of March tends to be choppy at best.

With these more bearish dynamics at play, I believe caution and patience remain warranted here.

# Inflation versus Unemployment Research Report

The below report will be focused on the Macro landscape.  In my view, there is overwhelming evidence to suggest that the S&P 500 has not yet bottomed for this cycle.  Most notably, I will be covering the relationship between inflation and unemployment throughout history.

Including 2022, we have now had 5 Inflation Spikes above 8% CPI since 1948.  Each of the previous 4 instances required a recession AND a spike in the unemployment rate above 6% to quell inflation.

The current unemployment rate is 3.6% after a strong [jobs report on Friday.](https://www.cnbc.com/2022/07/08/jobs-report-june-2022-.html)  In my view, we will need to see a spike in the unemployment rate above 6% for inflation to meaningfully deteriorate, and for the Fed to ease off the gas pedal.  History fully supports this thesis, as I will cover in detail below.

Let’s take a deeper look into each “Inflation Spike” the US has experienced since 1948. Follow along in the chart below:

**1948 Inflation Spike**

* 4/48: Inflation spikes to 8.3%. At the same time, the unemployment rate is 3.9%.
* 7/48: Inflation peaks at 9.8% in July 1948, with the unemployment rate at 3.6%.
* 9/48: Recession officially begins
* 8/49: Inflation bottoms at -2.9%, with the unemployment rate at 6.8%.
* 10/49: Unemployment rate spikes to a high of 7.9%.  Inflation is now -2.6%.
* 10/49: Recession officially ends
* **103% increase in Unemployment Rate from the first month inflation spiked above 8% to peak Unemployment.**
* **Increase in Unemployment from Inflation Peak to Trough: 89%**

**Research Observations**

Since 1948, we have had 4 inflation spikes above 8% CPI.

* All 4 were accompanied by a Recession within 2 years of said spike above 8%.
* All 4 were accompanied by a spike in the Unemployment Rate above 6%.
* On average, we saw a 93% increase in the Unemployment Rate from the first month inflation spiked above 8% to the cycle peak in unemployment.
* On average, we saw a 57% increase in the unemployment rate from the time inflation peaked to the time inflation bottomed.

If 2022 unemployment trends follow the average of the previous 4 major inflation spikes (>8% CPI)…

* Unemployment should spike to a peak of 6-8%.
* If inflation is currently peaking, the Unemployment Rate should be 5-6% when inflation bottoms.

**Relationship Between Inflation and Unemployment**

The relationship between inflation and unemployment exists, especially in periods of high inflation (>8% CPI), mainly because of the Federal Reserve.  The impact that monetary policy has on the economy and the equity market cannot be understated.  Tighter monetary policy leads to tighter financial conditions, creating a ripple effect on the entire economy, including unemployment.  In our current environment, financial conditions have tightened at a pace not seen in the last decade +.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!wC-X!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc1fce659-f001-4ebb-af64-9d14bc84622a_357x314.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!wC-X!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc1fce659-f001-4ebb-af64-9d14bc84622a_357x314.png)

Going even further back in history to 1967, we can see that there has been no Federal Reserve tightening cycle **this** aggressive that did **NOT** end in recession.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!RwjR!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F844c85cb-62b3-48a3-aa45-734ddec5c1fc_1296x1042.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!RwjR!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F844c85cb-62b3-48a3-aa45-734ddec5c1fc_1296x1042.png)

It is important to note that monetary policy works with a lag, which is known as the “response lag”.  Response lag is the time it takes for new monetary policy to affect the economy once it has been implemented.  Studies show the typical response lag is 6-15 months, meaning 2022’s rate hikes should most impact the economic data in late ‘22/2023.

With [Friday’s employment numbers](https://www.cnbc.com/2022/07/08/jobs-report-june-2022-.html) still very strong, it is clear we are not seeing the impact of tighter monetary policy on employment numbers just yet.  With that said, layoffs appear to be creeping up, and we have already started to hear from certain major companies about hiring freezes and planned layoffs (Tesla, Meta, Netflix, Oracle, etc.).

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!a15r!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9294a399-15c6-4cd7-9760-b0640242d26b_944x786.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!a15r!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9294a399-15c6-4cd7-9760-b0640242d26b_944x786.png)

The negative in Friday’s jobs report is that it gives the Fed the green light to continue hiking aggressively, at least until we get that spike in the unemployment rate I discussed above. The Fed is unlikely to reverse policy before such a spike, as consumer sentiment has plummeted to its [lowest levels ever](https://www.morningstar.com/news/dow-jones/202206105747/us-consumer-sentiment-fell-in-june-to-its-lowest-level-ever-university-of-michigan).

In my view, employment data will continue to worsen over the next year.  So, what does that mean for stocks?  I attempt to answer that question below.

**Relationship between Unemployment Rate and Stock Performance**

Since 1948, the S&P 500 has performed significantly better during the 12 months following a high unemployment rate than versus a low unemployment rate.

As you can see, 12 month returns with an unemployment rate of <4% have greatly underperformed the average 12 month stock market return.  Additionally, S&P 500 forward 1-year returns are positive only 64% of the time with an unemployment rate <4%, versus an 89% positive rate with unemployment between 5.9%-7.2%.

Based off this data, one can conclude that if you are expecting a surge in unemployment, it is best to wait until after we get that spike to buy the index. The data tells us that the S&P 500 is unlikely to bottom before there is a surge in unemployment.

This is also consistent with the average historical Bear Market returns, which shows that the S&P 500 tends to follow the economic cycle during recessions.

**Summary Points**

* The S&P 500 is unlikely to bottom until we see a meaningful spike in the unemployment rate (6-8%). This is part of the process of solving inflation, which has been observed throughout history.
* Historically speaking, buying the index after a significant spike in unemployment has provided the best returns.
* Big picture, my Base Case remains another wave down in the S&P 500 to the $2900-$3300 region before seeing a bottom for this cycle. As discussed in [“Light at the End of the Tunnel”](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/light-at-the-end-of-the-tunnel) , certain individual stocks have likely already started the bottoming process.

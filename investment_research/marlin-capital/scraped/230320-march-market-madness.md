# March Market Madness 

The resilience of this market continues to surprise many.

Last week, we saw another rally in equities following the Fed’s bailout of Silicon Valley Bank.  A blend of “bank runs”, hyperinflation, and recessionary fears have not been enough to take the market down.

Following last weekend’s Bank Crisis news, flows to money market funds reached their highest weekly inflows ($121B) since the peak of the March 2020 Covid Crash.  This is the 2nd largest weekly inflow to MM funds since 1990.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!m1X3!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1d3b53e3-178f-4543-a163-49073e4a5cda_961x613.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!m1X3!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1d3b53e3-178f-4543-a163-49073e4a5cda_961x613.png)

Total assets currently allocated to Money Market Funds has surged to an all-time high.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!tbFz!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F52fc3f33-2964-430c-a3df-7d060372d130_603x435.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!tbFz!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F52fc3f33-2964-430c-a3df-7d060372d130_603x435.png)

Investors and traders are not only fleeing to cash, they are actively betting on market downside via put options.  In fact, 3 out of the top 10 largest put option days in history happened in the last week.

On Friday (3/10), 37.4M put option contracts traded.  This was the largest number of daily puts traded in history.  On Monday (3/13), another 27.3M put options traded, the 8th largest put option volumes in history.  In other words, 64.3M put option contracts traded on Friday (3/10) and Monday (3/13), making it the largest 2 day period (by far) in history.

In my view, the move higher in the equity market last week has much to do with this bearish positioning.  Interestingly, the Information Technology sector is the sector with the least amount of fund exposure (by far), as per the BofA chart below.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!2Lyb!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F28434ad6-b0db-4527-8f95-ba95e8443f7b_591x401.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!2Lyb!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F28434ad6-b0db-4527-8f95-ba95e8443f7b_591x401.png)

It should come as no surprise, then, that the tech heavy Nasdaq has actually outperformed Small Caps for 11 consecutive days.  This Bear market has a way of fooling everyone, after all.

Over the past 10 sessions, the GS Basket of Mega Cap Tech stocks is up +6.3%, while the RTY is down -10.5%.  This 16.8% performance spread is the 2nd highest of all time.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!KRep!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4bd4ee44-31ce-427c-988e-f733f6be778a_809x485.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!KRep!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4bd4ee44-31ce-427c-988e-f733f6be778a_809x485.png)

In other words, Mega Cap Tech is holding up the market while breadth deteriorates.  After seeing NYSE New Highs outpace New Lows for all of 2023, we are now starting to see the opposite occur here in March.  This should serve as a warning sign to all investors.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!zDJH!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7f2f9002-9027-4893-8a4e-97e045634790_893x445.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!zDJH!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7f2f9002-9027-4893-8a4e-97e045634790_893x445.png)

One question on everyone's mind is whether or not we will continue to see a range-bound market.  After all, the S&P 500 is currently trading at the exact same price that it was in May of 2022 (10+ months ago).

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!H7bu!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbc05d171-c36d-4e6c-a210-c1e56377d88e_1103x645.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!H7bu!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbc05d171-c36d-4e6c-a210-c1e56377d88e_1103x645.png)

With traders continually focused on both tails and extremes in this environment, a range-bound market just might be the “max pain” scenario to frustrate both sides.  From a “Bank Run Armageddon is here”, to “the Fed has started the printing press and new bull market is here”, traders continue to position themselves on both extremes.

Every time we have seen the market indices start to break down, puts and shorts spike (mentioned above), causing a subsequent squeeze.  This is why it may ultimately be necessary to continue to frustrate both sides before the market can have a sustained move in one direction.

The wildcard here is that things are indeed breaking, as I discussed [last week](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/things-are-breaking).  In the aftermath of the SVB and SBNY bank failures, we witnessed the largest 3-day drop in US Treasury 2 YR yields since Black Monday, 1987.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!l_Vx!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5203ad6c-5e24-4458-a02d-de595c66bd05_595x435.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!l_Vx!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5203ad6c-5e24-4458-a02d-de595c66bd05_595x435.png)

In my view, this is the Fixed Income market’s way of telling the Fed to stop hiking because we are already facing a serious economic downturn in the near future.

Meanwhile, the US 2/10 YR yield curve saw its largest 3-day steepening since 1982.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!rYgy!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F58296904-85e1-489c-aa50-1789f25d9f24_587x416.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!rYgy!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F58296904-85e1-489c-aa50-1789f25d9f24_587x416.png)

Historically, the yield curve has always steepened into economic recessions.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Nmmk!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbaa714d3-cbab-4da5-9e03-0dcce61a26ae_611x427.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Nmmk!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbaa714d3-cbab-4da5-9e03-0dcce61a26ae_611x427.png)

I touched on this as it relates to the stock market in “[Bear Market Bottom Checklist](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/bear-market-bottom-checklist)”.  In the report, I wrote:

> “In each of the last 6 Bear Markets dating back to 1982, the 2/10 yield curve had steepened before the market bottomed.  If the yield curve was inverted, as it is currently, it un-inverted prior to the market bottoming.”

The types of moves mentioned above highlight the turmoil and volatility we are seeing across various asset classes right now.

Below is a plot of the 5-day rate of change of US interest rate implied volatility.  This is the largest 5-day move since at least 2006, and yet, stocks seem largely unintimidated by it.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!KTvF!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F497e615f-22a1-41b3-a036-11e39a421ce0_774x463.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!KTvF!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F497e615f-22a1-41b3-a036-11e39a421ce0_774x463.png)

As we head into this week, the big question is “What will the Fed do?”  There is widespread debate over whether we will see a 25 BP hike or a pause from the committee.  We will find out the Fed’s decision when the March FOMC meeting concludes this Wednesday (3/22).

As the below chart overlay from the 1970s suggests, it is likely to have a significant impact on the equity market.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!B2Ru!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff08e073c-0687-4c6b-9a9d-f8809516f16f_599x403.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!B2Ru!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff08e073c-0687-4c6b-9a9d-f8809516f16f_599x403.png)

As you can see above, equities largely followed the path of the Fed throughout the late 19060s and 1970s.  Fed easing allowed markets to rally temporarily, only to be undone by future tightening. In the 1970s, a second round of Fed tightening was needed to curb inflation.

This week’s decision is a critical one by the Fed, both for the economy and the stock market.  Stay tuned for another report coming this week following the Fed’s decision.

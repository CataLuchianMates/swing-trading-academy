# The Pendulum Swings Both Ways

Throughout this Bear Market (and before it began), I have made parallels to historical Bear Markets.  First, there was the comparison of ARKK to the Nasdaq during Dot Com Bubble and Bust, which has now fully played out.

I believe it is important to study and learn from history, because history rhymes. I do not view the similarities in chart patterns as mere coincidence, as I have stressed from the very beginning. Quite simply, this is what a Bear Market looks like when it is in the process of playing out. Nothing that has happened in the equity market this year is a new or unusual event that has not already occurred throughout history.

Further, the stock market, economy, credit, etc. move in cycles.  For anyone looking to learn more about cycles, I highly recommend the book “Mastering the Market Cycle” by legendary investor Howard Marks.

In the book, Marks describes the stock market as a pendulum that swings (often violently) between extreme greed and extreme fear:

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Z9JQ!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fedd8091f-7e3b-49d4-ba8e-fa15441953b5_917x215.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Z9JQ!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fedd8091f-7e3b-49d4-ba8e-fa15441953b5_917x215.png)

During bull markets, stocks overshoot to the upside as greed and optimism take over.  During bear markets, stocks overshoot to the downside as fear and pessimism take over.

So, the big question is: **Where are we on the pendulum now?** We know that stocks violently overshot to the upside in early ‘21 during a [massive QE induced Bubble that I described in “We’re Not in Kansas Anymore](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/were-not-in-kansas-anymore)”.  The froth from that Bubble has undoubtedly been cleaned, with the S&P 500 down over 23% YTD.  But, have we really overshot to the downside? Has the pendulum already swung to maximum fear and pain?

I prefer to take a data based approach in answering that question.  Morgan Stanley’s Mike Wilson made an excellent observation in his [Monday morning note](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/monday-morning-notes-1017).  Wilson argued that he expects the stock indices to bounce over the next couple weeks, as the S&P 500 has pulled back to its 200 week moving average, an important long term trend line.  Historically, the indices have only broken the 200 week moving average during major recessionary bear markets.  Well, we are in one (a major recessionary bear market), and I fully expect the 200 week EMA level to break once this latest bounce concludes.

If we assume the pendulum’s midpoint is the 200 week moving average (I prefer to use EMA – exponential moving average), we can measure the amount at which different historical bull and bear markets “overshot” the midpoint.  Using the 200 week moving average as our long term mid point, I went back and studied each major historical recessionary bear market to see what I might learn.

**At the peak of the Covid Bubble, the S&P 500 was 40% extended above its 200 week EMA.  This makes it the 3rd largest stock market bubble in the last 100 years, trailing only the Roaring 20’s and Dot Com bubble manias.**  These 2 bubbles were followed by wicked Bear Markets – The Great Crash of 1929 and the Dot Com bust.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!-jtP!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbadc0eea-3961-47a3-bf72-51e06656b9a0_1287x303.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!-jtP!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbadc0eea-3961-47a3-bf72-51e06656b9a0_1287x303.png)

In “Mastering the Market Cycle”, Howard Marks theorizes that “the greater the excess on the upside, the greater the excess on the downside”.  In other words - the bigger the bubble, the bigger the bust.  The bigger the party, the bigger the hangover.  The greater the pendulum swung to the upside, the greater it will swing to the downside.

Looking at the greatest 200-week EMA upside overshoots in history, I concluded that there is typically a substantial overshoot to the downside when the pendulum swings the other way.  The median downside overshoot in my study was 35%.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!W4B4!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc541cebd-14a1-46b3-972b-a06086a21c7d_1287x303.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!W4B4!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc541cebd-14a1-46b3-972b-a06086a21c7d_1287x303.png)

NOTE: Each of the bear markets listed above were associated with a recession in the US economy.  When there is no recession, the stock market can recover quickly, and typically does not spend much time (if any) below the 200 week EMA.  This is also confirmed by historical analysis.  Therefore, I believe it is more important to study recessionary bear markets, being that my base case is a recession in 2023.

In “[The Worst Third](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/the-worst-third)”, I concluded that stock market declines typically accelerate towards the end of the Bear Market.  In others words, Bear Markets are back end loaded, saving the very worst for last.

But, what about during recessions?  The market is forward looking, so at what point during the recession do stocks typically bottom?  The answer is typically about halfway through.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!JUj8!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8e5a74d8-31cb-49bb-852a-4413956c47b6_1284x1125.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!JUj8!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8e5a74d8-31cb-49bb-852a-4413956c47b6_1284x1125.png)

During my analysis, I concluded that declines in the equity market typically accelerate as the economy begins to enter into a recession.  During the 3 largest bubble busts (described above – 1929, 2000, 1937), the S&P 500 bottomed when the recession was about 3/4ths of the way to the finish line.

As you can see above, stocks bottomed deeper into the recession during the worst Recessionary Bear Markets in history (1929, 1937, 2007, 2000, 1973).

So, where are we on the pendulum now? In my view, the S&P 500 violently overshot to the upside, and spent the last ~10 months retracing back to the midpoint.  Now comes the overshoot to the downside, which has not even begun yet.

In the 2000 Bear Market, the S&P 500 spent 124 weeks below its 200wk EMA. In the 2008 Bear Market, the S&P 500 spent 117 weeks below its 200wk EMA.

In fact, the March 2020 Covid Crash was the only Bear Market in my above study with a quick recovery. **In all 5 of the other Recessionary Bear Markets covered, the S&P 500 spent over 2 years below its 200wk EMA (in each instance)**.

The S&P 500’s current 200 week EMA is $3,682.  The median downside overshoot in my study was 35%, which would suggest a bottom near the $2400 region.  Below is a matrix of potential scenarios.  Based off my study, I highlighted the most likely scenarios as a 25-40% downside overshoot of the 200-week EMA.

Summary

* The market overshoots to the upside in Bull Markets
* The market overshoots to the downside in Bear Markets
* We have not overshot to the downside yet. We have simply come back down to reality (from the 3rd largest stock bubble in the last 100 years).
* History suggests the overshoot to the downside will extend 20-40% below the 200wk EMA ($3682), indicating a potential bottoming range of $2209-$2946.

# Things are Breaking

The first major casualties of the Federal Reserve’s fastest tightening cycle in 4+ decades are beginning to surface.  To put this current rate hiking cycle in historical perspective, take a look at the below chart.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!jKmH!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9712e803-0641-4187-bcc6-7189ee06f2e9_644x563.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!jKmH!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9712e803-0641-4187-bcc6-7189ee06f2e9_644x563.png)

No one should be doubting the effects of tighter monetary policy.  Throughout history, the Fed always breaks something after hiking interest rates.  Now, things are starting to break.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!rCp9!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2377f5bd-84a6-48ff-998e-94c1db92ab1b_1095x681.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!rCp9!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2377f5bd-84a6-48ff-998e-94c1db92ab1b_1095x681.png)

Over the past few days, we learned about the [collapse](https://www.cnbc.com/2023/03/12/regulators-unveil-plan-to-stem-damage-from-svb-collapse.html) of Silicon Valley Bank (SVB) and Signature Bank (SBNY).  Both banks were shut down by regulators this weekend.

Silicon Valley Bank has been a major player in the tech industry for years, working with many of the biggest and newest names in the industry.  SVB was the 16th largest bank in the US before collapsing, and the 2nd largest bank failure ever.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!aAIe!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F50075c4b-2c47-4e6e-b279-7e7b814183e5_640x577.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!aAIe!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F50075c4b-2c47-4e6e-b279-7e7b814183e5_640x577.png)

On Sunday, it [was reported](https://twitter.com/NickTimiraos/status/1635044036041121792?s=20) that regulators will backstop all depositors at SVB, but shareholders and unsecured bondholders will not be bailed out.  All depositors at Silicon Valley Bank will have access to their money on Monday.  The Federal Reserve Boards also released the below statement:

> "The Federal Reserve is prepared to address any liquidity pressures that may arise. The financing will be made available through the creation of a new Bank Term Funding Program (BTFP), offering loans of up to one year in length to banks..."

This is a strong regulatory response, as it will restore help to restore some confidence in regional banks and prevent a contagion of more bank runs from spreading.  The Federal Reserve is prepared to address any liquidity pressures that may arise.

The broader implications of these events remain the same to me - the effects of tighter monetary policy are beginning to be felt in the US economy.

In the case of Silicon Valley Bank, the bank held $120B worth of interest rate sensitive securities without hedging any of their interest rate risk.  The bank’s failure is mainly a result of poor risk management practices.

The collapses of Silicon Valley Bank, SBNY, and Silvergate Capital should be stark reminders of what happens in periods of tight monetary policy.  Not all companies are built to survive the full business cycle.  Many companies that were born of easy monetary policy and 0 interest rates will struggle mightily in the current environment.

The period of 0 interest rates and QE that began in March 2020 helped to fuel a massive surge in corporate borrowing and investment. Many companies took full advantage of the cheap credit cycle to fund their growth initiatives, and some were able to grow rapidly as a result.

However, the downside of this environment was that many companies were completely reliant on debt financing and 0 interest rates.  With interest rates now high (4.57%), the cost of servicing their debt is high, and it is much harder for them to continue operating at a loss.

Beyond the impact to individual companies, the greater US economy is not built to withstand such aggressive tightening.  In my research of all economic cycles dating back to 1950, each time the Federal Funds Rate has risen by 455+ bps (as it has currently), a spike in unemployment and recession ensued.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!pV2y!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3eb88359-77ce-41e1-ad06-1fa9e3dc4a82_1242x1762.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!pV2y!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3eb88359-77ce-41e1-ad06-1fa9e3dc4a82_1242x1762.png)

We are now seeing the effects of higher interest rates in the banking sector, specifically among regional banks.  This type of stress in the banking sector is a major sign that the US economy beginning to deteriorate under the pressure of tighter monetary policy.

Ordinarily, one would expect to see the Fed begin cutting interest rates at this time.  However, with the Fed bootstrapped by high inflation, they are still expected to hike 25 bps hike in March (95% probability).

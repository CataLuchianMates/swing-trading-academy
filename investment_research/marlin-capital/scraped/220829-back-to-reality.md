# Back to Reality

The FCI easing and positioning based (short covering) rally of the last month is over.  September is likely to be a very volatile month for stocks.

The market began to price in a soft landing following the July FOMC meeting, but that is unlikely to happen.  Fed Chair Powell told investors as much at Jackson Hole, saying that Americans should brace for some economic pain.

> "Bringing down inflation will also bring some pain to households and businesses. These are the unfortunate costs of reducing inflation"

Fed Chair Powell emphasized the committee’s commitment to bringing down inflation, and even referenced the failures of Federal Reserve in the 1970s due to premature easing (something I have talked about in my [past 2 reports](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/late-summer)).

Let’s take a step back and identify the biggest problem facing the US economy today.  In simplest terms, the issue is:

**Too much demand for too little supply**

As I’ve highlighted throughout my reports this year, the Fed does not have the tools to fix the supply problem.  The Fed can only crush demand, which will bring balance to the equation and help to restore price stability.  The Fed is attempting to crush demand by decreasing household spending power, which will be achieved in 3 main ways:

**1.    By Forcing High Unemployment**

I have discussed this in detail (with historical case studies) [in the article linked here.](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/inflation-versus-unemployment-research)  Labor market weakness will be required for achieving price stability, and Fed Chair Powell has emphasized as much.  As long as unemployment remains near historic lows and wage gains continue at a breakneck pace, demand will continue to be overly robust.  In my view, the strength of the labor market is not a positive for equities because it gives the Fed the green light to continue tightening.  Stocks will remain vulnerable until unemployment spikes, as this will be a necessary ingredient in the process.  History shows that the market is unlikely to bottom much before that happens.

In both the 2000 and 2008 Bear Markets, the S&P 500 bottomed near the point in which jobless claims peaked.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!EGkS!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fedc0db7b-7131-4ac2-bcdb-31d1b55f7def_578x314.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!EGkS!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fedc0db7b-7131-4ac2-bcdb-31d1b55f7def_578x314.png)

Presently, initial jobless claims remain near 10 year lows.  In my view, a peak in jobless claims is likely at least 8-12 months away.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Lew2!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1e03c1dd-ff3c-479b-9cce-e2e632786641_779x325.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Lew2!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1e03c1dd-ff3c-479b-9cce-e2e632786641_779x325.png)

 The previous 3 extended Recessionary Bear Markets (1990, 2000, and 2008) saw an  **acceleration** of selling once the Unemployment Rate spiked above 5%.  In all three cases, anticipating equities would bottom before the labor market deteriorated proved to be a costly error.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!kN-k!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7cb0b937-ee68-4c5c-91f2-dfa49778ff6b_471x293.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!kN-k!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7cb0b937-ee68-4c5c-91f2-dfa49778ff6b_471x293.png)

Presently, the Unemployment Rate sits at 3.5%, near 50 year lows.   History tells us that markets don’t typically bottom until jobless claims spike substantially/the labor market deteriorates.

**2.    By Popping The Housing Market “Bubble”**   
  
Cracks in the US Housing Market are already starting to show.  US existing home sales have declined drastically the last couple months on a YoY basis.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Aj-c!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6efc22de-f19b-48e7-be57-fbb49f02f324_497x376.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Aj-c!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6efc22de-f19b-48e7-be57-fbb49f02f324_497x376.png)

It is certainly not due to a lack of supply, as there is currently a large glut of unsold homes.  The inventory of new homes for sale has risen to its highest level since April 2008.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!woVj!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F606b90d9-130f-4456-af60-be9263ea92e5_835x651.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!woVj!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F606b90d9-130f-4456-af60-be9263ea92e5_835x651.png)

Currently, there are roughly 11 months worth of excess supply under the current rate of sales.  Each of the previous 4 spikes above “10 Months Supply” ended with a recession.

The data points above are evidence that the Fed’s tools are beginning to make an impact in the housing market.  Still, many housing markets remain elevated and could soon fall 20%+, according to Moody’s Analytics.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!IwIZ!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F92e4347a-1483-442f-a8d3-8a6325760c8f_1281x1025.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!IwIZ!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F92e4347a-1483-442f-a8d3-8a6325760c8f_1281x1025.png)

The research firm highlighted 183 markets that could fall 20%+. In any case, there are a concerning number of parallels to 2008 any time you view a chart of the US housing market.  The chart below shows the 2 Year Change of US New Single-Family Home Sales.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!JXGt!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F188e8efc-3497-48e3-984f-e78708269eb6_792x447.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!JXGt!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F188e8efc-3497-48e3-984f-e78708269eb6_792x447.png)

We are currently in the midst of the biggest 2 Year decline since 2008.

**3.    By Popping The Stock Market “Bubble”**

If the 2 factors I mentioned above were not enough in the way of obstacles for the stock market to overcome, this is surely one.  Last year, households owned over 60% of US stocks and the equity rally was attributable to a large increase in consumer purchasing power.  This is a lesser factor than the 2 mentioned above (because the stock market is a function of everything mentioned and more), but it bears mentioning because a further stock market decline is beneficial for the Federal Reserve.

---

The factors mentioned above will lead to lower economic growth and future earnings.  Speaking of forward earnings, estimates have hardly begun to decline.  The current consensus for 2023 S&P 500 earnings is $244, which is roughly 3% off the peak estimate of $252.  The average recession sees a -15% forward EPS contraction, which suggests that 2023 earnings could come all the way down to $210.

The forward leading indicators for future earnings are pointing to additional cuts in the near future.  First off, earnings revision breadth is deeply negative, indicating that more and more companies are beginning to fall victim to the Macroeconomic slowdown.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!ekkV!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd87a5539-fb33-469f-a64b-a2ef1dd1e228_641x481.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!ekkV!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd87a5539-fb33-469f-a64b-a2ef1dd1e228_641x481.png)

Additionally, banks have tightened their lending standards, which bodes poorly for future S&P 500 earnings.

A net 14.3% of US banks have already tightened their lending standards.  The last 3 instances in which we have observed such a high level are 2000, 2007, and 2020.  Each case preceded a steep earnings recession.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!PERW!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc63e4271-3bc4-4af4-b5f3-75fdad0a81c0_1200x675.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!PERW!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc63e4271-3bc4-4af4-b5f3-75fdad0a81c0_1200x675.png)

While future earnings estimates are at risk, valuations are likely to compress further.  After the recent rally in stocks, the S&P 500 now trades at 18x NTM earnings.  This is still a significant premium to the historical valuation the S&P 500 has traded at during periods of high inflation.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!il8K!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F042d7156-b815-4219-9265-e1a8399d32f4_727x416.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!il8K!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F042d7156-b815-4219-9265-e1a8399d32f4_727x416.png)

**Warnings From The Fixed Income Market**

A week ago, [I told Marlin Capital readers](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/late-summer) to watch the US 2 YR Treasury Note as it began climbing back to new highs.  [I wrote](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/late-summer) “a breakout to new highs in the 2 YR (>3.46%) is likely to resolve itself with new lows in the S&P 500.”  Last week, the US 2 YR followed through to the upside and hit its highest level since (you guessed it) 2008.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!AfW-!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2fdbaef3-3100-4c9f-9260-46abc6b7fd6a_720x408.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!AfW-!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2fdbaef3-3100-4c9f-9260-46abc6b7fd6a_720x408.png)

Moreover, we are witnessing the deepest 2/10 yield curve inversion in over 20 years (deeper than 2008).  An inverted yield curve has historically led to some type of “break” in the economic system.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!bW_S!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2b30d3fb-16c6-4fa2-ae15-7d48181dd408_1159x429.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!bW_S!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2b30d3fb-16c6-4fa2-ae15-7d48181dd408_1159x429.png)

An inverted yield curve is the most accurate predictor of a recession dating back to 1960.  This is the Fixed Income Market’s way of saying “Danger Ahead”.  Typically, the Fed would begin to ease with such a deep yield curve inversion, but they do not have such a luxury with CPI still at 8.5%.

**Summary**

* For the last month, investors called Powell’s bluff, only he wasn’t bluffing.  There will be a lot of economic pain before this episode is fully resolved.
* Such restrictive policy in an already deteriorating economy is a recipe for a steep recession, not a mild recession.
* September is likely to be a volatile month for stocks.
* My base case for a bottom in the S&P 500 remains the $2900-$3300 region.

# Framing The Debate For 2024

2023 was a trip.  The imminent recession most of us predicted did not materialize and instead, inflation eased, growth is fine, volatility got smoked, and equities put up double-digit gains.

The NDX finished 2023 up +54%, its best year since 1999.

The explosive rally was driven by a multitude of factors, but most notably:

1. An extreme positioning swing as investors ‘re-risked’ back into Tech after a difficult 2022 (recall that investors entered the year carrying their lowest exposure to Tech in years).
2. A positive earnings revision inflection (boosted by cost cutting efforts),
3. A near perfect economic backdrop for quality, secular growth (falling inflation + mixed economic growth trends + more resilient consumer than expected)
4. AI Mania, led by the debut of OpenAI’s ChatGPT

Underneath the surface of this banner year for Tech, the Big 7 (Magnificent 7) finished the year up +96% vs. the Russell 2000 Index of Small Caps only up +28% on the year, highlighting how important it was to get the big names right this past year.

As we close the books on 2023 and look ahead to 2024, I think it is crucial to zoom out and appreciate how much the starting point matters.  More specifically for Tech, the starting point into 2024 (as compared to the starting point for 2023) would be characterized by the following…

The NDX is currently near ATHs (vs NDX sitting at -35% off highs entering ’23)…The market is pricing in 6 rate cuts in 2024 (vs the market having 2 on the board to start ’23)…Investor Bull/Bear sentiment is at 7 year highs (vs sentiment near multi-year lows going into ’23)…The NDX now trades at ~30x P/E (vs NDX trading at ~22.6x P/E entering 2023, which was 10% below its 7yr avg)…and the Big/Mag 7 added $5T+ of mkt cap in 2023 (this is larger than the current nominal GDP of every country not named US or China).

Given all that, what lies ahead for us in 2024?

Let’s start with the good news.  After a great year for the S&P 500 like we saw 2023, stocks historically continue to gain the next year.  A >20% yearly gain suggests more green ahead the next year, but a more normal return profile for 2024.  The year after >20% gains, the SPX has been up 80% of the time since 1950, with an average return of 10%.  However, only once did the SPX gain more the next year (1997), meaning a year of >25%+ is unlikely in 2024.  Only 4 times (out of 20) did the SPX gain >20% the next year.

**Framing the Debate for 2024 –**

Fed Rate Cuts Ahead: Last December, the consensus view was for a recession in 2023.  However, the economy proved to be resilient in the face of higher interest rates.  This year, the consensus is a bit less uniform, but favors a dovish Fed projected to cut rates 6 times.  The magnitude of rate cuts, and more importantly, the reasoning for rate cuts, yield 2 diverging narratives for equities this year.

**Soft Landing Continuation (The Bull Case) –** This is the consensus narrative for the economy in 2024, with 66% of Institutional Investors surveyed by BofA expecting a soft landing this year.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!aEf0!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F113c1722-cf7d-4402-8530-bbcf46957cc7_1190x658.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!aEf0!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F113c1722-cf7d-4402-8530-bbcf46957cc7_1190x658.jpeg)

Continued disinflation, growth strong (but not too strong), and the Fed cutting to the tune of 150+ bps.  This narrative has driven stock prices over the last couple of months (since the November 14th CPI report).

**Hard Landing Pivot (The Bear Case) –** The key differentiator in this scenario is economic growth weakening materially as well as the Labor Market deteriorating.  This scenario would potentially trigger more Fed Rate Cuts, in the realm of 300+ bps (depending on the severity of the recession).  From a broad market perspective, I would expect this scenario to send the S&P 500 south of $4000.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!EtXR!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6636f282-bc60-4b66-ae42-5fefb07c29af_1167x971.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!EtXR!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6636f282-bc60-4b66-ae42-5fefb07c29af_1167x971.jpeg)

This is the key debate for 2024, as history shows a wide divergence in stock performance when the Fed begins cutting rates.  Equities generally perform better as interest rates fall…as long as a recession is avoided.  If a recession is not avoided, stocks historically see new lows for the cycle during the economic recession (as I have pointed out in the past, stocks have never bottomed prior to an upcoming recession).

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!3kFr!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F18a0961a-8456-4903-ae55-3dc66921e6b7_1245x759.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!3kFr!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F18a0961a-8456-4903-ae55-3dc66921e6b7_1245x759.jpeg)

The leading economic indicators are currently pointing to a hard landing, as evidenced by the Conference Board’s Leading Economic Index.  In November, the Conference Board’s LEI fell for its 20 consecutive month, its longest streak of consecutive decreases since 2007-08.

**Key Considerations for 2024 –**

The 2024 US election.  This year’s election is a key risk factor for markets.

The table below shows the performance for the S&P 500 (dating back to 1929) for the 4-year election cycle.  You will note that the strongest year tends to be the 3rd year (2023), which played out this time around.  The election year (2024) typically sees middle-of-the-road returns, up +6.2% on average, but is positive 74% of the time.

**Earnings Expectations –** At some point this year, the “Earnings” in P/E (Price/Earnings) will matter again.  Last year was more about a valuation expansion than upwards earnings revisions.  As we are currently starting from a higher valuation in 2024, I believe the earnings part of the equation will be more important this year (this also feeds into the Hard vs Soft Landing debate which will greatly affect corporate earnings).

The table below shows YoY EPS growth expectations for each SPX sector as well as a few thematic baskets.  A few quick thoughts:

* These estimates are very much consistent with the 2024 Soft Landing consensus narrative that has helped propel stocks higher.
* These numbers typically get revised lower as the year proceeds, but even small revisions lower would be enough to fuel the Soft Landing scenario.  Historically, a recession results in 10-30% EPS cuts for the broad market.
* Tech remains relatively expensive but still leads in EPS growth.
* The Magnificent 7’s earnings growth edge is shrinking vs the S&P 500 ex Mag 7 (+19% vs +11%)
* Estimates for economically sensitive groups (Cap Goods, Materials, Energy) match uninspired investor sentiment toward them.  The year-end rally was not about economic growth.

**My Big Picture Views as we head into 2024:**

* Even in a Soft Landing scenario, I believe the return profile for stocks will look more normal this year (<20% index returns).  In a Hard Landing scenario, I believe stocks would likely finish negative in 2024.  The November ’24 Presidential Election is a key factor that will affect this debate.  There is every incentive for the current administration to attempt to delay a recession to the end of the year/2025 (after November ’24) if one cannot be avoided.  With that said, I expect 2024 to be more volatile (a few -10% drawdowns are likely to occur, in my view…we quietly had 2 in 2023).
* I think the 2 dominant themes for 2024 are Rate Cuts and Generative AI.  I suspect market sentiment will over-price and under-price both of them at times, creating a good backdrop for trading (right now, the market is arguably over-pricing both).  I think leaning on Small Caps over Large Caps continues to make sense at this stage, as the relative valuation is near multi-year lows even after the recent rally.

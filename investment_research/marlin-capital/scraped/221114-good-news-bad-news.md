# Good News Bad News

For a market that has desperately been searching for hope, Thursday’s downside surprise in CPI sure delivered plenty of it.  The question that most market participants have been wondering over the past couple months is: “Has inflation finally peaked?”

For a moment, the answer to that question is a tentative yes, which was met by an explosive move higher in stocks.  The October CPI release was softer than expected, with headline CPI coming in at 7.7% YoY versus 7.9% expected.  Markets reacted as only a post-Bubble Bear Market would, as a flood of buyers remain conditioned to pounce on any positive news for stocks.

Thursday had the largest SPX return ever while the VIX was still trading under 25, crushing the old record by 78 bps (March 16th, 2000).  While exhilarating, this is far from healthy market behavior.  As displayed below, the only similar occurrences happened during notable Bear Markets.

A key factor in each of these counter-trend rallies is positioning, as [I have pointed out](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/better-early-than-late) each time they have come about this year.  Last week, we saw the largest momentum unwinds / quant style short covering since the Pfizer vaccine announcement in November 2020 (~4 std dev move in short Momo, best day on record for ARKK).  Goldman Sachs’ Non-Profitable Tech bucket recorded its biggest daily gain ever on Thursday (+15.2%).

Unsurprisingly, the top performers of last week’s post-CPI rally were the biggest YTD losers.  This is logical because Thursday’s CPI number introduced some hope that things are starting to change.  The stocks that have performed the worst during the Macro headwinds of the last year are bouncing the most when there is a data point that delivers hope of these headwinds subsiding.

Notice how the best 1D performers post-CPI have performed YTD in the chart above.  As I mentioned above, positioning is a major factor in these counter-trend rallies, particularly in the momentum and speed of the move.  Short covering rallies are typically quick and violent, and this one is certainly no exception.  Prior to last week’s rally, daily Put/Call Ratio hit its highest level since 2008.

When positioning is so lopsided in 1 direction, a shift in momentum will cause an explosive counter move the other way.  Stocks that have been beaten down all year by shorts are acting like coiled springs now that they have a catalyst for a bounce.

On the Macro side of things, Thursday was the 3rd largest FCI easing day on record.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!kfYj!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2e662fad-fcb9-4a3a-b686-c120fa5c400e_1205x745.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!kfYj!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2e662fad-fcb9-4a3a-b686-c120fa5c400e_1205x745.png)

The only days where financial conditions eased more were:

(1) March 24th, 2020 – the day the Fed announced its crisis policy to combat a total economic shutdown; and

(2) March 2009 - the day the Fed announced its initial QE program in 2009.

Moreover, US 10Y Treasury yields had their largest daily decline since the Fed announced its initial Quantitative Easing Program in 2009.

Big picture, my views and target price (for SPX) have not changed.  While the downside surprise in inflation is a welcome sign, it is not a time to get complacent.  In my view, US companies will certainly not be immune to the effects of monetary tightening, as future earnings estimates are suggesting.  I expect the Fed to maintain higher rates until unemployment spikes significantly, at which time they will be forced to pivot.  In any case, a recession looms in 2023.

Coming out of last week, one could finally argue that the Fed is making progress in their fight against inflation.  On the other hand, one could increasingly worry about the trajectory of nominal GDP growth (with falling inflation) and corporate earnings (this is clearly manifesting itself in negative revisions to 2023 estimates, as I have pointed out [here](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/better-early-than-late)).

The primary detractor of equity performance in 2022 has been multiple compression.  As I have noted in past reports, future earnings estimates have remained steady despite growing risks.  In other words, most equity losses in 2022 are attributable to multiple compression, not earnings compression.  If the inflation narrative is set to improve now, there is growing risk that the earnings story is set to worsen.

So, are we just swapping one challenge for another? The answer to that question, in my view, is yes, although that is not an all-bad scenario for stocks that have been hyperfocused on inflation.

Over the short term, I believe the inflation factor will continue to dominate the narrative, potentially driving stocks higher in the process.  Over the long term, it does not set up as the launch pad for the next long-term bull market.

At the very least, risk assets have cleared a number of meaningful obstacles and now have a clearer path ahead into the December 15th FOMC meeting.  Following Thursday’s CPI release, the futures market is confident that we are heading for a 50bp rate hike.

**Technical Update**

The S&P 500 held support at the critical 200 week EMA level [I have discussed in the past](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/the-pendulum-swings-both-ways). This level has only been broken in recessionary bear markets.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!-Sp1!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0e8a6b7a-3dd8-4605-a3db-930e0bf960cc_1029x651.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!-Sp1!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0e8a6b7a-3dd8-4605-a3db-930e0bf960cc_1029x651.png)

For now, this critical level has held up on its 2nd test. The weekly chart is pointing to the potential of some bullish follow through of last week’s upside move.

On the daily chart, we have cleared the key 21 and 60 EMA levels (short and medium term resistance levels). We are now approaching the 200 day SMA, which is around the spot at which the previous 2 rallies have failed.

We are coming into the week 2% below the 200 Day Moving Average, a resistance level that I expect to be tested before this rally ends.

Key resistance levels to watch ahead include $4000, $4060, and $4080.

After a massive 2 day rally to close last week, some backtesting/consolidation would also be healthy for a further upside move.

Key support levels to watch below include $3900, $3840, and $3800. As long as the $3800 level holds this week, the door is open for this rally to continue. A move back below $3800 and the key short and medium term EMAs (21 and 60) pictured above would likely put an end to the bounce.

## David Marlin Q&A (Comments)

**Q:** Curious about your take on this from @litigious_dulce

"AMBUSHING COREWEAVE IN THE VALLEY OF DEATH

------
“Appear at places to which the enemy must hasten; move swiftly where he does not expect you. Strike him where he is unprepared, appear where you are not expected.”
------

In its most recent earnings presentation, $IREN guided for annual recurring revenue (ARR) of about $250m. Given that the Hoppers make about $28m ARR, IREN must expect the 9,000 Blackwells it purchased for $491m to make about $222m ARR, which is extremely low. 

IREN’s pricing strategy represents a deliberate assault on the economics of its competitors. With multi-year bare-metal rates anchored at approximately $2.40/GPU-hour for B200s (≈$46,000 each), $3.30 for B300s (≈$64,000 each), and $4.00 for GB300s (≈$76,000 each), IREN is offering capacity at a fleet-wide average of approximately $2.82/GPU-hour. This is roughly a 20 percent discount compared to the next reputable cloud service provider on like-for-like, multi-year contracts (meaning most, including $CRWV, charge much more)!

This discount is not arbitrary; it is structurally embedded in IREN’s business model. The company has built 800 MW of HPC-grade data centers, financed initially through its Bitcoin mining operations and now leveraged for higher-value AI workloads. Unlike many of its peers, IREN owns the land, the transmission lines, the substations, and the data centers. That vertical integration eliminates colocation fees, which constitute a significant recurring expense for neocloud competitors. It also ensures operational control, with IREN able to diagnose and address issues at the physical site before they escalate into service disruptions. The outcome is a service that matches or exceeds the quality of peers while structurally delivering a lower unit cost.

The contrast with CRWV is stark. CRWV is currently in a valley-of-death phase typical of capital-intensive businesses: a massive GPU fleet generating massive depreciation charges, substantial colocation and facility fees, escalating fixed obligations, and heavy operating losses. The path to profitability depends on reaching scale and utilization fast enough to cover those burdens. 

At this critical juncture, IREN is moving aggressively to capture hyperscaler demand for bare metal—a segment that has become commoditized. In a commoditized market, service quality is a baseline requirement, but cost is decisive. With pricing in the $2.40–$4.00/GPU-hour range, NVIDIA validation as a preferred partner, and growing recognition that IREN’s offering is as good or better in operational quality, IREN is positioning itself to undercut CRWV’s recovery trajectory.

This is not merely competitive positioning; it is the deliberate acceleration of a race to the bottom in pricing. Because IREN’s integrated structure allows it to survive and even thrive at these levels, the company has effectively forced the market to a clearing price that CRWV cannot sustain. Every client (particularly hyperscalers) that commits to IREN’s lower-cost fleet denies CRWV the revenue streams needed to cover depreciation and colocation fees, extending CRWV’s losses and reducing its ability to achieve operating leverage. In this way, IREN is starving CRWV of the breathing room required to stabilize.

The strategic parallel with Bitcoin mining is notable. IREN has already eclipsed $MARA in market capitalization for the first time, driven by superior execution and a structurally lower cost base in mining operations. The same playbook is now being applied in AI cloud services. In mining, IREN’s advantages were cost of power, efficiency of build, and operational discipline. In cloud, those same competencies have been translated into ownership of critical infrastructure, elimination of third-party rents, and control over power and cooling, all reinforced by NVIDIA’s public validation.

The implication is that IREN is positioned not merely to compete with CRWV but to eclipse it entirely in cloud services, just as it did with MARA in mining. Bare-metal compute has become a commodity, and in commodity markets, structure determines survival. IREN’s structure delivers both cost leadership and operational credibility, while CRWV remains encumbered by third-party fees and capital strain. By forcing the price war now, IREN has ensured that the contest will be fought on terrain where it holds a structural advantage—and where CRWV has little chance of long-term survival.

The valuation gap between IREN and CRWV must close."

# The Week Ahead

US equities sold off last week amidst a challenging Macro backdrop (RTY -3.5%, NDX -2.2%. SPX -1.9%). The US 10 YR yield tacked on another +16 bps to 4.76%, while the market is now only pricing in 33bps of cuts this year (vs 85bps one month ago). Interestingly, the implied probability of a rate hike over the next year has risen to 35%, which is a significant development in recent weeks (and obviously weighing on the market).

It was a week culminated by a mix of firmer growth and stickier inflation (NFP and ISM services), a sell the news reaction to Jensen’s CES keynote, Trump 2.0 uncertainty (tariffs, Tik Tok ban?), and the corporate buyout blackout.

Higher yields continue to weigh on market sentiment as interest rate sensitivity has returned as a key driving force for equity markets (remember 2022?). Friday’s hot NFP report furthered that dynamic as the 10YR yield pushed higher and US stocks sold off in reaction to the stronger than expected jobs report.

This week promises to be a busy one on the Micro and Macro fronts-

Monday - Nothing of note today

Tuesday - NFIB small business optimism and PPI

Wednesday - CPI + Earnings Season kicks off with banks (BLK, BK, WFC, JPM, GS, and C all set to report)

Thursday - Retail sales, import/export prices, homebuilder sentiment, jobless claims

Friday - Industrial production, housing tarts, and building permits

With rates becoming the most important variable for equities in recent weeks, this week’s inflation data becomes that much more important. The main concern for equity market investors is that we will see inflation re-accelerate this year, forcing the Fed to pause (or even raise, 35% probability). This week’s inflation data will either add to or quell those fears (temporarily) and will be be a key driver for short/medium term sentiment (and rates).

Positioning wise - Hedge Funds net sold US equities for a 2nd straight week, driven by short sales. Hedge Funds, pension funds, asset managers, and CTAs have all been net sellers over the past few weeks (since December). The selling pressure began a few days before December’s FOMC meeting and has continued into the new year.

Earnings kick off later this week with Banks. Analysts are forecasting +8% YoY EPS growth for the aggregate S&P 500. Consensus expectations are high relative to recent quarters, so the magnitude of EPS beats may be less than recent quarters.

I will be focused primarily on forward guidance for 2025 and Q1 against a backdrop of slowing nominal GDP growth, higher interest rates, and a strengthening US dollar. I believe the market will also be focused on how companies are adjusting their behavior ahead of the incoming Trump admin, including preparation for potential tariffs. With market concentration at ATHs (market cap of Mag 7 vs rest of market), Mega Caps will obviously be the most important drivers of the market and expectations will continue to be very high.

The investor mood into earnings season is anxious with the market selling off, but the market decline is more Macro/rates driven. A solid earnings season could certainly help sentiment to rebound/take some focus off the Macro. With that said, it is still a trickier setup into earnings for a lot of names (specifically Tech) with valuations elevated versus history. The NDX, specifically, skews more expensive relative to peers (though, as I wrote about in my 2025 preview, much of this is due to Mega Caps).

Active Portfolio -

I head into this week fully cash in my trading account with no plans to do anything here early in the week. As my readers who have been with me for awhile know, there are times when I am very active in my trading and times where I am not. For me, this is very much a time for patience. The very best traders, in my experience, know their strategy and when the general market is conducive to that strategy.

Know your strategy. Know what you’re good at (and what you’re not good at). And know when the market is cooperative with your strategy. If you are swimming against the tide, you will only make things more difficult for yourself.

If you feel the need to always be active/trading no matter the market conditions, this is more of a gambler’s mentality than a sound trading strategy. The gambler will always continue betting as long as the casino is open.

A baseball player does not swing at every pitch. While I ultimately believe this market dip will be bought up, there needs to be a specific setup with defined risk for me to begin to get involved. I am not seeing any potential trades meeting my criteria at this time, and blindly buying the dip with no setup is not part of my trading strategy. So, now is simply a time for patience and observation for me in my trading account. I do believe we will see much better pitches to swing at ahead (as early as later this week, potentially). Until then, I’m more than happy to wait and observe.

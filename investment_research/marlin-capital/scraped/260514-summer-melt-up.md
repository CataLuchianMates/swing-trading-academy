**The Meta Deal in Context**

Today’s earnings call was also the first deep public walk-through of the Meta deal management announced back on 3/16.

The headline number is $27B over 5 years, but the structure is more interesting than the number itself.

✅ $12B = committed dedicated compute capacity for Meta starting early 2027

✅ $15B = additional flexible capacity that NBIS can allocate at their discretion (to Meta or to a higher-paying AI cloud customer)

That $15B optional piece is key. Meta is essentially backstopping the capacity at a guaranteed margin, but NBIS gets to sell that same capacity to the open market if they can get better pricing elsewhere.

And per NBIS management on the call, this contract structure unlocks “billions” in asset-backed financing at attractive rates. This means that NBIS can scale their CapEx aggressively without diluting shareholders with new equity raises (like IREN and some other peers).

**Reader Q&A: On Trimming Big Winners**

I have been getting this question a lot in the Marlin Capital Substack chat lately, so I want to address it in tonight’s report.

The question was: is it shortsighted to lock in some profits on a big gainer up here?

My short answer is no.

There is no one size fits all answer here. Every subscriber is in a different situation. But if you are sitting on big gains and trimming some would give you mental peace, that is a perfectly reasonable thing to do. The mental side of trading is not something to be understated, especially for those running margin or leverage. If you’re constantly worried about a pullback or can’t sleep at night, you need to trim some.

What I personally do not do is exit entire positions in stocks that are still in clean Stage 2 uptrends and leading the market. As long as a name has given me no reason to exit, I let it continue to run.

For those who have been with Marlin Capital awhile, that typically means we end up exiting on the way down (after a stock shows signs of topping behavior).

There are basically 2 options. You are going to either sell too early or sell too late. That is just the nature of this game. There is no such thing as perfect market timing.

Personally, I would rather sell too late than too early. Especially in a market bubble.

Selling too early in a bubble = you might miss another 100% or 200%+ on a name that just keeps running. It is a bubble, after all.

Selling too late = you might give back 20-30% from the top if you have a good sense of overall topping behavior (which, our history at Marlin Capital says we do).

That is the trade-off in my approach. My strategy is to ride the winner until the price action tells me it has likely topped, then exit on the way down. Like William O’Neil says, my goal is not to sell at the very top price. That is impossible to do. My goal is to participate in the majority of the move.

My bottom line is this: trimming big gainers is smart depending on your situation. Exiting entirely/selling everything on the way up is not how I like to handle things for myself.

# Rinse and Repeat

Last week, another upside surprise in [Headline CPI](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/tuesday-market-notes-913) (8.3% vs 8.1% est.) extinguished any hope of an upcoming Dovish pivot from the Federal Reserve.  Accordingly, markets have continued to ratchet up future rate hike expectations, now pricing in a peak Federal Funds Rate of >4.50% in 2023.  This is a significant move higher from 1-2 months ago, where markets had implied the Federal Funds Rate would peak somewhere between 3.3-3.5%.

Financial Conditions have tightened considerably since Tuesday’s CPI report, which will continue to act as a major headwind for equities.  Post Tuesday CPI report, a number of events occurred that should be of interest to all equity market participants (because they spell potential doom for stocks).

1.       Fed Funds Futures projections hit new highs at 4.50%, having added ~55 bps of new hikes since last week’s upside surprise CPI print

2.       US Dollar makes new 20 year highs

3.       US Real Yields make fresh 3.5 year highs

4.       US Treasury Curves further inverted, pushing near “Bear Flattening” cycle lows as the Fixed Income market prices in a “Hard Landing”

With these 4 events unfolding, it is no surprise that the S&P 500 fell 4.8% last week.  Still, the surge higher in real rates points to new lows ahead for the S&P 500.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!konj!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2ad550bc-edfe-4518-8c03-be73e41eab9b_663x501.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!konj!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2ad550bc-edfe-4518-8c03-be73e41eab9b_663x501.png)

The move higher in Real Rates is likely to lead to further valuation compression across the market.  Additionally, the stark increase we are seeing in the cost of borrowing is the Fed’s way of slamming the breaks on the US economy.  This should ultimately result in an economic slowdown and lower demand for goods and services, assisting the Fed in their quest to lower inflation.

So, where does that leave equities?  In my view, such an economic slowdown is not yet priced into the market.  I feel very comfortable in saying this because consensus projections are **still** calling for S&P 500 EPS growth of >7% in 2023.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!F0SR!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa5291c2c-4de4-4a6b-95ae-b279666cbdd8_691x141.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!F0SR!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa5291c2c-4de4-4a6b-95ae-b279666cbdd8_691x141.png)

Given the recent commentary from FDX, DOW, etc., there is reason to believe that downside earnings revisions/warnings are **just beginning**.  According to Goldman, a US Recession would result in around -12% EPS growth in 2023.  History indicates a similar trajectory, with the average recession resulting in around -15% EPS growth.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!7ec7!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F85df3a12-cf3e-42b4-9d55-a9f876f889d4_558x331.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!7ec7!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F85df3a12-cf3e-42b4-9d55-a9f876f889d4_558x331.png)

A -12% earnings recession would result in $200 of earnings for the S&P 500.  Consensus 2023 earnings estimates are currently 18% **above** that number, at $243.  Even a -6% earnings recession in 2023, which could arguably be characterized as a “soft landing”, is far from “priced in” to the market.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!knMT!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1297ffb7-6960-4897-9c66-02a84c982cd0_755x545.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!knMT!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1297ffb7-6960-4897-9c66-02a84c982cd0_755x545.png)

To put it simply, earnings estimates are NOT currently priced for a recession in 2023 (or anything close).  In fact, they are priced for a solid year of growth.  This remains a major risk for equities, as I believe we are likely to see negative earnings growth in 2023 (resulting from an economic slowdown).  In my view, we will see 2023 earnings revised down 10-20% from current estimates before all is said and done.

So, where might that leave equities?  Even in a “soft landing/mild recession” scenario, it leaves equities significantly lower.  A -6% earnings recession could arguably be characterized as a soft landing, as mentioned above.  Yet, even in this scenario, current P/E valuations are elevated versus historical levels, especially for periods with high inflation (as shown below).

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!NVu0!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe6cbcc85-9978-40ef-938f-dcd559e05230_727x416.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!NVu0!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe6cbcc85-9978-40ef-938f-dcd559e05230_727x416.png)

As I have covered in [past reports](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/nothing-is-easy), the previous 4 Bear Markets each bottomed with the S&P 500 trading **below** 14x forward earnings.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!3rMf!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2246382c-d42d-474b-a8fa-22a9b72bce96_891x415.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!3rMf!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2246382c-d42d-474b-a8fa-22a9b72bce96_891x415.png)

A similar trajectory this time around would take the S&P 500 to my low $3k target area (even in the most bullish of earnings projections scenarios).

**No Capitulation Yet**

Although sentiment has remained highly depressed throughout this Bear Market, there are still no signs of major capitulation.  In my “[Bear Market Bottom Checklist](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/bear-market-bottom-checklist)” report, I talked about the fact that there has been no significant spike in the VIX (a precursor for prior market bottoms).  Tuesday was the first time in history the S&P 500 sold off more than 4% with the VIX closing below 30.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!rPn1!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0c92bcb7-3bff-4104-9a60-3075e812779d_699x523.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!rPn1!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0c92bcb7-3bff-4104-9a60-3075e812779d_699x523.png)

Additionally, Tuesday was the 8th strongest retail buying day of the year.  This is not typical action near Bear Market bottoms, and serves as a good illustration of the complacency and lack of fear out there.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!I5fp!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6e9e3551-6a73-4fb8-bd2d-f7b50d0a41c0_933x361.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!I5fp!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6e9e3551-6a73-4fb8-bd2d-f7b50d0a41c0_933x361.png)

Another negative that stocks must contend with is the “Buyback Blackout Period” taking place over the next month.  The Buyback Blackout window began last week with around 50% of S&P 500 companies in their closed window.  This will extend to basically all S&P 500 companies by the end of September, and should result in especially tough sledding for equities (over the next month).

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!jYF1!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ffeb42576-4bc9-465b-aff6-98419ff9a01d_625x286.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!jYF1!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ffeb42576-4bc9-465b-aff6-98419ff9a01d_625x286.png)

For stocks, the “Buyback Blackout” window could not come at a worse time.  It is coming at a time where liquidity is already deteriorating, with the Federal Reserve actively reducing the size of their balance sheet at the fastest pace in its history.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!rz0n!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F226056ab-7a09-46c0-962b-7772326bf967_500x442.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!rz0n!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F226056ab-7a09-46c0-962b-7772326bf967_500x442.png)

It is difficult to quantify the negative effects of QT, but there is little doubt that the policy is a major headwind for stocks.

This week, we will get the Federal Reserve decision on interest rates (on Wednesday) and hear from Fed Chair Powell.  The market is currently pricing in a 79.5 bps hike (so a near certainty of a 75 BP hike).

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!kdIJ!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbc5da3b9-570d-469d-9894-53993cd29ce1_678x113.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!kdIJ!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbc5da3b9-570d-469d-9894-53993cd29ce1_678x113.png)

Although we have seen some start to float around the possibility of a 100 bp hike ([Nomura](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/wednesday-morning-notes-914)), it remains unlikely in my opinion because the Fed does not like to “shock” markets.  With that said, I believe the market will be paying even closer attention to Powell’s commentary on the trajectory future rate hikes.

In any case, I expect there should be plenty of volatility following Wednesday’s FOMC meeting and into the weekend.  I will have a full report for everyone recapping the FOMC and full analysis towards the end of the week.

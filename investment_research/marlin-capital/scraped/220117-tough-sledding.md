Last week, the Nasdaq Composite closed below its 30 week EMA for the first time since March of 2020.  This broke a 93 week long trend that held during the composite’s entire move higher.

On the daily chart, Friday’s weak bounce off the 200-DMA leaves much to be desired.  New lows far outpaced new highs, while the majority of strength we saw came from oil and financials.

This should not be very surprising, as nearly 40% of Nasdaq stocks are down 50% from ATHs.  This is an astounding number when you see the Nasdaq composite is only 8% off its all-time highs.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!OwI9!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3e6d9651-94ea-4054-82ed-4484b75b28ee_634x412.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!OwI9!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3e6d9651-94ea-4054-82ed-4484b75b28ee_634x412.jpeg)

At no other point since 1999 have so many stocks been cut in half while the Nasdaq was so close to highs.  When at least 35% of stocks have been cut in half, the Nasdaq has been down an average of 47% (!!)

So, what does this all mean? In my view, it means that the index is extremely vulnerable to a larger correction.  This is precisely why I have not started to buy the dip, despite ARKK reaching my initial price target of $78.

Speaking of ARKK, the action we have seen over the last year or so continues to draw parallels to the bursting of the Dot Com Bubble.  On a technical basis, the patterns remain highly similar.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!DaYt!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9a7b819e-933e-421e-a6e3-62c62022a1f2_3220x972.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!DaYt!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9a7b819e-933e-421e-a6e3-62c62022a1f2_3220x972.png)

I do not view the similarities in chart patterns as merely a coincidence.  As I have said since the beginning of December, this type of price action is commonly seen near major tops.

For ARKK, the major top came last February.  This is also where we saw valuations peak along with the more speculative areas of the market.  I cover this concept in more detail in my 12/5 report [“We’re Not in Kansas Anymore”](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/were-not-in-kansas-anymore).

This is crucial to note because, as legendary investor Howard Marks states, it is important to constantly assess where investor appetite toward risk stands.  In our current environment, it is easy to see that investor risk appetite hit a peak last February.  This is when the devaluing of high multiple growth stocks began.

As I have discussed in detail, the Federal Reserve and monetary policy ultimately dictate the amount of risk investors should take.  The Fed has been signaling tighter monetary policy in response to 40 year high inflation levels, and the market is reacting accordingly.

None of this is new or unusual market behavior.  Late cycle stocks are currently outperforming.  More speculative, unprofitable tech/biotech stocks are currently underperforming.  The major difference this time, is that those tech valuations started at bubble levels (never before seen throughout history).

The fact is, most of the post-Covid gains we saw in software stocks can be attributed to multiple expansion.  The acceleration in revenue many companies saw from Covid is real, but fundamentals were not even close to the main driver of the software stock gains.  Software stocks accumulated generational gains post-Covid because at some point, multiples became detached from fundamentals.

Our current software median multiple is 10.5x.  Once again, I urge Marlin Capital readers to consider the possibility that we bottom near previous “cheap” multiples.  A move to the 7x median range, in line with previous sell offs, remains a >30% decline from here.

For stocks that still have high valuations (>20x P/S), history says the 5 year outlook is not so bright.

A high valuation ultimately means that a high degree of optimism is already priced into the stock. A high degree of optimism is likely to mean the favorable possible developments have been priced in; the price is high relative to intrinsic value; and there’s little margin for error in case of disappointment.

In our current environment, there remains a subsector of stocks that still trade at valuations we have never seen before (SNOW, S, etc.).  The risk/reward of investing in these stocks at these levels is not favorable, in my opinion, because you must assume they can sustain a valuation we have never seen any company sustain long term.  If you factor in significant multiple compression, these stocks are no longer attractive investments.

Now, how about the Nasdaq?  As I discussed in my 1/6 report [“A Long Cold Winter”](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/a-long-cold-winter), it is has become more important to watch the general market now that we are approaching correction territory.

On a fundamental level, I do not believe the major shift in Fed policy has been fully discounted.  The Nasdaq 100 PE Ratio remains historically elevated despite the recent 8% sell off.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!BM_y!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6c0f8882-7de9-4a4a-9016-4ff7097f29c8_2048x1252.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!BM_y!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6c0f8882-7de9-4a4a-9016-4ff7097f29c8_2048x1252.png)

In the two most recent corrections, the Nasdaq 100 PE Ratio bottomed below 20x.  We still have a ways to go to reach those levels (>30%).

As I have been discussing for awhile, the market has a number of challenges to deal with this year.  2022 will be an aggressive Fed hiking year.  Only about 20% of aggressive Fed hiking years since 1960 have been above average for equities.

Making matters worse, US equities have performed the worst in fast tightening cycles when inflation is high (>3%), falling 7.5% on average over the next 4 months following the first hike.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!J3Pq!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5e0a79ef-a660-43d9-a363-e55d15d72edb_1284x834.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!J3Pq!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5e0a79ef-a660-43d9-a363-e55d15d72edb_1284x834.jpeg)

By March, the Fed will have completed its taper and will be just starting to raise interest rates.  Not long after, the Fed will likely begin to reduce the size of its balance sheet, as many participants noted QT would begin sooner rather than later.  This is similar to 2018, where we had the combination of an aggressive rate hiking year and QT simultaneously.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Q2Q8!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7ad7dccf-3008-432f-b65e-bd4e024bae58_464x293.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Q2Q8!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7ad7dccf-3008-432f-b65e-bd4e024bae58_464x293.jpeg)

In 2018, the S&P 500 finished the year -6%, with one correction of -12% and one correction of -20%.  I am expecting a year of similar volatility in 2022, and I believe the first correction has already begun.

I have maintained since the beginning of December that we will see a 10-20% correction in the indices. The odds are becoming more likely, in my opinion, that this correction will be closer to the 20% variety, at least in the Nasdaq Composite.  The good news is, I view this type of correction as a great long term buying opportunity.

On a technical basis, I believe it is very likely that the Nasdaq Composite will test October 2021 lows ($14,180).  At my first buy point, the Nasdaq will be 12-14% off ATHs.  I plan to use 15-25% of my cash in my first tranche.

My second buy point is in the $13,500 range.  At this point, the Nasdaq would be 16-18% off ATHs.  I plan to use 35-50% of my cash in my second tranche.

My third buy point would correlate with the Nasdaq entering an Official Bear Market (21-24% off ATHs).  At this point, I would plan to use the majority of my remaining cash, provided the Nasdaq reaches the low $12k range.

---

My goal in any new investment, is for the stock to double over the next 3-4 years.  This equates to an 18-25% CAGR.  In many of my investments during the March 2020 crash (and in the years previous), my stocks gained 4x-20x in less than 3 years.  I was selling into these gains (in stocks like SHOP, NVDA, CRWD, SE, DDOG, etc.), as these investments greatly exceeded my wildest CAGR expectations.

With that said, these older positions are not my focus for new buys during this selloff. Instead, I will be targeting AFRM, COIN, ENPH, ABNB, SOFI, and RBLX. If the Nasdaq were to correct to my 3rd tranche level, I would also consider initiating positions in S, SNOW, UPST, and FVRR.

In the stock market, where you buy is just as important as what you buy.  I believe that sometime over the next 8 weeks, we are likely to see the Nasdaq bottom, but not before major buying opportunities present themselves.  As I mentioned above, stocks have not been truly cheap since the March 2020 Covid crash.  Stocks are still not cheap at this point, but I am hopeful a larger index correction will bring about the type of buying opportunities I have been waiting patiently for.

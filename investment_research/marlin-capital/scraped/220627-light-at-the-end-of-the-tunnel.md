# Light At The End Of The Tunnel

The S&P 500 **officially** entered a Bear Market just weeks ago, while the Nasdaq **officially** entered a Bear Market in late February.  I bolded the word “officially” because these are the exact definitions of what a Bear Market is.

As Marlin Capital readers know, I have been labeling this as a “Bear Market” since late November, even before the indices were 5% off their all-time highs.  As I pointed out in “[We’re Not in Kansas Anymore”](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/were-not-in-kansas-anymore), the Bear Market started in February 2021.  This is the moment when investor appetite towards risk peaked for this cycle, as was evident in the most speculative names (SPACs, ARKK, Chinese Tech, etc.).

At the time, I made comparisons to the Dot Com Bust, as this was the only good historical comparison for this behavior (in many ways).  I talked about the fact that during the Dot Com Bust, more speculative stocks began to peak many months before the indices on my [Twitter](https://twitter.com/Marlin_Capital/status/1473332648756011021) and in the report “[Wild Times in the Market”](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/is-this-market-crazy-or-is-it-just).

This is precisely what we saw in 2021-22.  Of course, a lot has changed since my initial reports.  Back then, we were worried about an aggressive Fed potentially hiking three or four 25 basis point hikes max.  Now, we are likely to get two 75 basis point hikes in consecutive meetings.  When you revisit these types of overlays, like below, you can see that the Covid Bubble Bust has already been every bit as brutal as the Dot Com Bust (in speculative stocks).

I believe that a stock trader ought to act like a detective when it comes to the market.  A detective must collect and compile all evidence and formulate a plan to solve the case.  As a stock trader, my goal is collect and compile all evidence and formulate a plan to maximize my profits.  The stock market is a notoriously difficult case to solve.

Sticking with the comparisons of the Dot Com Bust, let’s now take a look at the flip side of things.  As I pointed out above, back then, more speculative stocks peaked before the indices.  So, did some stocks also bottom before the indices?  The answer, in certain cases, is yes.  It is also important to note, that many previous market leaders like Cisco, Qualcomm, etc. bottomed around the same time as the Nasdaq, so the real answer is “yes and no”.

It is difficult to make generalizations about this period, but there were certain market leaders that did in fact bottom many months before the Nasdaq did.  These market leaders attempted to bottom 14-24 months into their respective Bear Markets.  As I noted above, ARKK and many speculative names have now been in a Bear Market for 17 months.  In the case of many unprofitable tech stocks, the magnitude of their declines also rival that of the Dot Com Bubble.

As highlighted in the chart above, certain market leaders attempted to carve out bottoms 12-24 months into their respective declines.  The Nasdaq still had 1 more wave down to go (in some cases 2) when these stocks began bottoming.

This is where I believe we are now, in our 2022 Bear Market, when it comes to the indices.  A recent [Soc Gen report](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/friday-morning-notes-624) highlighted that they believe a recession 75% of the way to being fully priced in.  This is also consistent with my views.  As I have discussed [in detail](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/nothing-is-easy?s=w), my Base Case for an ultimate market bottom is in the $2900-$3300 range in the S&P 500.  This is the spot at which I believe a recession will be fully priced into the markets (albeit, not a financial crisis like 2008).  If this thesis holds true, we likely still have 1-2 more waves down in the indices before hitting our ultimate bottom.

With that said, as you’ll notice in the above historical chart, the worst of the Nasdaq’s decline was over by the time individual stocks started to bottom.  It is also important to note is that it was not all smooth sailing for these market leaders despite bottoming before the indices.  Microsoft is a good example, highlighted below.

Microsoft made its low almost 93 weeks before the Nasdaq did, but could not muster up a lot of bullish momentum with the Bear Market still running its course. Even Amazon, whose stock was up 221% from its lows by the time the Nasdaq bottomed, did not see any major type of V-shaped bottom.

 This is where legendary trader Stan Weinstein’s “Stage Analysis” is a useful tool to study.  In recent times, we have become accustomed to “V-shaped” bottoms due to the dovish nature of the Federal Reserve.  Historically, stocks attempt to bottom from large declines via a “Stage 1” basing period, as seen below.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!kHLL!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7d3672e9-921d-4df0-ada7-da7ab0d6a511_490x232.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!kHLL!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7d3672e9-921d-4df0-ada7-da7ab0d6a511_490x232.png)

As market leaders attempted to bottom from the Dot Com Bust, we did not see any “V-shaped bottoms”.  Instead, we saw these market leaders enter Stage 1 before the indices, meaning their declines had ended and they started to form bases.  Basing is a healthy part of the bottoming period, and what I expect to see this time around.

Many growth stocks and more speculative issues, like ARKK, have been in a Stage 4 downtrend for 17 months.  As noted, this time frame is consistent with where market leaders attempted to bottom during the Dot Com Bust.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!zYkz!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb7916b1a-bdfd-4a85-a0bb-7cf0972b5e3b_1365x653.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!zYkz!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb7916b1a-bdfd-4a85-a0bb-7cf0972b5e3b_1365x653.png)

More importantly, for the first time during our current Bear Market, we are starting to see notable divergences in price action.  ARKK, my proxy for growth stocks, made fresh 52 week lows on May 12th at $35.1.  The Nasdaq broke through its Mid-May lows in June, making fresh 52 week lows on June 16th.  On this wave down in the indices, ARKK’s May lows held.

One of the hardest hit groups during the Covid Bubble Bust has been Chinese Internet stocks.  The Chinese Internet ETF KWEB declined from $105 to $20 in just over a year, an 81% haircut.  As I have pointed out in the past, Chinese ADRs are among the riskiest assets to own as they face delisting risk and geopolitical risks.  The KWEB ETF peaked in February along with the more speculative areas of the market.

As you can see in the above chart, the Chinese Internet Stock ETF made new 52 week lows on March 15th and is now 63% off those lows some 3 months later.  Chinese Tech stocks have been a solid indicator of where investor appetite toward risk stands throughout this cycle.

Biotech stocks are historically among the most speculative and highest beta industry groups in the market.  Similar to ARKK, the Biotech ETF XBI made new 52 week lows and mid-May, and those lows never broke in mid-June. This is another bullish divergence we are seeing in a speculative and high beta area of the market.

These bullish divergences from the indices are noteworthy because they are the very first we have seen throughout this Bear Market.  As highlighted above, bearish divergences started to occur in more speculative stocks many months before the indices finally peaked.  Now, it is entirely possible that certain stocks attempt to bottom months before the indices finally bottom.  There is a historical precedence to this as well, as outlined above.

**What’s causing it this time?**

The story in commodities may also be telling us about how rapidly the economy is deteriorating.  Copper is often used as a leading indicator for economic activity, and it is sending warning signs.  So, the good news is that commodities may be rolling over and providing inflation relief, but the bad news is that it seems to be hinting of a deteriorating economy.

A deteriorating economy should come as no surprise to Marlin Capital readers.  This is the result of the Fed raising rates at its fastest pace in 40 years.  As I have mentioned before, monetary policy works with a lag, so we are just now beginning to see the effects of tighter monetary policy.  However, when it comes to deteriorating economic data, we have seen nothing yet.  The economic data will start to get **a lot** worse from here, so that is something all readers should be prepared for.  This includes earnings cuts, profit warnings, GDP, unemployment, etc.

This is why, in my view, the S&P 500 remains too expensive for a recession to be fully priced in.  My base case for a bottom is the $2900-$3300 range.  This would likely equate to 1-2 more waves down for the indices.  Market leaders may now be attempting to carve out the beginnings of Stage 1 bases, just as certain leaders did during the same time frame as the Dot Com Bust.

**Summary Points**

* There is historical precedence to believe certain individual stocks and market leaders will attempt to bottom/form bases before the indices do.  It is still likely to be a bumpy ride until the indices finally bottom.
* We are seeing the first bullish divergences in ARKK/KWEB/XBI and more speculative stocks.  These ETFs peaked 17-18 months before the indices.
* In my view, a Recession is about 75% of the way to fully priced in.  My Base Case for the S&P 500 remains an ultimate bottom in the $2900-$3300 range.

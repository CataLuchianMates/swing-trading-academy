# Round Trip

US stock indices closed modestly lower last week after recovering from the biggest selloff in 2 years.  The S&P 500 neared correction territory on Monday (down over 10% from its peak) while the Nasdaq was down -15.8% from its peak.

We saw a massive volatility spike on Monday with the VIX hitting 65.7, its highest level since the March 2020 Covid Crash.  On Monday, the VIX fell from a high of 65.7 to 38.6 to close the day (-41%), its largest daily % move lower on record.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!gVjJ!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6e67c702-33c1-423a-a33a-026463882f44_344x509.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!gVjJ!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6e67c702-33c1-423a-a33a-026463882f44_344x509.png)

The VIX fell back to 20.6 to close last week.  This is a positive sign for bulls, as significant volatility spikes like the one we saw on Monday typically coincide with peak bearishness.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!GUod!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F55190b7a-b84b-4d88-80cc-4cd091b1d670_1195x475.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!GUod!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F55190b7a-b84b-4d88-80cc-4cd091b1d670_1195x475.jpeg)

This equity market selloff began following the July 31st Fed meeting, where a slew of bad manufacturing and employment data led to a huge flight to safety / recessionary trade.  Recall, we saw the biggest 2-day outperformance of defensives over cyclicals in 17+ years.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Z8m6!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff05697ce-3b5d-4fa2-a824-44e345424324_1103x583.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Z8m6!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff05697ce-3b5d-4fa2-a824-44e345424324_1103x583.jpeg)

At that point, the market was already on edge heading into last week.  This is when global headlines took over, particularly last Sunday night.  An increase in Japanese interest rates by the BOJ (albeit modest) resulted in a partial unwind of the so-called carry trade.  A sharp rise in the Yen over the preceding few weeks made the overly crowded trade unprofitable, causing most investors to head for the exit at once.  As a result, we saw the Japanese stock market crash over 12% last Monday, its biggest decline since Black Monday 1987.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!-QF5!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F09ad2f5f-5f4d-46dc-b93a-be9a3e917f93_565x309.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!-QF5!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F09ad2f5f-5f4d-46dc-b93a-be9a3e917f93_565x309.png)

This turmoil spilled over into US markets to begin last week.  US equity indices gapped down over 4% last Monday, but made lows for the week within the first hour of the Monday trading day.  In my view, last week’s selloff was exacerbated by extremely low liquidity in the futures market.  SocGen’s Nasdaq 100 trading liquidity indicators shows liquidity is currently extremely low in the futures market.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!cGAu!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F09198355-fcb7-41d6-b868-eb4709fad27a_1230x939.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!cGAu!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F09198355-fcb7-41d6-b868-eb4709fad27a_1230x939.jpeg)

With global markets in turmoil, a low liquidity futures market can be very easily manipulated.  When you couple that with panic headlines and pundits on CNBC calling for emergency 75bp rate cuts immediately (looking at you, Jeremy Siegel), it is no surprise most of the action in the futures market heading into Monday’s open was selling/shorting.

This led to Monday’s panic open and VIX spike.  Institutional investors saw Monday’s panic selling as a buying opportunity.  On Monday, US stocks saw their largest 1-day net buying in 5 month, according to GS Prime.  8 off 11 sectors were net bought, led by Tech and Defensives.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!6HHG!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6ad77bff-acad-4779-a9b7-2c235149c056_419x336.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!6HHG!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6ad77bff-acad-4779-a9b7-2c235149c056_419x336.png)

Hedge Funds and other institutional investors continued to buy the dip throughout last week.  Corporates, Asset Managers, Hedge Funds, and Sovereigns all bought the dip in Tech.  Last week’s buying was the largest in nearly 3 months.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!MXgg!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1e228863-1300-4ce5-986e-ed3d43eb3e2d_601x484.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!MXgg!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1e228863-1300-4ce5-986e-ed3d43eb3e2d_601x484.jpeg)

By the end of the week, we had a nice bounceback in some Macro/economic data.  The Services sector remains solidly in expansion territory at 55.5.  Moreover, we saw a reassuring drop in weekly jobless claims on Thursday, leading to the SPX’s biggest daily gain since November 2022.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!CkyW!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ffe270524-093c-48c8-a512-656b73efc07f_603x337.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!CkyW!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ffe270524-093c-48c8-a512-656b73efc07f_603x337.jpeg)

While the previous week’s employment/manufacturing data was obviously concerning, last week’s positive data points show that the resulting recessionary trade was probably a bit of a knee-jerk reaction.  In my view, this is why we saw institutional investors re-risk some last week, as things are (on the whole) not as bad as they previously seemed.

Japan’s stock market started the week with its most severe 1-day decline in decades (-12%).  By the end of the week, Japan’s markets had recoupled most of its lost ground, with the Nikkei 225 only finishing down -2.5%.

The S&P 500 finished the week flattish while the Nasdaq closed -0.2% on the week.  All of that to say, while a lot obviously happened last week, nothing really happened.  We ended the week at roughly the same spot we ended the previous week.

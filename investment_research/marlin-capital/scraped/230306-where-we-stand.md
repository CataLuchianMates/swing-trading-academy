# Where We Stand

With much being made about a “New Bull Market” starting, I believe it is an opportune time to re-visit my [Bear Market Bottom Checklist](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/bear-market-bottom-checklist) report.

**What are the technicals saying?**

As I discussed in “[No Man’s Land”](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/no-mans-land), the main reason for so many “New Bull Market” calls has been the technical strength of this rally.  Indeed, we got a bullish golden cross on the SPX daily chart.  I discussed the significance of the golden cross [here](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/green-candles), as it is has historically preceded above-average stock market returns.

The daily SPX chart below shows the index bouncing at critical 200-day MA support ($3940) to close out last week.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!jb83!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc997fed3-2e17-4816-b566-97c12f6975c9_1029x651.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!jb83!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc997fed3-2e17-4816-b566-97c12f6975c9_1029x651.png)

The SPX is now firmly above all key daily moving averages (21, 50, 200) as we head into this coming week.  My personal definition of a golden cross on the daily chart has also triggered (21 EMA > 60 EMA > 200 SMA).

On the longer term monthly chart, a golden cross has not yet triggered.  Notice that the monthly chart remains in a down trend, with the 10-month MA below the 30-month MA.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!3MLa!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe1aabc5d-1912-482a-9027-1cd46f2a35bc_1031x651.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!3MLa!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe1aabc5d-1912-482a-9027-1cd46f2a35bc_1031x651.png)

In [“No Man’s Land”](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/no-mans-land), I discussed how the 2000-02 Bear Market had a similar 17 week rally in the indices before making new lows.  As long as the S&P 500 does not break February 2nd highs ($4196), this pattern is still valid, making $4196-$4200 the key long term upside level to watch above.  A break above $4196 in the SPX would make the current rally longer in duration than any historical Bear Market rally I have studied (since 1971).

With that said, it is important to note that not all stocks bottom at the same time.  Historically, some stocks will bottom from Bear Markets before the indices do.  I wrote about this [here](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/no-mans-land) and my viewpoint remains the same.  Based off historical analysis and recent price action, I believe it is likely that certain stocks have already seen their Bear Market lows.  This is why I believe selective long trade opportunities will continue to present themselves this year.

**Key Technical Points**

* The S&P 500 daily and weekly charts are both up trending, while the monthly chart remains in a down trend
* A break above $4200 in the SPX would make the current rally longer in duration than any Bear Market rally I have studied (since 1971)
* History tells us that certain individual stocks have likely already seen their bear market lows

**What are the fundamentals saying?**

Despite the technicals looking more bullish, the Macro/Fundamentals are telling a different story.

In “[Bear Market Bottom Checklist](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/bear-market-bottom-checklist)”, I gave 5 Macro Indicators that have historically preceded Bear Market bottoms.  As of March 2023, none of my Macro indicators have reliably signaled yet.  In the previous 6 Bear Market bottoms (dating back to 1982) a minimum of 4/5 indicators had triggered.  Even in 1974, 2/3 indicators had triggered (my data source does not go back further).

Let’s start in order.

**1.    Fed Cutting Rates**

In the past 7 recessions dating back to 1971, equities never bottomed before the Fed began cutting rates.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!lRts!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0e25b133-0986-49f2-afde-a3099790f66d_671x470.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!lRts!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0e25b133-0986-49f2-afde-a3099790f66d_671x470.png)

Current futures pricing implies the Fed will raise the policy rate to a peak of 5.4% before starting rate cuts in October 2023 (6-7 months from today).

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Sg8e!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7d2daad6-2332-4752-98fc-878bcadd0c52_1169x381.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Sg8e!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7d2daad6-2332-4752-98fc-878bcadd0c52_1169x381.png)

One further note on this – historically speaking, each time the Fed has raised the federal funds rate by at least 460 BPs, (>4.6%), it has always led to a recession and spike in unemployment.  In other words, a soft landing has never actually occurred after the Fed has tightened policy to this extent (and this quickly).  I wrote about this in more detail [here](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/inflation-versus-unemployment-research).

**2.    Unemployment Spikes**

Unemployment has spiked before 6 out of the last 7 Bear Market bottoms (excluding 1987).  In 1987, there was no recession and most of the Bear Market’s decline came during 1 day (Black Monday).

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!IuDZ!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0fbb8b3d-34c3-43bf-976e-1ed89ce969e9_763x1083.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!IuDZ!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0fbb8b3d-34c3-43bf-976e-1ed89ce969e9_763x1083.png)

Historically speaking, unemployment has typically risen at least 2%+ during official recessions.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!q8Uu!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4aa456f5-914e-471c-967a-6dd76e602b57_834x961.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!q8Uu!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4aa456f5-914e-471c-967a-6dd76e602b57_834x961.png)

Currently, the Unemployment Rate is sitting near multi-decade lows (3.4%).

**3.    2/10 YR Yield Curve Steepens**

In each of the last 6 Bear Markets dating back to 1982, the 2/10 yield curve had steepened before the market bottomed.  Currently, the 2/10 yield curve continues to invert even further, and is now the most inverted it has been since 1981.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!VAcQ!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd76b0b99-3caf-4344-8267-7a61fe1ae47a_928x386.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!VAcQ!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd76b0b99-3caf-4344-8267-7a61fe1ae47a_928x386.png)

Past instances of such an inversion have always preceded a recession. As you can see above, the past 6 recessions were each preceded by an inversion of the 2/10 yield curve.

**4.    2 YR Yield Peaks**

A rising 2Y yield is typically a sign of future rate hike expectations.  In the previous 6 Bear Markets, the S&P 500 did not bottom until the 2-year yield had peaked for the cycle.

The 2Y yield just made new highs for the cycle last week, hitting a high of 4.96% before briefly pulling back Thursday and Friday (allowing equities to rally).

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!lyRb!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F657bb107-f4ea-4e7b-b07f-fc440216d30a_929x387.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!lyRb!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F657bb107-f4ea-4e7b-b07f-fc440216d30a_929x387.png)

This will be an important sign to watch from the Fixed Income Market moving forward.  A breakout above 5% in the 2Y could spell trouble for equities ahead.

**5.    US PMI Bottoms**

In [6 of the last 7 Bear Markets](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/bear-market-bottom-checklist) (excluding 1973), the PMI improved vs. its 12 months lows prior to the SPX bottoming.  The ISM Manufacturing PMI improved slightly (to 47.7) in February, from 47.4 in January, which was its lowest reading since May 2020.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!MDuI!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa7b18aa2-af67-438f-a15b-77bb2b827ca8_717x339.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!MDuI!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa7b18aa2-af67-438f-a15b-77bb2b827ca8_717x339.png)

It is still too early to determine if this is a meaningful low or short term bounce, so we will leave this one unchecked for now.

**6.    ERP rises 75 BPs**

Equity risk premium shows the earnings yield on the S&P 500 minus the interest rate on 10 YR Treasury Notes.  The higher the risk premium, the more attractive stocks are relative to bonds.

Historically, [we have seen](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/bear-market-bottom-checklist) the Equity Risk Premium rise at least 75 BPs prior to the market bottoming.  This rise in ERP has occurred in all 7 of the prior Bear Market bottoms.

Currently, the SPX risk premium is near its lowest levels since 2007 and in Morgan Stanley’s “Death Zone”.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!iKK5!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F65ade802-4dbe-40de-9495-01ed909a6b4a_620x351.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!iKK5!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F65ade802-4dbe-40de-9495-01ed909a6b4a_620x351.png)

One additional indicator to add - stocks have never bottomed before a recession officially starts.  In Recessionary Bear Markets, equities have historically bottomed when the Recession is about halfway through.

**Summary**

The Macro/Fundamental factors historically associated with the beginning of a new bull market are not currently present. Could it really be different this time? This is always a possibility that should not be dismissed. But, as the chart shows below, it will need to be **very** different this time.

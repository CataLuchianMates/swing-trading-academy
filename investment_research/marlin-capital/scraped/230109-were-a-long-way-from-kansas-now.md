# We're A Long Way From Kansas Now

The largest combined monetary and fiscal stimulus experiment in US history is ending in the only possible way it could.

I began writing about the “Covid Bubble” over 13 months ago in [“We’re Not in Kansas Anymore”.](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/were-not-in-kansas-anymore)  The general gist of my report was that crisis monetary policy from the Federal Reserve (in response to the Covid-19 Pandemic) fueled one of the largest asset bubbles in the history of financial markets.  It is difficult to quantify just how big the Covid Bubble truly was, but I took a stab at that in [“The Pendulum Swings Both Ways”](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/the-pendulum-swings-both-ways).  In that report, I wrote:

> “At the peak of the Covid Bubble, the S&P 500 was 40% extended above its 200 week EMA.  This makes it the **3rd largest stock market bubble** in the last 100 years, trailing only the Roaring 20’s and Dot Com bubble manias.  These 2 bubbles were followed by wicked Bear Markets – The Great Crash of 1929 and the Dot Com Bust.”

This is as good of quantifier as I can come up with for the Bubble.  To make matters worse, the Bubble was even more pronounced in ARKK/growth/young unprofitable companies.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!kLk1!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fde0d980d-8c0c-42af-918b-48e4ff9b255d_627x427.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!kLk1!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fde0d980d-8c0c-42af-918b-48e4ff9b255d_627x427.png)

The “new economy” and “digital transformation” were simply monikers born from an explosive cocktail of easy monetary policy on steroids.  As shown above, the Old economy basket has now overtaken all of ARKK Innovation’s gains from the bubble (and then some).

This is a fascinating comparison because, as I pointed out in [“The Pendulum Swings Both Ways”](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/the-pendulum-swings-both-ways), markets typically overcorrect to both sides.  Notice the outperformance divergence of ARKK to the old economy during the era of easy monetary policy.  We are now seeing an outperformance divergence the other way, with the old economy basket outperforming innovation during an era of tight monetary policy.  I suspect this divergence likely has more room to grow until there is a “reversion to the mean”.

I pointed out many of the buzzwords that fueled the Bubble in [“We’re Not in Kansas Anymore”](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/were-not-in-kansas-anymore), but in reality, all bubbles end the same way - in pain.  The stark shift in monetary policy created a necessary repricing of financial assets, the likes of which rivals any bust cycle in history.

Metaverse, Hydrogen, FinTech, SPACs, Crypto, and other profitless innovations (software, e-commerce, biotech) have already retraced their entire Covid Bubble gains. For those who study market history and market cycles, the below chart looks all too familiar.  As I pointed out in [“History Repeating”](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/history-repeating), the largest asset bubbles **always** retrace all of their parabolic gains during the bust phase.  The Fed turned the lights out on the Covid Bubble in 2022, and history repeated itself once again.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!eArU!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9c15f242-ff56-46d4-a9d2-4234c9ad1b4c_618x419.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!eArU!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9c15f242-ff56-46d4-a9d2-4234c9ad1b4c_618x419.png)

Now, it is important to avoid the trap of becoming more bearish the lower the market gets. The time for extreme bearishness was 1 year ago, when stocks at all-time-high valuations were set to face the most aggressive Fed tightening year in many decades.  That year is now behind us, as the Fed has already front loaded 4%+ of the projected 5% peak terminal rate.

After this round-trip in 2022, I can confidently say that I believe we are much closer to the end of this asset repricing than the beginning.  Still, this does not mean that the valuation de-rating is complete by any means. These names could absolutely have more downside in 2023 as the pendulum swings back the other way.  But, as we look ahead to 2023, the bubble has already popped and many of the market’s greatest inefficiencies have been corrected.

The market now values profits over eyeballs (future potential growth), a stark contrast to what we saw in 2020-2021.  With that said, the aftermath of the Dot Com crash is quite interesting in one regard.  As demonstrated in the chart below, the performance of tech companies diverged greatly in 2000-2004 based on their initial and subsequent profitability.

All unprofitable companies were severely punished during the 2000-01 crash, but then, they each went their separate ways based off future profitability.  Companies that remained unprofitable continued to suffer over the next few years.  However, unprofitable companies that became profitable by 2004 rallied sharply, eventually catching up to companies that had been profitable all along.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!yvWe!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb54400e2-f3f8-4a3a-95d5-a7495773ff5e_647x423.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!yvWe!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb54400e2-f3f8-4a3a-95d5-a7495773ff5e_647x423.png)

The above chart indicates that there will be big potential opportunities in companies that can eventually turn a profit. Ultimately, the market will work itself out once we turn the page on this chapter of stagflation/harsh monetary policy.  There are always inefficiencies in the marketplace because, as I mentioned, markets overcorrect to both sides.  Howard Marks has [described the market as a pendulum](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/the-pendulum-swings-both-ways).  It is always swinging from one extreme to the other, spending very little time at the midpoint.  This is the nature of financial markets.  Reversion to the mean can be a powerful strategy if you have a good understanding of where the mean lies.

For stock pickers who are able to sift through the wreckage to identify survivors that will go on to become profitable, there will be many attractive opportunities on the other side of this all.  But, when is the other side of **this all**?

The market cycle typically follows the economic cycle in several distinct ways.  In my view, we are currently in “Stage 5” in the below diagram.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Qu_l!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3c0d63ce-3f7a-4196-8f11-6e0e487a7b7b_617x540.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Qu_l!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3c0d63ce-3f7a-4196-8f11-6e0e487a7b7b_617x540.png)

This is typically the part of the cycle where we will continue to see **high quality, low risk value** stocks outperform.  The type of companies built to outperform in this kind of economic environment have low valuations, are profitable, and are less sensitive to rising interest rates. For those looking to play this part of the market/economic cycle, I have highlighted where I believe we currently are in the diagram below.

Keep in mind the latter parts of the economic cycle can move quickly depending upon the speed at which the economy deteriorates. For now, utilities and healthcare are two defensive sectors built to excel - at least until things start to materially worsen economically.

Speaking of the economy, in the history of US recessions (with the exception of the Dot Com Bust), equity markets have historically bottomed before the bottom in GDP, payrolls, S&P 500 earnings, housing starts, and the peak in household/corporate delinquencies.  The ISM survey has been the most accurate coincident indicator of a bottom in equities.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!8j6N!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fda77e77f-856d-4368-a325-4827cfc5b88c_1256x582.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!8j6N!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fda77e77f-856d-4368-a325-4827cfc5b88c_1256x582.png)

If history is any guide, the S&P 500 bottom would also coincide with the end of Fed rate hikes (and the beginning of Fed cutting).  I wrote about this in [“Bear Market Bottom Checklist”](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/bear-market-bottom-checklist), along with a myriad of other “equity bottoming indicators” that will be crucial to watch in 2023.

Members of the Federal Reserve continue to adamantly express their plan to raise interest rates above 5% and hold them there for the duration of 2023.  Hedge Funds are not buying it, as I noted in [Friday’s morning notes.](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/friday-morning-notes-16)  In fact, they are betting record amounts that the Fed will fold and begin cutting by the end of the year.  Something has to give, as one side will ultimately be proven wrong 1 year from now.  In my view, hedge funds are simply placing a bet on the deterioration of the US economy in 2023, which would ultimately force the Fed into easing.

As I mentioned above, a Federal Reserve pivot is typically a pre-requisite to a stock market bottom.  However, history shows that equities perform quite poorly in the immediate aftermath of a Fed pivot.  On average, stocks have bottomed 11-14 months after the first Fed rate cuts.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!U_ol!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0b402b35-f089-4cd4-ae97-233cfec3c1fc_822x417.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!U_ol!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0b402b35-f089-4cd4-ae97-233cfec3c1fc_822x417.png)

Historically, the forward-looking Bond Market also begins to price in an impending Fed pivot before it takes place.  This is evidenced by the fact that the 2/10 yield curve [has steepened prior to the previous 6 Bear Market bottoms](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/bear-market-bottom-checklist). There is no sign of this occurring yet during the current cycle.

Leading indicators continue to suggest that a major growth slowdown is coming to the US and Europe.  Manufacturing data is typically a good coincident indicator of when a bottom in the equity markets will occur.  The below chart predicts where these surveys are headed given rising inventories and falling new orders.  As long as the ISM is in decline, history tells us that it will be difficult for the S&P 500 to bottom.

The below chart projects the decline in S&P 500 earnings based off leading economic indicators.  The current projection points to 10%-20% EPS decline for the S&P 500.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!zrfM!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fdcfc18b4-e052-4c3b-a6fb-7242ea436873_630x422.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!zrfM!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fdcfc18b4-e052-4c3b-a6fb-7242ea436873_630x422.png)

The key leading indicators are pointing to a major growth slowdown, and in my view, forward estimates are still not properly reflecting such.  In fact, consensus is predicting the mildest earnings recession (for any economic recession) in US history.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!ib3q!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9f9941c0-e640-4082-a4df-d956b1b2107c_777x383.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!ib3q!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9f9941c0-e640-4082-a4df-d956b1b2107c_777x383.png)

The current consensus estimate for 2023 S&P 500 EPS is around $225 (+2% YoY).  JPM’s LEI-based model is predicting a **10-20% EPS decline** for 2023 instead, which would put earnings somewhere between $176-$198.

Currently, the S&P 500’s forward P/E multiple is perched right at its 30-year average.  However, if earnings estimates continue to slide, the multiple is actually higher than it looks on the surface.  If JPM’s EPS model is correct, the SPX is currently trading at a Fwd P/E multiple of 19.6x-22x, rather than the 16.7x depicted below.  This is still quite high (40%+ premium) compared to all previous Bear Market bottoms over the last 30 years, including the December 2018 market meltdown and March 2020 Covid Crash (which were quick “Bear Markets” due to instantaneous Fed pivots).

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!HyGm!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1b0017b9-5d6b-4f16-906a-77ae2b7fdc06_624x423.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!HyGm!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1b0017b9-5d6b-4f16-906a-77ae2b7fdc06_624x423.png)

As depicted above, the previous 5 Bear Markets (dating back to 1990) each bottomed at or below a 14x Fwd P/E multiple for the S&P 500. Historically speaking, market-wide valuations are not cheap enough to suggest that we have reached a recessionary bear market bottom.

**Summary Points**

* The Federal Reserve turned the lights out on the Covid Bubble in 2022
* The market’s greatest inefficiencies have already been corrected, but the pendulum has not yet swung to extreme fear/panic
* Opportunities are likely to arise in unprofitable, depressed tech names that can eventually turn a profit
* Leading indicators suggest we are heading for a recession, but this is not yet reflected in consensus earnings estimates
* Market-wide valuations are not cheap enough to suggest that we have reached a recessionary bear market bottom
* My target for the S&P 500 remains $2900-$3300

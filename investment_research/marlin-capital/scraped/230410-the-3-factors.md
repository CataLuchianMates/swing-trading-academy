# The 3 Factors

**Macro**

Many Macro indicators have been showing signs of deterioration, which should be seen as a concern for investors. Last week, the ISM Manufacturing Index printed a 46.3 reading for March, its lowest reading since May 2020.

In the past 70 years, whenever manufacturing ISM dropped below 45, recessions occurred on 11 out of 12 occasions (exception was 1967).  Also note that non-manufacturing ISM came in at 51.2, its 4th weakest reading since the 2008 GFC.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!qHjc!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7efba108-9ded-4387-8cf8-36e6145cfff9_737x433.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!qHjc!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7efba108-9ded-4387-8cf8-36e6145cfff9_737x433.png)

Historically speaking, the ISM Manufacturing Survey is the most accurate coincident indicator of a bottom in equities when a Recession occurs.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!tKjL!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6a93e6f1-1ac3-4a83-9acf-5ea91572563b_1255x582.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!tKjL!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6a93e6f1-1ac3-4a83-9acf-5ea91572563b_1255x582.png)

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!VXdS!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ffdac2204-f041-47e5-a0a1-9989c5d62e89_414x344.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!VXdS!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ffdac2204-f041-47e5-a0a1-9989c5d62e89_414x344.png)

Another historically accurate Recession indicator is the US 2/10 yr yield curve.  I have discussed the inverted yield curve in detail in the past, including how we had the most inverted yield curve since 1981 before the recent steepening. The inverted yield curve has preceded all 6 of the last 6 recessions dating back to 1980.

Historically, the US 2/10 yield curve flattens and inverts in anticipation of recessions.  Then, the yield curve steepens as recessions begin, as short rates fall in anticipation of rate cuts.  The US 2/10 YR yield curve has steepened from -110 bps to -50 bps in the last 4 weeks, with the Fed funds futures market pricing in 3 rate cuts before the end of 2023.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!P1Em!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F93f0e7b1-d994-46e7-8f0c-2b37d176d867_575x417.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!P1Em!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F93f0e7b1-d994-46e7-8f0c-2b37d176d867_575x417.png)

The labor market continues to show signs of strength, as evidenced by [Friday’s jobs report](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/march-jobs-report-april-7-2023-123139159.html).  Unemployment has not spiked significantly yet, coming in at 3.5% for Mach versus 3.6% expected.  Unemployment is a lagging indicator, meaning that it tends to spike and peak after the recession has already begun.  Historically, peak unemployment is one of the best “all-clear” signals for equities coming out of a Bear Market.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!I98v!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff6e2775d-cf6c-4254-8795-3c6ef2f2a3ec_567x431.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!I98v!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff6e2775d-cf6c-4254-8795-3c6ef2f2a3ec_567x431.png)

Moreover, buying equities when Unemployment is high (>5-8%) has produced the best forward market returns (since 1948).

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!kSqG!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe21e1945-ab6f-484d-8590-d0132efff07e_738x406.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!kSqG!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe21e1945-ab6f-484d-8590-d0132efff07e_738x406.png)

This is because stocks have never historically bottomed before the start of a recession.  In fact, past market history suggests the worst of the market declines happen during the Recession.

Since 1929, around 2/3rds of the S&P 500 peak to trough drawdowns have occurred during, not before, US recessions.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!0N5_!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3bbfe79b-fe12-4f4d-9a74-32968ea8e8d9_793x448.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!0N5_!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3bbfe79b-fe12-4f4d-9a74-32968ea8e8d9_793x448.png)

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!uhMp!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0d00e12e-282f-48e4-a835-03ae3df7843e_559x492.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!uhMp!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0d00e12e-282f-48e4-a835-03ae3df7843e_559x492.png)

To take it a step further, the worst of the declines often happen when the yield curve uninverts and the Fed cuts rates.  I talked about this and included a data table in “[We’re A Long Way From Kansas Now](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/were-a-long-way-from-kansas-now)”.

Finally, lending standards have tightened significantly, another leading sign of an impending recession. When lenders tighten their standards, it means that they are becoming more risk-averse, and it becomes harder for borrowers to get access to credit.  In response to the banking crisis, lenders have tightened their standards even more.  Historically, lending standards never get this tight without a recession.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!b85y!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1d33d263-1952-47c4-ab9b-1ec65de07e19_549x435.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!b85y!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1d33d263-1952-47c4-ab9b-1ec65de07e19_549x435.png)

With the labor market still so strong, the Fed may not be done hiking rates yet.  Following Friday’s jobs report, the probability of a Fed rate hike in May rose to over 70%.  Even if the Fed stops here, though, it is likely too late to matter for the economy.  In my research, the Fed has never hiked rates more than 470 bps at a time without a recession occurring (and we are not in a recession yet, according to the [Sahm Rule Recession Indicator](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/SAHMREALTIME)).

**Fundamentals**

The fundamentals of the market have also been showing signs of deterioration, with earnings being a key concern.  When the ISM new orders index falls below 45, it has typically meant that an EPS recession is on the horizon.  This is because new orders are a leading indicator of future earnings growth.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!SeOU!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0a819780-b341-40aa-a30f-31aca21d0d0e_750x438.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!SeOU!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0a819780-b341-40aa-a30f-31aca21d0d0e_750x438.png)

Consensus expectations are for EPS to fall by -7% YoY in Q1, the largest decline since Q3 2020 and a significant deterioration from the -1% YoY decline posted in Q4.  However, if analysts projections come to fruition, this quarter will represent the trough in S&P 500 earnings growth.  This is where I believe a significant risk to the downside remains.

In addition to earnings, there has been a flight to safety, with strong balance sheet stocks [greatly outperforming](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/home-on-the-range) weak balance sheet stocks.  Companies with strong balance sheets are better able to weather the pressure of higher interest rates.  As a result, investors have been flocking to these types of stocks.

Mega-cap tech stocks are considered as safe as they come, since these stocks will be able to weather the storm of higher interest rates.  As a result, these stocks have been outperforming in 2023.

On the other hand, high cash-burning stocks may struggle under the pressure of higher interest rates. This includes companies with high operating expenses and low cash on their balance sheets along with high debt levels.

Two companies that fit this criterion that I will be stalking for potential short opportunities include Ncino (NCNO) and Sunnova Energy (NOVA).

nCino has $82M of cash on their balance sheet versus $96M of debt.  Meanwhile, the company generated negative (-$86.9) of operating income over its last 12 months.  At this rate, the company will burn through the rest of their cash balance within the next year.

A second company using finite amounts of cash/high debt to fund operating losses is Sunnova Energy.  The company currently has $367M of cash on its balance sheet versus $5.43B in debt (the market cap is $1.7B).  Despite the low cash ratio, the company continues to operate at a heavy loss, generating negative operating income of (-$90M) over its last 12 months.

Be sure to follow along with the daily notes for entry ideas on potential short setups in these names.

**Technicals**

Despite the deteriorating Macro and Fundamental indicators, the technicals of the market have been showing strength.  All key moving averages are currently pointing up, indicating a bullish trend.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!oWkM!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2fd2b48d-396e-4089-b7d9-3b7839e61c21_953x720.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!oWkM!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2fd2b48d-396e-4089-b7d9-3b7839e61c21_953x720.png)

I believe positioning is responsible for much of the market strength, as I have [discussed](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/home-on-the-range) in the [past couple weeks](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/the-week-ahead-ee2).  [Hedge fund long positioning](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/the-week-ahead-ee2) has remained depressed, as institutional investors refuse to take on any significant risk in this environment.  Ironically, this (along with [record high cash levels](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/home-on-the-range)) is helping to drive the market higher, at least in the short term.  With that being said, institutional participation is a requirement for a sustainable new bull market, and we are not seeing anything to suggest that tide is turning yet.

The other key factor I discussed as a major driver of the rally has been the expansion of the Fed’s balance sheet.  The Fed reversed [6 months of QT](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/home-on-the-range) in the aftermath of the regional banking crisis.  New data from last week shows the balance sheet contracted by $74B last week (as QT resumed).  If this reversal continues, it will be a major headwind for stocks moving forward. I will continue to monitor this action closely.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!HY6p!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe83b38e0-6d02-4f99-810c-7d70a86e4640_2048x1223.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!HY6p!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe83b38e0-6d02-4f99-810c-7d70a86e4640_2048x1223.png)

With that being said, some stocks are likely in the process of bottoming, which is a normal occurrence in a Bear Market. Not all stocks bottom at the same time, and historically, some start to show signs of recovery before others.  I talked about this in detail [here](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/no-mans-land) and [here](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/light-at-the-end-of-the-tunnel), and my views remain the same currently. During the Dot Com Bust, many stocks completed their bottoming process before the Nasdaq did.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!qJ7w!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F08d6473d-5bbe-470c-80ec-5b6a674d45c3_939x153.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!qJ7w!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F08d6473d-5bbe-470c-80ec-5b6a674d45c3_939x153.png)

This is why I believe we will see both long and short opportunities continue to arise throughout this year.

For long positions, my focus will continue to be on strong balance sheet companies whose margins are not being eroded (basically, the opposite of my short ideas). This year, I believe it will be especially important to stay nimble and patient for the right opportunities.

# How Late Is It?

As Marlin Capital subscribers know, I have been bearish on equities since late November, claiming that we are in a Bear Market (even when indices were near all time highs).  We are still in a Bear Market here in April, and below, I discuss what may be coming next.

In percentage terms, the 20% multiple contraction we have seen thus far in the S&P 500 is in line with previous mid-late cycle de-ratings.  However, as I have discussed in the past, the magnitude of the decline matters far less than where valuations actually stand.  This has been a painful lesson for many new investors who were conditioned to buy 20% dips in expensive names. In most cases, those 20% dips have turned into 70 and 80% dips, as multiples have re-rated from Bubble territory back to reality.

On a historical basis, the S&P 500 Fwd P/E Ratio remains above historical norms.  Typical mid-late cycle de-ratings ultimately bottomed below the 16x Fwd P/E range, indicating more multiple contraction may be in store here.

Note that in 2018, we bottomed under 14x Fwd PE, before Jerome Powell ultimately pivoted away from his hawkish stance. As I’ll describe below, 2018 is the best recent comparison to 2022, only we will have an even tighter Fed this time around.

Additionally, we have not yet seen the type of capitulation move typically seen near major market bottoms.  The percentage of NYSE stocks below their 200 day MA currently sits in the 40% range.  In the past, this number has gone well below 30% before major market bottoms were formed.

On a technical basis, I plan to continue to monitor two key levels of support that have held since 2009: the 200 week EMA, and the 40 month EMA.  As I have maintained, I believe a downside move to these key moving averages is inevitable for the market, at least before this cycle ends.

As evidenced below, the 200 week EMA has acted as support throughout this secular bull market that began in 2009.

For patient long term investors, these key moving averages have provided terrific buy points in the past.  The big question for me, is if we will break below these moving averages during this cycle, as we did in 2000 and 2008.

I do not like to simply shrug off comparisons like this, as I believe studying market history is one of the most underrated and underutilized tools out there.  The historical overlay comparisons that I previously shared of ARKK and the Nasdaq Bubble proved to be accurate, as ARKK proceeded to drop over 40%. (It is still an accurate overlay to this point, for those still wondering.)

As I have discussed in detail, the Macro picture is another big concern for equities.  We are embarking on the fastest rate hiking cycle in decades, to go along with the fastest quantitative tightening cycle we have ever seen.

In past reports, I have compared today’s Macro backdrop to 2018, a year in which we also dealt with aggressive rate hikes (4 total) and QT simultaneously.  The market struggled in 2018, finishing down 6%, with 2 market corrections of >10% (the final one bottomed after 23%, with a 14x Fwd PE).

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!PHp_!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F02c834a0-e7cc-43d7-b5cc-20ad1093a84f_579x415.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!PHp_!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F02c834a0-e7cc-43d7-b5cc-20ad1093a84f_579x415.png)

This time around, we will be dealing with an even more aggressive Fed.  The Fed has indicated plans to hike rates 7 times in 2022 (versus 4 in 2018), and many members support at least one 50 BP hike.  Additionally, the QT cycle promises to be nearly twice as fast as 2018, with a planned $90B per month balance sheet runoff (versus $50B per month in 2018).

Investors who remember 2018 should also recall the Powell pivot, which ultimately helped stocks to bottom in December ’18.  Investors hoping for a similar Powell pivot in 2022 may be disappointed as the Fed focuses its efforts on fighting the inflation crisis.

In the past, 2yr UST yields have been an accurate leading predictor of where the Federal Funds rate could be headed.  As evidenced below, 2yr UST yields are pointing to the Fed following through with its aggressive plan this cycle.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!neSZ!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7a25aa87-67fc-4bbd-bb2b-0a24b1099f8e_1284x699.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!neSZ!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7a25aa87-67fc-4bbd-bb2b-0a24b1099f8e_1284x699.png)

It will be important to monitor 2yr UST yields moving forward to see where they ultimately peak during this cycle. A move above 3% would certainly be a risk off indicator for equities.

Investors trusting in the Fed to engineer a soft landing are playing a dangerous game.  Historically, Fed tightening cycles are simply ticking time bombs that have ended in recessions.  When the Fed tightens the screws, the wheels begin to fall off (eventually).  It has not been a matter of if, but when.

Many other signs of deteriorating economic health have already begun to surface, forcing most research institutions to lower their US growth outlook.  This makes the possibility of stagflation in the 2H a very real scenario (slowing growth, elevated inflation).

Speaking of inflation, some have pointed to the March CPI number released yesterday as evidence that inflation could finally be peaking.  Whether or not this turns out to be true is anyone’s best guess.  From my anecdotal observations living in the nation’s largest city (New York City), this does not appear to be the case, especially in the rental and housing market where it is the norm to see prices up 120% YoY. At this point, inflation goes well beyond “just” used car prices.

At this juncture, it is impossible for anyone to know if inflation is peaking or not.  More important to note, however, is that the damage from inflation has already been done to the consumer.  This is evidenced in the below table, which shows that net economic sentiment is abysmal across virtually all demos.

The US is a consumer led economy with nearly 70% of GDP coming from consumption.  Inflation essentially acts as an extra tax on the consumer, and the damage that 40 year high inflation has already done to the consumer is irreversible.

Moreover, inflation is expected to remain elevated above the Fed’s target of 2% throughout this year and into 2023. Taming inflation will be unquestionably be a long journey for the Fed. It’s a long way down from 8.5% to 2%.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!SzVP!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff9e73171-e84d-4aaf-9f96-1936ff95e920_828x544.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!SzVP!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff9e73171-e84d-4aaf-9f96-1936ff95e920_828x544.png)

If this is the case, and inflation remains above target into late 2023, it will be very difficult for the Fed to pivot away from their aggressive tightening plan (at least until the economy or equity market cracks).  In June 1969, Fed Chair William McChesney Martin famously said (referring to the Fed’s unsuccessful attempts to lower the 6% inflation rate), “We’re going to have a good deal of pain and suffering before we can solve these things.”

This is where I believe we stand at this juncture.  The Fed is faced with the tall task of halting the 8.5% inflation rate without sending the economy into a recession.  As I have discussed in the past, this is a scenario that I do not view as likely.  With its limited tools, the Federal Reserve will not be able to tackle the supply chain problems contributing to inflation.  Interest rate hikes are likely to be felt in consumer demand destruction, which would only put more stress on the economy and potentially speed up the inevitable recession.

As we know, the market is a forward looking mechanism that typically looks 12-18 months into the future.  To outperform the market, I believe it is key to anticipate what has not yet been priced into the market, but will eventually need to be.  At this point, I feel very confident in saying that a recession is not priced into equity markets, despite the increased warning signs.  With that said, I believe we will ultimately start to see a recession priced into the markets in late 2022/early 2023.

**Summary**

Keep the big picture in mind. Don’t get caught up in the day to day volatility of Bear markets. We are entering late cycle, and the speed of Fed tightening is likely to cause problems for equities and the economy.  Before this cycle ends (as all cycles do, with a recession), I believe it is highly probable that the indices pull back to the key EMAs I mentioned above (200 wk, 40 month).  This is where I will be looking to buy in my retirement account.

For traders, this remains a very difficult market to navigate with very high degrees of intraday volatility.  Keep this in mind when position sizing.  In markets like this, it is best to stay patient and look to preserve capital by keeping high amounts of cash (if not 100% cash).

# No Guidance

US stocks were mixed last week (SPX -1.5%, NDX -2.3%, RTY +1.2%) as investors continue to struggle against US Trade Policy uncertainty and comb through data of the early Q1 Earnings reports for signs of whether (and how much) of the economic damage is already baked in.

The good news is - the US Stock Market has stabilized since the announcement of the 90-day tariff pause. In “[Hard Dollar”](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/hard-dollar?utm_source=publication-search), I wrote:

> “The 90-day tariff pause announcement removed the extreme Bear Case scenario (deep recession + $220 EPS) from the market, which has allowed stocks to stabilize since last Wednesday.

Trading volumes have normalized after seeing the 3 highest volume sessions in the history of the US stock market. The VIX (which I have predicted likely already peaked for this cycle at ~60), has continued to move downwards and finished last week below 30 for the first time since late March. A peak in the VIX would mean that we are past the point of peak fear/volatility (which, in turn, allows the market bottoming process to begin).

Last week, we saw the VIX fall -7pts despite the S&P 500 finishing the week -1.5%. Seeing the VIX fall this much while the SPX also falls is rare, and has only happened 1 other time in history (since 1990) - April 3rd, 2020. This was after the 2020 Covid Crash had already bottomed (h/t subutrade).

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!wvE_!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F736811b5-22a4-4ba2-b0a5-65f7918aa06a_2048x862.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!wvE_!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F736811b5-22a4-4ba2-b0a5-65f7918aa06a_2048x862.jpeg)

Again, these factors have allowed the market to stabilize and bounce off capitulation/panic lows. We are currently +9% off the April 7th lows in the S&P 500.

On the other hand, we are currently -14% off the February 19th highs in the S&P 500. In “[Hard Dollar”](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/hard-dollar?utm_source=publication-search), I wrote:

> “But now, reality is setting in. And the reality is, there is not much fuel for a sharp rally back to SPX $6000+ with companies guiding down/removing guides, stagnating EPS growth (+3% YoY proj), and worsening economic data.”

Putting it all together, this leaves us in “no man’s land” - stuck somewhere in the middle between April 7th panic lows and ATHs.

My overarching view is that the VIX has likely peaked while positioning has likely bottomed, creating a floor for the market. At the same time, based off historically high valuations (>20x Fwd P/E), deteriorating fundamentals/stagnating growth, and continued trade uncertainty, I see a clear ceiling in the market as well. In [“Choppy Waters”](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/choppy-waters), I predicted that we will be stuck in a $5000-5700 trading range for the next few months.

In [“Choppy Waters”](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/choppy-waters), I highlighted some examples of choppy market bottoms (2010, 2011, 2015). This is my expectation for 2025, as there is no Fed pivot or major catalyst for a V-shaped market recovery (like 2018 and 2020). Market bottoms are often a long, grueling process.

In 2011 and 2015, we actually saw the SPX briefly make new lows months after the bottoming process began (but the VIX did not make new highs). This is also a possibility in 2025, but absent significant deterioration in the economy/labor market/trade, I do not see much more downside past $4835 (current lows). I believe that the market bottoming process has more than likely begun here in 2025, but that it will be a choppy bottom and we may need to re-test lows (or close to $4835).

I mentioned above that I see a clear ceiling in the market, and that there is not much fuel for a sharp rally back to SPX $6000+. So far, Q1 Earnings Season is helping to confirm my views in that regard.

59 out of 500 S&P companies (18% of index) have reported Q1 results so far. Earnings have beaten consensus by +5%, driven by Financials (which beat by +8%), but the breadth of the beats is below average (59%/49%/36% of companies beat on EPS/sales/both vs. average of 64%/59%/45%).

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!jvg0!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe203895b-8852-4157-8715-509623b874f4_989x734.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!jvg0!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe203895b-8852-4157-8715-509623b874f4_989x734.jpeg)

Most concerning, though, is the lack of forward guidance. Only 8 of the 59 S&P companies that have reported so far have issued forward guidance (13.5%). **None guided above consensus.** All 8 companies that actually issued guidance, guided below/in-line with consensus.

Companies tend to shut down guidance amid uncertainty, and that is exactly what we have seen so far through Q1 earnings. During Covid, just 10% of companies issued annual guidance vs. an average of 40% in the 4 quarters prior. So far in 2025 (though still early), we are at 13.5% of companies issuing guidance.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!LIBY!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6174cb28-82c7-40bd-b8fe-ab2267fca2af_629x453.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!LIBY!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6174cb28-82c7-40bd-b8fe-ab2267fca2af_629x453.jpeg)

Moreover, 3 month guidance (0.3x) is currently tracking the weakest since Covid.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!6ixB!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F019b6ab8-8e9e-4880-9709-40665e06731b_551x441.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!6ixB!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F019b6ab8-8e9e-4880-9709-40665e06731b_551x441.jpeg)

Companies remain optimistic in words, but commentary on conditions has deteriorated greatly: mentions of “better” or “stronger” vs “worse” or “weaker” are at 2008 GFC levels.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!tuFZ!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8ee502fd-18a0-4850-a6c2-cf4c28c4d5a7_563x500.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!tuFZ!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8ee502fd-18a0-4850-a6c2-cf4c28c4d5a7_563x500.jpeg)

We are also seeing the weak general market conditions weigh on earnings beats (meaning we are seeing smaller rewards for beats this quarter). Companies which have beaten on both EPS and revs have outperformed the SPX by +0.5% the following day, well below the historical average of +1.5%. This indicates that the Q1 results matter less than usual amid the current climate. Earnings misses, on the other hand, are being punished even more than normal. Misses have underperformed by -3.2% the subsequent day, more than the historical average of -2.5%.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!wrZE!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbbf98dd8-a720-44b3-859a-adc44952b8cd_1147x835.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!wrZE!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbbf98dd8-a720-44b3-859a-adc44952b8cd_1147x835.jpeg)

As I mentioned above, the last time we saw this few companies issue forward guidance was during Covid. During Covid, the premium valuation for companies that continued to guide hit a peak, and companies that withdrew guidance underperformed peer groups by an average of -3% between the date they last issued guidance and re-instated guidance.  **In other words, during times of uncertainty, a valuation premium exists for companies that are still issuing forward guidance.**

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!I1yo!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9455f766-c1c9-4040-93e0-4c7aa7835a80_541x416.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!I1yo!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9455f766-c1c9-4040-93e0-4c7aa7835a80_541x416.jpeg)

It is not simply just issuing guidance that matters for the market, though. Historically, companies that guide below consensus twice in a year underperform the market by -8% on average. Companies with 0 below-consensus guides outperform by +2%, on average.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!COlI!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2158da52-e896-4a18-9831-61f80ea5bc6c_587x471.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!COlI!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2158da52-e896-4a18-9831-61f80ea5bc6c_587x471.jpeg)

All this to say - during times of uncertainty, companies that are still issuing forward guidance are likely to perform better (so far, only 8/59 that have reported issued guidance). Out of those companies, the ones that are guiding in-line/above consensus are at the top of the food chain. Unfortunately, as mentioned above, 0 companies have actually guided above consensus so far in Q1 ‘25.

Needless to say, this is not your typical “Bull Market Earnings Season”, where we see ~40% of companies issuing guidance with many guiding above. So far, only 13.5% of companies are issuing any guidance, and none are guiding above.

Moreover, we are seeing less of a reward for earnings beats (+0.5% vs. +1.5% avg.) this quarter. Putting this all together, it is very clear to me that this is not an ideal Earnings Season for my [Earnings Gapper Trade Strategy](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/the-earnings-gapper-trade-setup?utm_source=publication-search). As I highlighted in “Hard Dollar”, this is indeed a difficult environment for traders, and that has very much continued through the start of Q1 earnings season.

For me, this means being even more selective on Earnings Gapper trades. If only ~13% of companies are issuing any guidance at all, my focus list will shift to only these stocks. From there, I will narrow my focus to companies issuing guidance in-line/above consensus (so far, there are 0 that have issued above guides). Overall, I expect <10% of companies reporting this quarter will meet my criteria for Earnings Gapper trades (perhaps <5%).

It remains to be seen if these companies (guiding in-line/above) will be treated as unicorns and bid up by the market, or if they will succumb to the general market weakness and fade their gap ups. If the risk/reward is there, I will be taking the trades (in stocks that meet my criteria). However, as I mentioned, it is evident that very few companies will actually meet my criteria this quarter. Out of those that do, there is no guarantee these trades will work out as they normally do (during Bull Market Earnings Seasons).

Overall, this remains a “Hard Dollar” market environment for me. The worst thing you can do in an environment like this is to force new trades for no reason. For me, this type of environment means being **even more selective** in entering new trades (and as my readers know, I am always selective, but now even more so). Big picture, I am expecting a choppy market bottom (like 2010, 2011, 2015). As it stands, my view is that we will be stuck in a $5000-5700 trading range for the next few months.

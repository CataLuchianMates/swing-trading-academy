# History Repeating?

I studied 5 of the biggest asset bubbles throughout history.

The common theme in every bubble is the asset retracing ALL of its parabolic gains.

Is ARKK next, or is it different this time?  So far, ARKK has been following previous bubbles to a tee.

Before I get into the case study, it is important to note that history does not always play out the exact same way.  However, I do believe in studying market history to draw parallels from previous times.  History does not repeat, but it often rhymes.

One thing that holds true, throughout history, is that markets gyrate from one extreme to the other.  First, market participants exhibit high levels of optimism, greed, and risk tolerance, causing asset prices to rise.  But then, for some reason, they switch to pessimism, fear, risk aversion, and skepticism, causing asset prices to fall.

This “Boom and Bust” cycle is evident in every asset throughout history.  Legendary investor Howard Marks describes this phenomenon in his book “Mastering the Market Cycle”:

> “For a bullish phase to hold sway, the environment has to be characterized by greed, optimism, exuberance, confidence, credulity, daring, risk tolerance, and aggressiveness.  But these traits will not govern a market forever.  Eventually they will give way to fear, pessimism, prudence, uncertainty, skepticism, caution, risk aversion and reticence…Busts are the product of booms, and I’m convinced it’s usually more correct to attribute a bust to the excess of the preceding boom than to the specific event that sets off the correction.”

As I mentioned, the “Boom and Bust” cycle is evident in every asset throughout history.  The action we have seen in ARKK post-March 2020 is a textbook example of a “Boom and Bust” cycle.  For more details on the ARKK Bubble, please read [“We’re Not in Kansas Anymore”](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/were-not-in-kansas-anymore), where I detail many signs that ARKK is a Bubble in the “Bust phase”.

But what’s next?  ARKK has already declined 50% from peak to trough.  When will this “bust” phase finally be over?

Below, I study 5 of the biggest Bubbles throughout history to see if the past can help us answer that question.

---

**Roaring Twenties Bubble:**

The Roaring Twenties is symbolized by one of the most epic booms in stock market history.  The Dow Jones Industrial Average soared from $63 in August 1921 to $381 in September 1929, a 505% increase.

The epic boom ended with an equally epic bust.  On Black Monday 1929 (10/28), the Dow declined nearly 13%.  The next day, Black Tuesday, the market dropped nearly 12%. By mid-November, the Dow had lost almost half of its value. The market bottomed in June of 1932, when the Dow closed at $41.22, 89% below its peak. The index did not return to its pre-crash highs until November 1954.

As you can see in the above chart, the Dow retraced ALL of its parabolic move.  In other words, the index declined to below its pre-Bubble prices during the bust.

**Japanese Nikkei 1989 Bubble:**

In the late 80’s, many thought that Japan was in the process of becoming the world’s greatest economic power.  The Nikkei Index advanced from $6,850 in 1982 to $38,957 in 1989, a gain of 469%.  In the 5 years prior to its peak, the Nikkei rose 3.4x.

Following its peak in 1989, the Nikkei lost 17% in just 9 weeks, 30% in 14 weeks, and 49% in 40 weeks, wiping out three years worth of gains.  The index declined over 82% from the peak to trough in 2008.

As you can see in the above chart, the Nikkei retraced ALL of its parabolic move.  In other words, the index declined to its pre-Bubble prices during the bust.  The Nikkei has still not fully recovered from this crash over 30 years later.

**Late 90’s Dot Com Bubble:**

In the late 90s, the birth of the internet led to a boom of colossal proportions in tech stocks.  The Nasdaq advanced from $546 in 1992 to $5,132 in early 2000, an 840% gain.  In the final melt up of the Bubble, the Nasdaq gained 278% in 17 months.

By October 2002, the Nasdaq had declined 78% from its peak in early 2000.  As I have noted, the current price action in ARKK is following a very similar path to the Dot Com bust. Below is the Nasdaq weekly chart during the Dot Com bust.

**2008 US Housing Bubble:**

The US stock market and housing crash of 2008 had its origins in the unprecedented growth of the subprime mortgage market beginning in 1999.  By the Fall of 2008, it became clear that the effort to expand home ownership had led to purchases of homes by many people who couldn’t afford them.  Borrowers were defaulting on subprime mortgages in high numbers, causing turmoil in the financial markets, the crash of the stock market, and the ensuing Great Financial Crisis.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Hfz-!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1577fb18-4903-4c3b-96a6-8e19af5723bd_652x554.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Hfz-!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1577fb18-4903-4c3b-96a6-8e19af5723bd_652x554.png)

When the bubble popped, homeowners were left with less equity in real estate than before the bubble began. In other words, the Housing Bubble more than retraced ALL of its parabolic gains following the 2008 crash.

**Bitcoin 2017 Bubble:**

In 2017, there was a massive frenzy of new money entering the cryptocurrency space.  The price of Bitcoin rose from $3,000 in August of 2017 to a peak of $19,870 in December of 2017, a 556% increase in roughly 4 months.

Five days after Bitcoin’s late 2017 peak, the price fell below $11,000, a decline of 45%.  A year later, Bitcoin bottomed at $3,164, an 84% decline from its peak.

The common theme in all 5 bubbles is the asset retracing ALL of its parabolic gains.  It is important to note, on a technical basis, where the parabolic move begins.  Many bubbles begin from normal uptrends, meaning there is no excess or unusual market behavior.  Then, something changes, and the angle of the uptrend gets closer and close to 90 degrees.  This is what is known as a parabolic move.

For ARKK, I believe the parabolic move started somewhere in the $33-$60 range.  In my view, a full retrace to post-Covid highs ($60) is more likely than not.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!G-0n!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F69a1bca2-7285-4662-a5df-47cfd1de5579_1589x963.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!G-0n!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F69a1bca2-7285-4662-a5df-47cfd1de5579_1589x963.png)

A more bearish scenario would include ARKK retracing its full post-Covid move from $33 to $159.7.  Remember that this was an incredible 384% gain for the ETF in under 1 year.  It is possible ARKK will give back that entire move, but we also need to remember that Covid-low prices came during a massive market panic.

In my view, a move to the $50-$60 range would constitute a full retrace of the parabolic move and a “return to the mean”.  This would represent a full deflation of the Bubble, as we have seen in all 5 Bubbles I outlined above.  I believe there is a high probability that ARKK bottoms in this $50-$60 range.  If history is any indication, that is where we are headed (at the very least).

# My Thoughts Post-Fed Meeting 

**My Thoughts Post-Fed Meeting**

The important question to ask coming into any Fed meeting is: what has already been priced into the market?  In this case, the market came into Jerome Powell’s press conference expecting 4 rate hikes in 2022, balance sheet purchases to end in March, and QT to begin this summer.

Fed Chair Powell began the press conference by talking about how the Fed’s communication with market participants has been working.  In simpler terms, the market has accurately priced in the Fed’s expected policy moves this year (mentioned above).

It was a strong start, but Powell only got more hawkish from there.  The Fed, and Jerome Powell, emphasized the need to “Be nimble”.  Initially, the market took this to mean that the Fed will be nimble on potentially softening economic data.  In the press conference, Powell made it abundantly clear that this means the Fed will “be nimble” in case inflation persists higher and longer than expected.

Throughout the press conference, Powell took every opportunity to talk about the upside risks to inflation.  When asked about the biggest risk, Powell immediately talked about it being that inflation is higher than expected.  He talked about how the supply issues are taking much longer than expected, raising the risk of higher persistent inflation.  Powell went on to say he does not expect supply chain issues to work themselves out by the end of 2022, and in some case (semiconductors), not until the end of 2023.

Many questions asked about the extent of how fast the Fed could tighten (.i.e. 50 pt hikes, hiking every meeting).  Powell did not rule anything out, instead opting to talk about how strong the labor market is and how high inflation is.  Powell talked about a “very, very strong labor market” throughout the conference.  He stressed that that the Fed has quite a bit of room to raise rates without dampening employment.  Again, he emphasized that the Fed will “be nimble” in regards to how fast they will tighten.

There were several questions asking Powell to make comparisons to 2018, the Fed’s last tightening cycle.  Powell emphasized that we have a much stronger economy right now than we did in 2018, with higher growth and higher inflation, and that those differences are likely to be reflected in the monetary policy.  Again, he did not rule out 50 pt hikes and raising every meeting, neither of which we saw in 2018.

Once again, 2018 is our baseline comparison to this cycle.  In 2018, we had the combination of QT and a Fed tightening cycle.  In 2018, the S&P 500 finished the year -6%, with 1 correction of 12% and 1 correction of 20%.

Now, the question is: what has already been priced in? As Powell said, I believe the market has the Fed’s current plan priced in (4 rates hikes, QT beginning in the summer, etc.), but the Fed Chair also took every opportunity in the press conference to emphasize the risks that the Fed will have to move faster than expected.  It is no surprise then, that US rate hikes bets jumped from 4 to 4.3 as Powell reaffirmed his hawkish stance.

The question to ask now is: how much more does the market have to price in? It is now 4.3.  The answer is no one knows, and that may be the problem.  Jerome Powell’s tone was crystal clear on the strength of the economy and labor market, and the need to address inflation.  We may have to get to the point where 5-6 rate hikes are priced in, because Powell repeatedly signaled that it would be a possibility.

As for equities, what has already been priced in?  There is no way to know, but in my opinion, stocks have not bottomed yet for 2022.  History tells us that the combination of QT and rate hikes together have not been kind to equities (2018).  In the 2nd half of 2022, we will actually have to deal with the policy effects of QT on market liquidity and the effects of rate hikes on the economy.

If you look at 2018 as an indicator, the Fed started QT in late 2017, so the market dealt with QT for the entirety of 2018.  We also had the combination of an aggressive rate hiking year.  Below is the Nasdaq chart for 2018.

As you can see, we had one 12% Nasdaq correction, followed by a 20% rally, and one last 24% correction.  There is not a lot of history of QT to go off of, but if 2018 is any indication, there will be plenty of volatility once QT is actually implemented.

This is why I have started to buy the dip in my IRA, but am still holding 37% cash for my 3rd tranche buy.  I started to buy because quite simply, many growth stocks are attractive buys for those that have long term outlooks.  If they have another 20-30% downside, I am okay with that and will happily buy more at those prices.  At the same time, I have been in no rush to buy, because I believe we will ultimately see the market trade lower at some point this year.  I am targeting a move in the Nasdaq to the low $12k range for my 3rd buy tranche.

The question is: will that come now, or later?  Perhaps Monday’s low will be taken out in the 2nd half of this year as the market struggles with the after effects of tighter policy.  Or perhaps, it will come after the Fed decides they will need to hike 6 time this year.  From a technical standpoint, we have to be open to all possibilities from here. For now, Monday’s lows must hold in the Nasdaq in order for stocks to rally.  Otherwise, I believe the final wave down will likely come now.

Overall, I am expecting these lows to break at some point in 2022.  The risks remain to the downside in my opinion, and the biggest risk remains the Fed moving faster than expected. As an investor, I will continue to be patient with my remaining cash position. As a trader, I will continue to monitor the price action for clues of what may come next.

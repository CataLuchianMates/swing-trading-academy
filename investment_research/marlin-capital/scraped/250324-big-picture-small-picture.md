# Big Picture, Small Picture

The S&P 500 eked out a small gain last week (+0.5%), breaking a 4-week losing streak that had featured consistent -2% + selloffs.

On the bullish side of things, the US economy has clearly slowed, but last week’s data showed that growth is not collapsing. In addition, the trading community has already shed a huge amount of length in US equities and overall positioning is now underweight. Positioning is no longer the headwind it was throughout this correction, and in fact, it is now a tailwind (at least in the short term).

On the bearish side of things, if this turns out to be more than just a growth scare and the market needs to price in a recession, the S&P 500 P/E multiple will likely continue to be under significant pressure (and is far from cheap). As I have covered in detail, if we do see a recession in 2025, it is very unlikely the market has seen its lows for the year.

Putting it all together, while there is some comfort in cleaner positioning and cheaper valuations, it feels like we are still in more of a “risk off” market until we get some more clarity on fiscal policy affects. In the meantime, valuations have been re-priced lower and the trading community is no longer “all in” US equities.

As I have talked about, the 2 biggest parallels I can draw to current times are the 2018 correction and 2022 Bear Market. The 2018 correction reached -20% in the S&P 500, but was fairly quick in time and did not include a recession. The 2022 Bear Market was a 9+ month selloff (much longer) and included a mild recession.

In Q4 2018, we saw an initial -11% drop followed by a +8% rebound.

In Q1 2022, we saw an initial -12% drop followed by a +9% rebound.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!jwMQ!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F37fe5fc7-0b40-4f93-8b2d-ccab0b1a9eda_2366x532.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!jwMQ!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F37fe5fc7-0b40-4f93-8b2d-ccab0b1a9eda_2366x532.jpeg)

In both cases, the S&P 500 broke below the 200-day MA, but rallied back to the 100-day MA. If we see a similar pattern here in 2025, we would see an SPX rally to $5900-$5930 (another +4-5% higher).

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!sLVR!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9a7bf373-a113-4f35-8594-797054d0e36f_1197x886.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!sLVR!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9a7bf373-a113-4f35-8594-797054d0e36f_1197x886.jpeg)

However, in both cases (2018 and 2022), markets ended up falling further following the initial bounce and positioning got much more bearish with gross exposure falling a lot as well before the ultimate bottom was in.

Looking at periods when the S&P 500 was down at least -9% within 20 days from ATHs, there’s nearly always a double dip that typically ranges between -3% and -15% with average around -9-10%.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!-mTc!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4a7e20ca-65e3-42f7-846d-8f921cb87ee9_1128x644.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!-mTc!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4a7e20ca-65e3-42f7-846d-8f921cb87ee9_1128x644.jpeg)

Additionally, it is clear we saw a major peak in JPM’s Tactical Positioning Monitor. After a high in JPM’s Tactical Positioning Monitor, the SPX has ultimately experienced larger peak to trough declines than we have seen so far. Peak to trough declines have typically been between -14-25%, with average peak to trough post JPM TPM highs of -20%.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!xY5y!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc70665d0-daa8-4a39-9cde-418152c6d115_605x590.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!xY5y!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc70665d0-daa8-4a39-9cde-418152c6d115_605x590.jpeg)

This is the big picture. Now for the small picture:

I talked last week about positioning shifting from a major headwind to a tailwind (at least through the end of March). We have seen extreme de-risking throughout this market correction, particularly among institutional and foreign investors.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!gqyc!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe2797e98-3c65-4fc2-be5b-9a024ff4437a_1280x589.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!gqyc!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe2797e98-3c65-4fc2-be5b-9a024ff4437a_1280x589.jpeg)

Last week, I talked about how there were some telltale signs of trend exhaustion in the de-risking of US equities. CTAs (the systematic trend following community) are essentially max short US equities and max long European equities.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!5MTb!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ffffad2e5-8c00-42b2-b7b5-13f12bea1755_1125x744.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!5MTb!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ffffad2e5-8c00-42b2-b7b5-13f12bea1755_1125x744.png)

In “The End of March Shift”, I wrote:

> “Based off lopsided positioning alone, I expect to see some reversion of this trade into quarter end. I believe we will see some rebalancing back into US equities here.”

This remains my view heading into the final week of March. CTAs have net sold over $160B of US equities in the last month, and are now modeled to buy in all scenarios (as they are already “max short” and have no more to sell). In an up market, they will be buying aggressively, which could force a sizable squeeze in US stocks.

Additionally, US Pensions are modeled to buy $30B US equites for quarter end. $30B to buy ranks is the 5th highest amongst all buy and sell estimates in absolute dollar value going back to January 2000.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!DcBz!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1b00512d-63d7-4940-b5cc-82cec9062d48_1070x754.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!DcBz!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1b00512d-63d7-4940-b5cc-82cec9062d48_1070x754.jpeg)

As I discussed last week, these 2 major positioning tailwinds have me bullish for the final week of March. I entered long SPXL (3x S&P) last week to play this potential end of March rally. I sold 1/3rd on the initial pop to $149.21 (+2.5%) during Powell’s speech (to de-risk a bit on the rest of my trade). I am now holding 2/3rds of my SPXL long into this week. My next targets here are $154-$162.

**Summary Points:**

* Big picture - it all comes down to “recession” vs “no recession” now. If this is indeed just a growth scare, the worst part of the selling in this correction is likely over (however, as mentioned above, it is typical to see a “double dip” in either scenario). If this turns out to be more than a growth scare (i.e. recession), though, it is very unlikely US equites have bottomed. This selloff would likely reach -15% + (and recessionary selloffs last much longer in time).
* Small picture - based off lopsided positioning and big quarter end pension buying, I am bullish on US stocks through the end of March. I am long SPXL (2/3rds of original) to play this bounce. I believe we can see a bounce to the 100d MA in the SPX ($5900-$5930) before rolling over for the typical “double dip”.

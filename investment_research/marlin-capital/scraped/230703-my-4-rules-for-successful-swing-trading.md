# My 4 Rules for Successful Swing Trading

With a Holiday shortened week coming up (the market is open for a half day closing at 1 EST tomorrow (7/3) and closed Tuesday, 7/4), I will keep this week’s post short and sweet.

Below are my top 4 rules for successful swing trading.  Keep in mind that the below 4 rules and commentary are for my personal trading strategy.

1.      Do your homework

2.      Buy the ticket

3.      Take the ride

4.      Live with the result

---

**1.      Do your homework:**

This rule emphasizes the importance of conducting thorough research and analysis before making new trading decisions.  This includes sector and relative strength scans, examining fundamentals, industry trends, price momentum, and any relevant news or events.

All my swing trades are mapped out and carefully planned beforehand (meaning while the market is closed).  This includes the price at which I will enter the trade, how many shares I will enter, my stop loss on the trade (max loss), and my upside price targets on the trade.

Planning out the trade in this manner beforehand allows me to analyze the risk/reward on the trade (max loss versus upside price targets) and determine whether it is a risk worth taking.  If I determine that the reward (upside price targets) is reasonably higher than the risk (max loss), than it is a trade worth taking.

By doing your homework, you reduce the risk of making impulsive/emotional or uninformed trades.

Let’s take [Thursday’s GTLB long entry](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/thursday-morning-notes-629) as an example.  In [Thursday’s morning notes](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/thursday-morning-notes-629), I wrote:

> “Long entry will be on a break above $50.  Stop will be placed below $48 (-4% risk)  I’m targeting a move back to recent highs ($54) and later, YTD highs ($58.)”

Here, everything is planned out beforehand.

The price at which I will enter = a break above $50

How many shares I will enter = this is something one must determine for themselves based off account size, risk tolerance, etc.  You should know your max loss on each trade in dollar ($$) terms and be fully comfortable losing that amount before entering.

My stop loss on the trade = a break below $48 (-4% risk and should be calculated in $$ terms).

My upside price targets on the trade = $54 (8% gain - 2x risk), $58 (16% gain – 4x risk)

**2.      Buy the ticket:**

“Buy the ticket, take the ride”.  This is a phrase my first boss on the trading floor used to recite religiously.  I am splitting this rule into 2 parts because I believe both parts are equally important.

“Buy the ticket” refers to the act of making a trading decision and committing to it. Once you have completed your research and analysis (i.e. done your homework), it is time to take action and execute the trade.  This rule encourages traders to have confidence in their analysis and not be hesitant or indecisive.

For me, this often means setting a “buy stop” order so that I will be automatically filled once the stock breaks above a certain level.  Let’s take [Thursday’s GTLB long entry](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/thursday-morning-notes-629) as an example.  In [Thursday’s morning notes](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/thursday-morning-notes-629), I wrote:

> “Long entry will be on a break above $50”.

On these types of entries (buying once the stock breaks a key level), I will typically put a buy stop to be automatically filled in case I am not watching the market or the stock at that time.

**3.      Take the ride:**

This is the 2nd part of the “Buy the ticket, take the ride” idiom my former boss used to say.

This rule implies that once you have entered a trade, you should be prepared to stay in the trade until it hits your stop loss or upside price targets, rather than constantly reacting to short-term market fluctuations.  It suggests sticking to your well-defined plan without being swayed by market noise or short-term volatility.  Taking the ride requires patience, discipline, and perspective.

I have often said that I do not watch every tick in the market.  This is purposeful and something I learned quickly while trading on the floor.  For my strategy, watching every market tick increases the chances of doing something emotional/impulsive (i.e. not taking the ride/following your plan).  I learned firsthand (while trading on the floor) that watching every market tick does more harm than good to my performance, and so I began going for walks along the East River, reading, conducting more research, and writing instead, etc.).

**4.      Live with the result:**

This rule emphasizes the need to accept the outcomes of your trades, whether they result in profits or losses.  Trading involves inherent risks, and not every trade will be successful.  It is important to have realistic expectations and understand that losses are a part of the trading process.

Throughout history, the world’s greatest traders have typically achieved a win rate between 55-65%.  This means that 55-65% of their trades ended with profits, while 35-45% of their trades ended with losses.

Once you’ve done your homework and executed the trade, you must live with whatever happens.  This means not second guessing or questioning yourself, but learning from each individual outcome.

This rule encourages traders to learn from their experiences, analyze what went wrong or right, and make necessary adjustments for future trades.  Legendary trader Nicolas Darvas outlines a framework for post-trade analysis in his book “How I Made $2M in the Stock Market”.

Overall, these 4 rules provide a framework for disciplined trading. They emphasize the importance of research, decision-making, patience, discipline, and self-reflection.  In my career, I have found these 4 rules to be the most important for the proper execution of my trading strategy.

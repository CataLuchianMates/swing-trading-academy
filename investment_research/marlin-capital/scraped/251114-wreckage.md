It is a market of stocks. To assess the overall health of the market, you must look at the entire stock universe. One can not just look at the S&P 500, Mag 7, or the specific group of market leaders at that time.

In a bearish market, leaders become followers. The leaders are typically the last to be sold. The generals are the last to get shot.

This is why it is critical to notice when the market environment begins to change. You should feel the ground beneath you start to shake. Breakouts stop working. Bullish setups stop working. Your trades stop working. Positive news gets sold off. Names on your watchlist begin breaking down. Entire sectors begin breaking down.

#### Hard Dollar Environment

I talked about this on Tuesday in [“Losing Steam”](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/losing-steam)👇👇

We are currently in the trickiest part of the market cycle to trade right now.

We are in the beginning phases of the Bear Cycle.

Stocks are topping 1 by 1.

The bearish disease is spreading throughout the market.

In my view, the most speculative stocks have already topped for the cycle. This means we are now past the moment of peak risk-taking/risk aversion for this cycle.

You can expect -80%+ peak to trough drawdowns in these types of speculative names.

Other stocks, along with the indices, have held up relatively well. History says the indices still have 1 more wave up after the individual stock topping process begins, but there is certainly no guarantee.

But even if that is the case, the most important question you should be asking yourself: “how is the risk/reward here?”

In my view, trying to squeeze more profits out of the 8th or 9th inning of the cycle is not a good risk/reward setup. Jesse Livermore and Bill O’Neil agree:

> “More money is lost trying to play the first and last innings; the real money is made in the middle innings” - Jesse Livermore

> The common fear is “what if it goes up more without me?” That may happen, but remember, your goal is not to sell at the very top price. That’s almost impossible to do. Rather, you just want to participate in the majority of the move.” - William O’Neil

For me, this is not the part of the market cycle where I am looking to get aggressive at all. This is the part of the market cycle where I prioritize risk management, because it is very easy to give back gains here.

Even for the stocks that are still in their Bull phases, they are not trading with the same type of bullish rhythm as they were 5 weeks ago. The entire market environment has changed. The bearish disease has spread throughout the market.

When the market gets bearish, there is no escaping the selling. Relative strength becomes a bull trap. Leaders become laggards the next day.

#### **SMID Cap 6**

We closed out 70% of our SMID Cap 6 Portfolio on October 17th when the market environment began to change. This was a pretty quick flip for us, but I saw the warning signs piling up. Our stocks were still near highs at that time, so this has allowed us to see the final 30% through. Our heavy sells make these drawdowns a whole lot more manageable and less stressful (if you sold 70% when we were up >70%, you should still be looking at >50% gains in this portfolio overall).

For me, “seeing the final 30% positions through”, means giving them to the 10-week EMA on the weekly. The legendary Bill O’Neil used the 10-week MA as his key long-term sell signal in his CANSLIM strategy. I believe the 10-week MA gives the best balance between being able to hold a winner during a routine pullback and exiting when it’s more than that (ie, if we used the 8wk EMA, we would have had to sell NBIS at $44 on 7/7). And as mentioned, exiting 70% near highs allowed us to really do this with less stress.

But now, all of our SMID Cap 6 names except GRAL are set to close this week below their 10-week EMAs. For NBIS and APLD, this week will be their first closes below the 10-week since we bought them. This is my long-term sell signal, so I am planning to exit the rest of these positions tomorrow (all 6 of them/the entire portfolio).

#### Active Trading Portfolio

I have been a boring trader for the last month, but I make no apologies for that. I shifted to “risk off” mode once the market environment began to change. This means I have placed very few trades and the ones I have placed, have been low conviction/small sized.

We came into today with 2 long tries (GLW and SKYT), and both trades failed early and the day. We took our losses and moved on. With each passing trade, you should be getting lighter and lighter on position sizing. As I talked about on Tuesday, this is the trickiest part of the market cycle to trade. It is not a time to be aggressive or heavy in position sizing. But beyond the bearish market environment I have been emphasizing, your P/L should tell you everything you need to know about position sizing.

You can look at every indicator and signal under the sun. In the end, the only indicator that matters is the feedback on your trades. It is basic logic after all. Are you making money or losing money on your trades? I covered this concept in [“Negative Feedback Loop”](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/negative-feedback-loop?utm_source=publication-search). When recent feedback is poor, step off the gas pedal. Trade smaller. The market is telling you to do so. Until your trades start working/gaining traction again, you should remain in this type of “risk off” mode. When your trades start gaining traction and following through, it is time to get more aggressive again. Your account P/L will tell you when that is. Remember, your P/L will tell you if it is an “easy dollar” or “hard dollar” environment FOR YOUR STRATEGY.

**The Druckenmiller Lesson**

This reminded me of the Stanley Druckenmiller 2015 interview where he talks about what a challenge trading through that late-cycle Dot Com Bubble period was.

In his famous 2015 interview, Druck described how he navigated that tumultuous period. He saw the speculation. He saw the excess. He started shorting too early, and let his emotions get the best of him. Eventually, he threw in the towel and bought the top.

Here’s the full Druckenmiller quote:

> "In 1999 after Yahoo and America Online had already gone up like tenfold, I got the bright idea at Soros to short internet stocks. And I put $200 million dollars in puts in, and I lost it all in about 6 weeks...
>
> Now it’s October [1999], and the Nasdaq’s up +400% or something ridiculous. And I had Soros—this is the Quantum Fund—up +35%, **but I’m complaining every day about missed opportunity**...   
>   
> So I went and hired a couple of gunslingers, because we only knew about IBM and Hewlett-Packard. I needed Veritas and Verisign. I wanted the 6 [likely referring to aggressive tech plays]. I hired a couple of young guys... they had their own little accounts and I would plow in on top of their positions... And we ended up the year, I think, something like +42 net or something after being in this deep hole because I rode this crazy NASDAQ wave in 99…   
>   
> The next thing that happens is... January of 2000 I go into Soros’s office and I say **I’m selling all the tech stocks, selling everything.** This is crazy…at 104 times earnings. **This is nuts... we’re going to step aside, wait for the next fat pitch.** I didn’t fire the two gunslingers. They didn’t have enough money to really hurt the fund, but they started making +3% a day and I’m out. It is driving me nuts. I mean their little account is like up +50% on the year. I think Quantum was up +7. It’s just sitting there…   
>   
> *[2 months later, the market continues ripping while Druckenmiller is still 100% cash]*
>
> “Anyway, I pick up the phone finally. I think I missed the top by an hour. I bought $6 billion worth of tech stocks, and in 6 weeks I had left Soros and I had lost $3 billion in that one play. You asked me what I learned. I didn’t learn anything. I already knew that I wasn’t supposed to do that. **I was just an emotional basket case and couldn’t help myself.**"
>
> - Stanley Druckenmiller, 2015 Interview

A couple takeaways for me on this Druckenmiller story:

(1) Even the greats make mistakes and succumb to their emotions sometimes. This is a difficult game.

Even Druck, one of the all-time trading GOATs, got chewed up and spit out in the final innings of the Dot Com Bubble. As I have talked about before, I believe late cycle markets are notoriously the most difficult periods to trade.

**(2) Will It Really Be That Easy?**

The Druckenmiller story also got me thinking: Will it really be that easy this time?

In other words, we had a very clean uptrend off April 2025 Liberation Day lows with very little volatility. It was a textbook Bull Market rally with higher highs and higher lows for 8+ months.

This is our first real market hiccup since. Will the first hiccup/bout of volatility really be the cycle top? That’s almost too easy for bears.

Or, is this just the first Bear Trap (like how Druck got bear trapped into shorting in 1999)?

The biggest bubbles always have bear traps. It’s part of what makes them so explosive. Bearishness starts creeping in. Bears get excited and start shorting too early. They think they’ve called “the top”. They end up getting squeezed, adding fuel to the fire as they’re forced to cover their shorts.

Only when the final bears have thrown in the towel, does the real peak occur. Kind of like how Druckenmiller threw in the towel and bought the top in March 2000.

Which brings me to right now. 👇

**2026 Bear Trap?**

In my view, from a market psychology perspective, it would make the most sense if this current market dip ends up being a bear trap.

This also fits perfectly into our 2 historical comparisons (the Dot Com and Covid bubbles). In both cases, after the speculative leaders began to peak for the cycle (during an SPX bear trap), we entered a more challenging market environment.

Also in both cases, the first meaningful index dip was indeed a bear trap. Both times, the market ended up grinding higher for 1 more wave higher, trapping all the bears who shorted too early. This is when these bears finally threw in the towel.

Could we see the same thing happen here? I think there’s a very real possibility.

**A Hard Penny Environment**

As I talked about yesterday, I am keeping an open mind here. [I expected 2026 to be a significantly more challenging year going in](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/the-marlin-capital-2026-preview?utm_source=publication-search), and it has met my expectations so far.

Regardless of how the big picture plays out, **the most important thing to know right now is this: we are currently in a “hard penny” environment.** This is a term coined by the legendary trader Mark Minervini.

A “hard penny” environment is a volatile, treacherous market where bullish setups fail, stocks churn, and you must work incredibly hard for small gains. It is characterized by high risk, low liquidity, and frequent sharp price swings. You are fighting hard for every penny of profit (hence the term "making pennies on the dollar”).

I want to touch on my strategy in the **Active Trading Portfolio** when we get a choppy, “hard penny” market environment like this.

I start with buying **1 new position** (tester position) at a time. I do not add another until that first position begins to work.

I also size that tester position **smaller** than I would in a strong tape. For example, if you were risking -1% of your account per trade in a strong market, you should start by risking maybe half that (-0.5%) in a hard penny environment.

When your tester position begins getting traction (ie when the market is giving you positive feedback), that is your green light to add more exposure. This is called **progressive exposure**.

Progressive exposure and position sizing are the 2 keys to keeping drawdowns controlled any time it’s a tough market. In a tough market, it is not the time to force trades or press your luck. Let the market tell you when it is ready.

**The Big Picture**

As I talked about above, I believe we are currently in the hardest part of the market cycle to trade (late cycle). Stan Druckenmiller’s story proves that even the greatest traders in history are not immune to getting chewed up during these periods. There is no such thing as perfect market timing.

But, in my view, the psychological setup (combined with both of our historical comps) suggests this dip is more likely a Bear Trap than the ultimate top. I believe the setup for another wave higher is quietly building.

I am keeping an open mind and letting the price action guide me. What I know for certain right now is that we are currently in a hard penny environment, and I am trading accordingly (with smaller tester positions & progressive exposure).

When the fat pitches come, I will be ready to swing.

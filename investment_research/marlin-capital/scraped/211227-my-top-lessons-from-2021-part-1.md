# My Top Lessons from 2021: Part 1

**My Top Lessons from 2021: Part 1**

As all Marlin Capital subscribers know, I manage an Active Portfolio and a Long Term Portfolio.  The two portfolios have completely different strategies, with my tax advantaged account (and crypto account) being geared towards long term investments.  My taxable account is geared towards position trading and swing trading.

This is why I will talk about my top lessons as an investor and as a trader separately.  In Part 1 below, I dive into my top lessons as an investor.  Part 2 will include my top lessons as a trader, and will be released later this week.

---

My top lessons from 2021.

**As an investor…**

Patience.

As investors, our minds can trick us into thinking that taking more action in our portfolios will improve performance.  That is usually not the case.  Less action, is actually the more effective way to manage a long term portfolio, in my opinion.

True long term (5+ years) investment opportunities do not come around every day.  The company in which you may be investing could be the same one, but the time to actually make the investment for the long term comes around maybe once per year.

For example, let’s say I want to own stock in an exciting young tech company for the next 5-10 years.  My goal, as an investor, is to maximize return and minimize risk.  The best case scenario for any investor would be to buy the stock at its lowest price, meaning the point where the stock is at its lowest level over the next 5-10 years.

Of course, it is quite difficult to predict when that time will be.  It is clear, however, that investing during corrections and periods of fear is the most effective strategy.  We can use different indicators to help tell us when these periods occur.

From a big picture standpoint, we must assess where investor attitude towards risk is at each moment.   The more risk averse investors are at the moment, the better long term investment opportunity there is.  When there is fear and panic in the market, it is the **opportune time** to invest.  A risk averse market offers an exaggerated payoff for risk bearing.  

To an investor that will be holding the same stock in 2028, any external factors happening in the stock market in December of 2021 are irrelevant. This is why I **only** buy during major sell offs and panics.  My goal is to buy equity in my highest conviction companies when the stock price is at its lowest level each year.  The time to actually get aggressive, for long term investors, is rare - and that is why patience is key.

2021 taught me to be more patient as an investor.  As a growth stock investor, you will see your stocks go on sale 30+% every year, without fail.  In the case of 2021 and more volatile years, you will likely see at least 2 of these growth wide corrections per year.  When the selling is simply the result of market volatility, **the best** investment opportunities are created.  There is no other time, in my opinion, you should look to make a long term investment.

When a stock is at or near all-time highs and is on a big run, it is a time to be patient.  Stocks go through periods of buying and selling.  During the buying phase, optimism is high and risk is seen as low.  But in fact, risk is actually higher at this point because of the amount of optimism that has already priced into the stock.  The company has a smaller margin for error when investor optimism is at its highest.  Legendary investor Howard Marks describes this phenomenon in his book “Mastering the Market Cycle”:

The only thing you are guaranteed with long term investments is volatility.  But if you are an investor in the company, as I am with each of my holdings, your investment thesis should take years to play out.

When I invested in Shopify in 2017, my thesis was that the company would capture the #2 position in US e-commerce market share.  I knew from my due diligence, that this was a multi-year thesis which would ultimately need to play out.  This is why I did not obsess over the stock on a day to day basis.  Instead, my top priority was to watch what was happening with the business on a quarterly basis.  Over the long term, the stock price will follow the success of the company.  Over the short term, many external factors will come into play that **will** affect the stock price.

My SHOP investment in 2017 is an analogy to my investment thesis in AFRM right now.  This is a multi-year thesis, as I’ve laid out in [my projections](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/affirm-updating-my-projection-model).

This is the same thing with my investment thesis in COIN right now.  I envision holding this stock in 2030.

Same with ENPH right now.

And so on.

Over the course of 2021, I had to remind myself that I only want to hold the absolute best companies in my long term account.  It is easy to forget this principle in a Bull market where every new company looks like an incredible investment.  Big picture, I only want to have investments in companies that I see myself holding in 2030.  Companies like Asana and Monday.com have intriguing financials, but I see these as active trades rather than 5-10 year investments.  This is why I have consolidated my long term account into 12 positions.

**Recap**

As a long term investor, my goal is to buy my favorite companies when the stock price is near its lowest level each year.  It is easy to succumb to FOMO during bull cycles, but it is always a better idea to be patient and wait for a correction.  On average, we see a 14% correction in the S&P 500 about once per year.  This is the time to be aggressive.  Note that we did not see one of these events in 2021, making it all the more likely we will see a 10-20% correction in the indices in 2022.

These corrections are the opportune spots to buy, although growth stocks often go off on their own tangent and give separate opportunities.  Still, we are looking at 1-2 spots per year where I will be looking to buy in my long term account. The rest of the time should be spent sitting and watching the company, rather than the stock.

This is also why much of my day-to-day focus goes toward my active account.  There is simply no action to take in my IRA every day, or even every month.  If I did not have an active account, I would not even be watching the stock market on a daily basis.  I would simply be watching the companies in which I am invested, and continually verifying the company’s progress against my investment thesis.  For a long term investor, anything happening today in the market will eventually be yesterday’s problems.  A long term investor must stay focused on the business and use volatility to their advantage.

This is why my top lesson to take from 2021 is patience.  Be patient and preserve cash for those 1-2 times per year your stocks go on sale.  Wait until there is blood in the streets and the panic is high.  These times do eventually come, and your returns will thank you in the long run.

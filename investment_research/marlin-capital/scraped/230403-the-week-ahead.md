# The Week Ahead 

Last week, I noted that all the pieces were in place for a rangebound market, with a slight upside bias “based off extremely negative sentiment, short discretionary positioning, and a large pile of cash on the sidelines.”  Well, the upside scenario fully played out last week into the end of Q1.

If you told me a few months ago that several regional bank failures, including the 9th largest in the US, would end up being a bullish event for the stock market, I would have called you crazy.  But, that is exactly what has occurred so far in 2023.  This may seem surprising at first, but a closer examination reveals that the Fed's injection of liquidity into the balance sheet played a critical role in the recent market rally.

The Fed’s balance sheet expanded by $370B in March.  The only historical comparisons to such an unprecedented liquidity injection are:

1.       The March 2020 Covid Crisis

2.       The Fed’s policy response to the failure of Lehman Brothers during the 2008 Great Financial Crisis

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!dJqV!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F882c288a-bd26-4eec-a393-7bd5723dd77e_591x437.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!dJqV!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F882c288a-bd26-4eec-a393-7bd5723dd77e_591x437.png)

In March 2020, the S&P 500 bottomed shortly after the liquidity injection.  In the Great Financial Crisis, the S&P 500 ended up bottoming in March 2009, roughly 5 months after the Fed’s policy response.

According to a study by BofA, Fed balance sheet expansion is responsible for the majority (51%) of S&P 500 gains since the 2010.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!CDgD!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff532a814-9d7e-46b9-8728-e10259029259_593x401.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!CDgD!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff532a814-9d7e-46b9-8728-e10259029259_593x401.png)

BofA found that Fed liquidity is (by far) the biggest driver of stock returns, with earnings growth coming in 2nd at 23%.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Dsv5!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3370ea21-52c4-4c85-9a40-7d0a50b884a0_598x401.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Dsv5!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3370ea21-52c4-4c85-9a40-7d0a50b884a0_598x401.png)

Regardless of whether or not the Fed's action is officially considered Quantitative Easing (QE), it is certainly not Quantitative Tightening (QT).  The Fed's injection of liquidity into the balance sheet is a crucial factor that has prevented the market from collapsing in the face of the regional banking crisis.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!wQjd!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F89bbdf89-2ea9-4b86-af49-d53d1104d682_895x537.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!wQjd!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F89bbdf89-2ea9-4b86-af49-d53d1104d682_895x537.png)

Moreover, the Fed’s policy response in March allowed financial conditions to continue easing, creating a more bullish (than bearish) backdrop.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!_qDA!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fdc452c4a-81d5-4824-a45a-845fdf53da8e_541x507.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!_qDA!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fdc452c4a-81d5-4824-a45a-845fdf53da8e_541x507.png)

This trend is notable, as major market bottoms typically occur after financial conditions have already hit peak tightening.  With that said, it remains to be seen whether we have seen the ultimate peak in financial conditions for this cycle given the wall of monetary and credit tightening still taking place.

Despite the recent noise surrounding the regional banking crisis, credit spreads and default swaps are showing no signs of a major credit event.  This trend continued throughout last week, further confirming that the issue in the banking system is not spilling over, at least not yet.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!gHbx!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7dc781ee-7b8c-4aad-bf0a-ee0e16f11cb1_661x399.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!gHbx!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7dc781ee-7b8c-4aad-bf0a-ee0e16f11cb1_661x399.png)

With that said, the delayed effects of monetary tightening typically filter through to deteriorating credit conditions eventually.  The chart below highlights the risk of credit spreads rising a lot higher (a key component of the Financial Conditions Index) based off the 10Y yield.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!qPR4!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe9adf66e-816f-4e62-9cc1-8147beea9812_819x533.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!qPR4!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe9adf66e-816f-4e62-9cc1-8147beea9812_819x533.png)

Furthermore, the regional banking crisis sparked a widespread move into safer assets at the expense of riskier assets.  This was the first time we saw real fear (a flight to safety) in this Bear market, and as I mentioned last week, ultra-light positioning helped to spark this rally.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!odOD!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F508e794f-8a30-473d-bb68-788a97adadf8_681x505.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!odOD!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F508e794f-8a30-473d-bb68-788a97adadf8_681x505.png)

According to GS Prime data (as of last week), hedge fund net long exposure remains near 5-year lows, and has not moved meaningfully in the last 4 months.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!rAEG!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff0aab48e-b0a5-4770-a83e-12fb023f3fdc_973x531.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!rAEG!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff0aab48e-b0a5-4770-a83e-12fb023f3fdc_973x531.png)

Hedge Funds have refused to chase this rally to the long side.  When we see this type of wide-spread risk aversion in the market, contrarian buying often follows.  The market move in Q1 was certainly contrarian when you consider all wave of negative sentiment we saw coming into the year.

As we begin Q2, the market remains in a very unique position with many conflicting factors at play.  Banks continue to tighten lending standards, which has historically always led to a recession and drop in GDP growth.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!MAUT!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F074e04af-f52d-4ed0-a8f2-9c55e253ef87_549x435.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!MAUT!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F074e04af-f52d-4ed0-a8f2-9c55e253ef87_549x435.png)

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Cvvg!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fba9923eb-9696-4f71-b9d9-658f8df3ef7b_899x529.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Cvvg!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fba9923eb-9696-4f71-b9d9-658f8df3ef7b_899x529.png)

Looking ahead, the pieces for a sustainable uptrend/new bull market are not in place, in my view.  The Nasdaq 100 currently trades at a near 24x P/E ratio (assuming no earnings recession ahead).  Stocks are currently near their highest valuations in the last year despite all the “bad news” we have seen.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!yhV2!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe868b402-b659-4aaf-8bf8-1a31597fffd2_957x539.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!yhV2!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe868b402-b659-4aaf-8bf8-1a31597fffd2_957x539.png)

With that said, the technical picture remains bullish as we head into this week. The Fed's injection of liquidity and subsequent easing of financial conditions have prevented a significant credit event from occurring. Technically, we have cleared all my short term key resistance levels, and in my view, the technicals are pointing to a rally to $4200 and YTD highs.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!-PEr!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4bca651e-f52c-4ecc-8e02-0aeeae8daae9_715x533.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!-PEr!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4bca651e-f52c-4ecc-8e02-0aeeae8daae9_715x533.png)

There is 0 reason to fight this trend, especially as we do not know the Fed’s next move, and whether or not the balance sheet will continue to expand, or QT will resume as planned. This will be key to watch in Q2.

In the meantime, the S&P 500 boasts a strong technical setup heading into this week. Key resistance levels ahead include $4150-$4160 and YTD highs at $4190-$4200.

On a pullback, look for support to come in at previous resistance levels $4075-$4080 and $4050. The last line of technical support for this uptrend to stay intact is $3990-$4000. As long as we stay above $4k, there is 0 reason to fight the rally, and more importantly, 0 reason to fight the fed.

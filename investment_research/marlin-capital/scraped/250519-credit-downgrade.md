# Credit Downgrade

Last week was another big one for US stocks with the S&P 500 advancing all 5 days and tacking on +5% overall. The SPX has now rallied +19% from April Liberation Day lows, which means the index is now flat YTD.

The big news this weekend was the Moody’s US Credit Rating downgrade, sending US equities lower this morning as the 30yr breaks 5% and the 10yr back through 4.5%. Objectively, Moody’s downgrade lags the other rating agencies, which have all downgraded the US from AAA within the last 15 years. With that said, the big question will be whether or not there remains debt holders who require at least 1 AAA rating to hold debt. Most of the commentary I’ve read seems not particularly worried as these goal posts can be moved.

Looking back, there have been 2 major credit downgrades for US debt: the August 2011 debt downgrade and the October 2013 debt downgrade.

**August 2011 -** the downgrade occurred on August 4th, triggering a -4.8% decline in the SPX. The index remained volatile with a -6.7% decline on August 8th, a -4.4% decline on August 10th, and a -4.5% decline on August 18th. During this time, the VIX had averaged ~20 coming into the downgrade, increasing to 31.7 on August 4th and peaking at 48 on August 8th. The VIX fell back below 25 on October 28th.

**October 2013 -** the downgrade occurred on October 15th. The SPX fell -0.71% that day. It would take until November 7th to have a 1-day decline that exceeded that, -1.3%. The VIX was 15.5 on October 1st and spiked to 20.3 on October 8th, and then fell back into the 13s by the following week.

This weekend’s credit rating downgrade provides US stocks a clear catalyst to sell off, but I firmly believe the market was setting up for a mini-correction regardless. I talked late last week about how short-term overbought the indices were getting after the huge lockout rally (and how new any new buys up there were simply a bad risk/reward):

Positioning wise, we saw huge buying in US stocks last week. US equities saw the largest new buying since December 2021, driven by both short covers and long buys, suggesting Hedge Funds were “stopped in” amid the big market rally. Overall GS Prime book Gross Leverage +4.1 pts. Net leverage +3.3 pts. Last week’s huge jump in equity positioning was among the largest ever recorded.

The systematic fund community has now purchased over +$165B of equities in the last month (after -$380B of sales during the market decline), taking net length to around a 4 out of 10 (still below neutral). GS Prime expects a lesser $30B of baseline buying in the next 1 month (so still a tailwind for stocks, albeit less than it has been the last few weeks).

Yet, even after the big systematic buying of the last month as well as last week’s massive Hedge Fund chase buying, total positioning still sits in “light” territory.

This is a major factor to me in why I do not see the Moody’s credit downgrade becoming anything more than a short-term selling catalyst. Hedge Funds were wildly underexposed to the +19% post-Liberation Day market rally, and last week finally showed evidence of forced buying.

Hedge Funds remain underexposed coming into this week, and are now faced with a choice: use this early week selloff as an opportunity to add more net exposure (getting closer to neutral), or remain underexposed and risk continued underperformance if this dip gets bought and the market rallies back to ATHs.

Overall, “bad news” matters much less when positioning is already light going in. As I talked about in detail throughout the Q1/early Q2 market selloff, the biggest reason the correction was so violent was because of the massive positioning unwind we saw. We went from ATH long positioning to near ~15 year lows in ~2 months.

Now, total positioning is already in light territory. Hedge Fund positioning is below neutral. Foreign investor positioning is below neutral. Who is the major seller here that would stocks significantly lower? Are funds, who finally “stopped in” to the market last week, suddenly going to turn around and sell what they bought last week because of the Moody’s credit downgrade? In my view, this is unlikely.

Big picture, I believe the market was in need of a mini-correction to shake off the overbought conditions, and this weekend’s credit downgrade news provides that catalyst. I expect this dip to ultimately be bought up. I do not see any major sellers in the market here at these levels, and believe it is more likely that we see underweight funds buying this dip than turning to sellers. I remain bullish here overall.

# Christmas Week

Markets cooled off last week with the SPX -2% and NDX -2.3% as investors digested a more hawkish Fed meeting. In response to last week’s FOMC meeting, the Fed Funds Futures market is now pricing in just shy of ~2 rate cuts vs. closer to ~3 prior. We have also seen a continued re-pricing in the rates market with 10Y yields moving higher in 9 of 10 sessions, and closing last week above 4.5% for the first time since May.

The degree of hawkishness by the Fed was much greater than anyone anticipated going in. Here’s what I wrote following the meeting on [Thursday](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/post-fed-meeting-notes-1219):

> The Fed delivered on its well telegraphed 25bps cut as expected. However, what surprised markets was how **hawkish** the committee leaned on the decision. The biggest takeaway was the median dot plot showing only 2 cuts for 2025 vs. consensus of 3. 4 members had no cut for today. An overwhelming 14 people had 2 cuts or less for next year. Only 5 people had 3 cuts or more for next year. So, the more hawkish message in the median dots was not a particularly close call.
>
> Powell did not do anything to walk back the hawkish statement during his presser, making it clear that the Fed is back to caring more about inflation than economic growth. As Powell put it “the central bank’s year-end inflation projection has kind of fallen apart”.
>
> The last line of the presser was a pretty good summary of Powell’s tone: “We’re at 4.3%. That’s meaningfully restrictive. And I think it’s a well-calibrated rate for us to continue making progress on inflation while keeping a strong labor market.” Message being - we still think we are restrictive now (Powell did reiterate that policy was restrictive about 10 times), and that’s where we want to be for awhile (bias is on hold). I think this is a firm message from the Fed/Powell that the committee is going to be on hold and the bar for further cuts is high.

Last week’s -2% drop in the S&P 500 was broadly consistent with historical examples of real yields rising sharply. With that said, Friday offered some relief after PCE came in slightly cooler than expected (+2.4% YoY vs. +2.5% expected). With the Fed shifting its main focus to inflation over growth in 2025, these are welcome numbers for the market (and inflation data will be even more important to watch going forward).

When you zoom out, last week’s sell off looks like nothing more than a speed bump for the Nasdaq. We are now essentially back to levels last seen on December 10th and the **NDX is still up +1.7% for the month of December.**

  Looking ahead, it should be a quieter stretch from here with 5.5 trading sessions left in the year. Attendance is likely to be light and liquidity will be challenging.

The Hedge Fund community got overly short the tape post-FOMC, and Friday’s price action suggest the squeeze risk is high. According to GS Prime, Hedge Funds sold US equities at the fastest pace in 8+ months last week, driven heavily by short sales (3.3 to 1). In cumulative notional terms, the short selling in US equities over the past 4 sessions is the largest since early January and ranks in the 100th percentile on a 5-year lookback.

# Price Drives the Narrative

As experienced investors and traders know, price plays a critical role in shaping market sentiment.  2023 is a great example of this phenomenon in action.

The year began on a cautious note, with the market still reeling from the worst performance for the S&P 500 since the 2008 “Great Financial Crisis”.  Coming into the year, a recession was all but certain and investor sentiment was nearing record lows (by some metrics).

In 2023, the tide has turned, with the S&P 500 rallying 12.4% YTD.  The robust performance of the stock market (albeit with less-than-ideal participation), coupled with economic data and Q1 earnings largely surpassing (very low) expectations, has led to a growing consensus of a soft landing for the economy.

With stocks rallying this year, we have seen investors and traders chase the move higher.  [Last week](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/the-chase-is-on), I discussed how we saw the largest weekly inflow to US tech stocks on record and how hedge funds have been chasing this tech rally at a breakneck pace.

Moreover, we have seen S&P 500 call volume jump to its highest levels ever.

This surge in optimism suggests that the bull side is no longer a lonely camp (as it was coming into 2023), with market participants increasingly optimistic about the market and economy’s trajectory.

In my view, nothing has changed but price.  It is important to remember that the stock market is not the economy, and the market has historically (often) been wrong concerning future economic downturns.  This is why, dating back 100 years, the S&P 500 has never actually bottomed before the onset of an economic recession.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!XTdF!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8168d502-3477-444d-b6e2-b18b01e3b19f_654x551.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!XTdF!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8168d502-3477-444d-b6e2-b18b01e3b19f_654x551.png)

Legendary investor Stanley Druckenmiller recently shared his views, cautioning that the absence of a hard landing thus far does not necessarily indicate a soft landing ahead.  In fact, Druckenmiller argues that the fact that a hard landing has not yet occurred makes it all the more likely to occur in the future.

> “In no way does the fact a hard landing hasn’t started yet change the probability of whether it’s hard or soft. I would actually argue since it’s taken so long the Fed has ended up with a higher terminal rate. And in fact, inflation gets stickier the longer it stays in the system. This increases the probability of a hard landing.” – Stanley Druckenmiller.

For those interested, it is very much worth exploring Stanley Druckenmiller’s [full interview with Bloomberg Live linked here](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=53wThFFuOqU).

I share Mr. Druckenmiller’s view in that the absence of an economic downturn, or the fact that it is taking longer than expected, is not evidence that we are “in the clear”.  I remain in the “Hard Landing” camp for the economy, despite the fact that this has now become a contrarian viewpoint.

**Technicals**

As I mentioned last week, there is no reason to fight a strong market trend.  Although the participation during this YTD rally has been much weaker than typical, the S&P 500 and Nasdaq have remained in a strong uptrend.

Over the past 2 weeks, we have seen breadth expand a bit as some YTD laggards have begun to join the party.  Coming into this week, I maintain my long positions in the small-cap growth ETF IWO and ARKK (my proxy for high beta/speculative tech).  I believe these ETFs will continue to present catch up opportunities to YTD highs.  Conversely, the long Mega Cap tech trade appears overcrowded and potentially exhausted, which may limit its future upside.

Speaking of this potential broad market exhaustion, the S&P 500 has now closed 2+ standard deviations above its 50-day Moving Average for 5 straight days.  Prior instances of this occurring since the beginning of 2022 have coincided with short term market peaks.

This indicates an excessively overbought state.  Consequently, I believe there is a possibility of a general market pullback here in the very near future.

Another consideration is Options Expiration this week, which has been a nuisance for the market since the beginning of 2022.  Overall, I have found that OpEx week has been positive only 24% of the time since the beginning of 2022, while the week following OpEx has been positive 71% of the time.

With Put/Call ratio subdued and Call Volume reaching record highs (see charts above), it would be unsurprising to see the market consolidate/pull back this week.

With that said, I am currently not looking to add any further long exposure in the early part of this week. Instead, I will be closely monitoring my existing long positions (ARKK & IWO) with stop losses in place at breakeven.  For ARKK, my stop will be placed at $41.61.  For IWO, my stop will be placed at $233.10.

If these trades prove successful and begin to gain more traction, and the market flags/consolidates or experiences a mild (but healthy) pullback, I will be looking to add further long exposure in certain stocks.

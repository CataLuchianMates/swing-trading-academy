# Heading into February 

The S&P 500 advanced slightly last week, but it was an ugly close to the week with rug pulls everywhere.

The tide started to really turn aggressively by the end of last week. By the end of the week, many signs of “risk off” action emerged.

My #1 concern, as has been the case since the Nasdaq peaked at $24,020 in October, is the price action in the most speculative stocks.

New subscribers - now is a good time to revisit [My 2026 Preview Report](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/the-marlin-capital-2026-preview). My big picture thesis heading into 2026 was that we are in the latter stages of this Bull Market and individual stocks will start to peak and fall out of the Bull Race this year (one by one).

Historically, this is how markets top. Topping is a process, not an event. Individual names start to peak and enter their [Stage 4 decline phases](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/grinch-selloff?utm_source=publication-search) at their own pace.

**This brings me to my #1 concern right now. 👇**

Throughout market history, the most speculative stocks are typically the first names to peak for the cycle. During the topping of the Dot Com Bubble, the most speculative stocks who had nothing more than a business plan (i.e. Pets..com, eToys, etc.) peaked first.

From that point on, the weakness continued to spread. Individual names got taken out one by one. By the time the S&P 500 topped in March 2000, over 40% of the SPX was already at 52 week lows.

The same thing happened during the Covid-QE Bubble (in 2021-22) with SPACs, ZM, ARKK, etc. peaking many months before the S&P 500.Pre-revenueSPACs (most now bankrupt) with nothing more than a business plan began peaking 11+ months before the SPX did. ZM peaked 14 months before the SPX. ARKK peaked 11 months before the SPX did.

By the time the S&P 500 topped in January 2022, 66% of the Nasdaq was already in a Bear Market (and 35% of the NDX was down over -50%). I wrote about this rapidly deteriorating market breadth way back in December 2021 in [“Wild Times in the Market”](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/is-this-market-crazy-or-is-it-just?utm_source=publication-search). This report was written about 1 week before the SPX topped and we entered the 2022 Bear Market. Most every stock had already topped for the cycle at that point.

And this brings me to my #1 concern with the price action in our current market cycle. The most speculative leaders of the post-April Bull run (like pre-rev OKLO, quantum names, etc.) put in huge Climax Top patterns back in October. I covered the key signs of a “Climax Top” in [“Weakness” on 10/21](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/weakness?utm_source=publication-search), and why stocks like OKLO and IONQ were textbook examples.

[As I said at the time](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/risk-off-a70?utm_source=publication-search), I believe stocks like OKLO likely topped for the cycle back in October (but that they would still have a big bounce that ultimately forms a lower high). Well, they had those big bounces over the last couple months. OKLO bounced from $70 to $115 (a +64% rally). IONQ bounced from $38 to $56 (a +47% rally). But now, they are following through to the downside (after forming significantly lower highs), and losing their 200-day moving averages. This is Bear Market behavior.

When the most speculative leaders of the Bull Market peak, this is the moment of max risk tolerance for the cycle. In other words, this is the moment of peak bullishness for the cycle. It is the exact point of peak optimism.

This month (January 2026), we saw several more warning signs start to emerge. High Beta momentum outperformed the SPX by ~24% to start the year, one of the larges outperformances on record. Based on the last 2+ decades of data, this officially puts us in blowoff top territory.

This is the largest outperformance of High Beta since early 2021. As you can see in the table above, early 2021 was the Cycle Top for High Beta.

Historically, the S&P 500 tends to peaks 6-14 months after the most speculative stocks peak. This is why, in [my 2026 preview report](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/the-marlin-capital-2026-preview), I said that it is possible we see the SPX cycle top this year (6-14 months from October 2025 would be 2H of 2026).

This is also why I have said that individual stocks will begin to drop out of the Bull race throughout this year (at their own pace). This is how markets top, with individual stocks beginning to peak 1 by 1 and the weakness continuing to spread throughout.

This was a very tricky market setup heading into the year. And now, it’s gotten ever trickier.

The Micro (strong EPS growth, record AI CapEx) and Macro (Fed cuting rates w/ no recession) both say we should continue rallying higher. But, the overall price action since October indicates telltale signs of a market topping process beginning.

At the end of the day, I follow the price action. The market does not ***have*** to do anything. There does not even need to be a clear reason for the market to go down (or up).

The only meaningful reason is that there is more selling demand than buying demand. You can speculate on the reasons why that may be the case, but nothing else really matters.

[As I have talked about before](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/weakness), I like to think of price action as a battle between buyers and sellers (Bulls and Bears). Bulls had been effortlessly dominating the price action battle from April lows up until mid-October. It was a rip-roaring Bull Market.

But then, something changed. All of the sudden, a switch flipped.

I don’t predict who will win the battle (between Bulls & Bears), I just react to what’s happening. And the environment has undoubtedly changed since mid-October.

So, how will I be handling this concerning/bearish price action? **👇**

I will be managing each of my 3 portfolios based off their own individual strategy.

#### Active Trading Portfolio

Overall, it was a solid month for our trading portfolio. Our winners outweighed our losers.

But, we saw the environment meaningfully shift in the last 2 weeks in our trades. Our winners pulled back aggressively, and our only 2 new long entries in the last 2 weeks failed (SEI for -6.6% loss and RDW calls for -58% loss).

[I have talked about this before](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/negative-feedback-loop?utm_source=publication-search). Your P/L and feedback from the market is the best indicator of when to be aggressive/conservative. Early in January, we hit on some big winners, so we continued to push the gas on new trades. But by the end of the month, the tide started to really turn. Our winners pulled back aggressively and our 2 new entries failed. This is why we are able to limit the damage, as we only placed 2 new trades in the last 2 weeks.

At the beginning of my career, I might have continued trading at the same pace as early January and ended up with 5 new losers instead of 2. This was an important lesson for me to learn. You do NOT need to place more trades when your existing trades are not working. You need to place significantly less or better yet, **none**.

As I’ve become more experienced as a trader and traded through multiple different cycles and market environments, I’ve learned exactly what an “easy dollar environment” looks like (for my strategy). When it’s not the right environment for me, I fully shift to “capital preservation” mode.

This reminds me of a terrific Warren Buffett quote about Hall of Fame baseball player Ted Williams. It is one of the best metaphors for stock trading I have heard:

> “We try to exert a Ted Williams kind of discipline. In his book ‘The Science of Hitting’, Ted explains that her carved the strike zone into 77 cells, each the size of a baseball. Swinging only at balls in his “best” cell, he knew, would allow him to bat .400; reaching for balls in his “worst” spot, the low outside corner of the strike zone, would reduce him to .230. In other words, waiting for the fat pitch would mean a trip to the Hall of Fame; swinging indiscriminately would mean a ticket to the minors.”
>
> - Warren Buffett

Similar to Warren Buffett and Ted Williams, I am only looking to swing at pitches in my zone. As Buffett calls them, the “fat pitches”.

This is precisely why I am heading into this week 100% cash in my Active Trading Portfolio, with no plans to add any new positions at the beginning of this week. Suddenly, red flags are everywhere. Last week was an expectation breaker for me.

For me, there are no “fat pitches” in the market right here. Further, I would be skeptical about any market bounce in the short-term right here, even though were a due for one. In fact, shorts could very well return to the menu this week (if we get the anticipated bounce).

Overall, caution is warranted here. The warning signs are building. Stay tuned for updates this week.

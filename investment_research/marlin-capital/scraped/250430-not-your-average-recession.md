# Not Your Average Recession 

Today’s Q1 GDP print came in at a disappointing -0.3% (vs +0.3% exp), raising the stakes for Q2. If Q2 GDP comes in negative, it means that we are currently in a recession.

For context, the last time we saw Polymarket recession odds this high was April 7th, (~67%), when the S&P 500 was trading below $5000. Yet, the SPX is still +13% off its lows, showing resilience despite recession odds climbing to new highs.

The Fed's current unsupportive stance adds a layer of complexity to the equation. Despite today’s negative GDP data, Polymarket still assigns a 93% probability of no rate cut in May, largely unmoved today.

Historically, the Fed almost always cuts rates during economic recessions. Let’s take a look back at the last 5 economic recessions to see how the Fed was reacting, and what it meant for US stocks:

**2020 Covid Recession**

Context: The Covid-19 Pandemic caused a sharp but brief recession. The Fed responded swiftly to mitigate economic damage.

Federal Funds Rate: The Fed cut rates to 0 during an emergency meeting in March 2020.

**2008 Great Financial Crisis**

Context: The GFC was triggered by the housing market collapse. The Fed took unprecedented actions to stabilize the economy.

Federal Funds Rate: The Fed was aggressively cutting rates and providing liquidity throughout this recession.

**2001-02 Dot Com Bust**

Context: The Dot Com Bust was the catalyst for this mild economic recession. The Fed responded aggressively to support the economy.

Federal Funds Rate: The Fed began cutting rates in January 2001, before the recession officially began (March '01). The Fed cut rates aggressively throughout this recession.

**1990-91 Recession**

Context: This recession was triggered by the Gulf War, rising oil prices, and a credit crunch.

Federal Funds Rate: The Fed began cutting rates before the recession officially began (July '90) and continued actively cutting rates throughout this recession.

**1981-82 Staflation Recession**

Context: This recession followed closely after the 1980 recession, driven by continued high inflation and the Fed's tight monetary policy. The Volcker Fed maintained high interest rates to break the inflationary spiral.

Federal Funds Rate: For the early part of this recession (July 1981 to late 1981), the Fed was not cutting rates and maintained high rates to combat inflation. Rate cuts began in 1982, well into the recession (which began in July '81). Thus, the initial phase of this recession qualifies as a period where the Fed was not cutting rates.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!xA9n!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff160d040-f2eb-4e3e-8235-09c7bfba297d_711x183.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!xA9n!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff160d040-f2eb-4e3e-8235-09c7bfba297d_711x183.png)

As you can see, during the last 4 recessions, the Fed was actively cutting rates during the early phases. The last time they held firm during a recession was the 1981-82 Volcker recession. The 1981-82 recession was associated with a -27.1% peak to trough SPX drawdown, while the SPX bottomed at an ~8x fwd P/E ratio.

The Fed's divergence from the norm here in 2025 is worth monitoring closely. Typically, a supportive Fed is one of the key reasons markets are able to look ahead and carve out a bottom during economic downturns.

**Recession Dynamics and Market Bottoms**

Historically, equities bottom around 50-60% of the way through a recession.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Hulz!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe52e7767-6a31-4aac-9cc6-05dc4c62bd0a_559x492.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Hulz!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe52e7767-6a31-4aac-9cc6-05dc4c62bd0a_559x492.jpeg)

If GDP turns positive in Q3 (implying a short, 2 quarter recession), the April 7th SPX lows would align with this timeline. However, if the recession extends into the 2nd half of 2025, new SPX lows would become a much larger risk.

The SPX’s median recessionary drawdown is -23%. We hit -21% at the April lows.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!X7wG!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa3863831-94d0-4b73-80b8-637539a9d66a_643x382.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!X7wG!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa3863831-94d0-4b73-80b8-637539a9d66a_643x382.jpeg)

As I have discussed in detail, recessions typically bring earnings recessions as well. Since 1970, every economic recession has seen a corresponding earnings recession, with a median EPS decline of -15%.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!llHD!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe15ccf9e-989e-4d6a-80f2-ef6b79639037_394x305.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!llHD!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe15ccf9e-989e-4d6a-80f2-ef6b79639037_394x305.jpeg)

Consensus is NOT pricing in an earnings recession yet here in 2025, despite all the negative revisions we have seen. Speaking of those negative revisions, S&P 500 EPS Revision Breadth is now below the 2022 Bear Market levels (and the lowest since the 2020 Covid pandemic).

So, do I still believe the bottom is likely in?

I continue to believe the bottoming process has already begun. With that said, my view remains that it is likely to be choppy bottom, not a clean V-shape. The key risks to my view that the bottom is in are:

1. Recession Duration: A prolonged downturn into late 2025 or 2026 would likely pressure markets further.

2. Earnings: The depth of any earnings recession will be critical. A -15% EPS drop is not baked into current estimates.

**Final Thoughts**

It is important to stay nimble here. The SPX’s ability to rally off lows today, despite rising recession odds, shows some underlying strength. Again, we received bad news this morning, and the market dip was largely bought up. This is a character change from the early April market action.

With that said, the lack of Fed support and potential earnings weakness demand caution. As always, I’ll keep you updated as the data evolves.

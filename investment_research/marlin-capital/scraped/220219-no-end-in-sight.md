It is important, even for new subscribers, to read Parts 1-4 as I give a detailed explanation of what is happening in the market.  We are in the “Bust” phase of a bubble, and history tells us that we are likely heading lower.

In my 12/5 report “[We’re Not in Kansas Anymore](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/were-not-in-kansas-anymore)”, I gave a Bear Case price target of $44 for ARKK.  At the time, ARKK had started to break down near $100, and it became clear to me that we were in the process of a bubble bursting and it would get very bad.  I knew that if ARKK followed previous bear markets and bubbles, as I highlighted in “History Repeating”, the Bear Case scenario would be the one that plays out.  So far, ARKK is following previous bubbles to a tee.

I believe sentiment is important to gauge at all times, as the very same market cycles play out time and time again throughout history.  Howard Marks highlights this in his book “Mastering the Market Cycle”, which was greatly helpful to me in determining where we were in the market cycle throughout this massive boom and bust we have seen post-Covid.

Currently, I believe it is pretty clear we are in the “Denial” stage of the bust.  I think the reality of what’s happened has still has not fully sunk in yet for a lot of people.  Some companies will never recover to their bubble highs.  Cathie Wood sounded like she was in deep denial in her recent CNBC interview, where she refused to admit her stocks were in a bubble and that many will never recover.

Twitter can also be a good gauge of sentiment.  Let’s take a look at where sentiment is.

This is a presumably an investor who started during the bubble.  At this point, there is still no fear or panic from this investor, or most on Twitter.  As you can see, he is not questioning or doubting his investments.  He is saying “My investments are with great companies. They will come back”.  This is the denial phase.  Closer to the bottom of the Bear Market, the investor will likely be doubting whether he is actually “right”.

The current environment is simply not how Bear Markets end from a sentiment standpoint.  Closer to the bottom, I believe we will be seeing many on Twitter saying they sold because they could not afford to lose any more money.  This is how the Bust phase of a bubble has always ended throughout history, pretty much without fail.

Additionally, there are still many investors that are convinced their stocks are the best companies ever assembled and should trade at 47x sales in a recession.  There are still investors conditioned to believe that buying stocks at 47x revenue into 7 rate hikes and QT is a good idea.

Before the Covid Bubble, 10-14x sales was about as expensive as any stock would get.  I recall investing in Shopify in 2017 at 9x sales, and thinking, “I know it is very expensive, but I still think it will do well long term”.  The company was growing revenue over 70% YoY at the time.  Now, there are (presumably new investors) who are still conditioned to believe that their stocks should trade at 47x sales, because the company is that outstanding. If a company is that outstanding, I would think insiders would be buying and attempting to get their hands on as much stock as possible. In the case of SNOW, insiders could not sell fast enough after the company went public and soared to over 80x fwd sales. There are still no insiders buying the stock here.

The issue for many newer investors, is that they confuse how much the stock price is down from its ATH with how “cheap” it is.  A stock that is down a lot is not the same as a stock that is cheap.  This fact speaks to just how high valuations got during the boom phase of the bubble.  As I highlighted above, many software stocks still have bubble valuations.  I do not see those lasting, and I do not believe that this Bear Market will fully bottom until the most expensive stocks are at normalized valuations.

There are a number of stocks, down 80-90% already, that may be seen to some as cheap on a multiple basis.  In my opinion, these stocks will get cheaper.  There is no rule saying that cheap stocks cannot get cheaper, as this is exactly what you should expect in a Bear Market.  The companies that have already declined 80-90%, are simply stocks that less institutions want to hold into a Fed tightening cycle/inverted yield curve/recession.

This brings me to the Macro picture, which I’ve discussed at length in Parts 1-4 and in [my podcast](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/market-talk?utm_source=url).  It is a negative one for stocks.  We are likely to get up to 7 rate hikes this year as well as QT, [which as I’ve studied](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/my-thoughts-post-fed-meeting-de7?utm_source=url), seems to cause more market volatility.  Economic growth will slow, and the signs point to a potential yield curve inversion at some point in 2022.  This would be an indicator of an impending recession likely to come in 2023-24, which I believe will ultimately need to be discounted by the market in the 2H of this year.  As you can see, financial conditions are already beginning to tighten greatly, and the Fed is still easing!

This means the Bear Case scenario I gave way back in early December is playing out so far, in my opinion.  If you compare ARKK to previous bubbles, it is playing out to a tee (see above chart overlays).  In fact, ARKK is actually weaker than the Nasdaq and Nikkei at the same point, as it failed to bounce as much prior to this recent breakdown.

For ARKK, I believe we are now starting the 3rd wave down.  I am expecting that this move will take us to the $50-$55 region.  Unfortunately, I cannot say that the ETF will find a bottom there.  Many other bubbles have had 4-5 waves down, including the Late 90s Nasdaq bubble.  Below is my Bear Case scenario.

As I have mentioned before, I use technical analysis to help determine probabilities.  At this point, I believe the evidence is showing that the highest probability scenario to play out is ARKK declining to the $40 region.  A move to the $40 region would a 38% decline from Friday’s close.  Many individual names may decline a further 50% from here.  In a Bubble, no one believes it can happen, until it does.

As I mentioned in “[We’re Not in Kansas Anymore](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/were-not-in-kansas-anymore)”, I opened up my Bear Market playbook in late November and will continue to follow it.  For me, this means I will continue to look to the short side for trade opportunities in my Active Portfolio.  My top 2 ideas remain HCP and GTLB.  These stocks still have bubble valuations, and I do not believe these are exceptional companies in any way. Both companies are already showing slowing growth, and software spending budgets will only decline as the economy starts to slow.

Since I believe ARKK has another 30%+ decline to come, I am going to be even more patient in my IRA.  I currently have a 37% cash position, and likely won’t start adding until most names decline another 30%+ from here.

**Recap**

My message – stay patient and eliminate any thought of FOMO from your brain.  We are in a Bear Market.  There is nothing to fear missing out on.  I would expect each stock to continue to move lower, regardless of whether it is perceived as a “bargain”.

Nation Champion Trader Mark Minervini has a rule: after a market leader tops, there is a 50% chance that the stock will decline by 80%, and an 80% chance that the stock will decline by 50%.  I believe this will hold true for ARKK too, which was THE symbol of the post-Covid bubble.  As of Friday’s close, the ETF has already declined nearly 60% from its ATH.  I believe it is headed for a potential 80% decline, which would be a move to $32.  Previous Bubbles I studied typically declined by 80% or more, so be prepared for Bearish conditions ahead.

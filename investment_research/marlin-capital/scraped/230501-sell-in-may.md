# Sell in May?

Heading into 2023, concerns about an upcoming recession in the US were growing.  However, more resilient economic data than expected has caused many people to question whether the economy can handle higher interest rates.

In March, we saw several large-scale bank failures caused by a higher Federal Funds rate and an inverted yield curve.  While investors worried about the contagion effect that might arise from these bank failures, the Fed stepped in to backstop the banking system and all depositors.

So far, the Fed’s intervention in response to the failure of Silicon Valley Bank has marked the stock market low for 2023.  This is why, despite yet another large-scale bank failure emerging last week (FRC – 14th largest US Bank), the stock market has been unaffected and unbothered.  In fact, the stock market’s fear gauge – the VIX, is now sitting at 18 month lows.  The last time the VIX was at this level, the S&P 500 was at all time highs (>$4800).

In other words, despite a number of major bank failures and leading economic indicators deteriorating, stocks simply don’t mind (at least so far in 2023).

Goldman’s Tony Pasquariello summed up the current state of the market in a private call with clients last week –

> “Can you imagine if we exit 13 years of 0 interest rates, plus roll off billions in bond holdings, and the penalty is a simple 15% equity pullback from all time highs… all the way down to an 18x multiple? It would be the most elegant dismount of all time!”

Speaking of valuation, if you exclude the Dot Com Bubble and Covid Bubble, we are currently looking at 50-year highs in the PE Ratio of the S&P 500.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!DlhS!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9f755dbd-fead-4c2c-8ca7-30354458ef25_699x287.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!DlhS!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9f755dbd-fead-4c2c-8ca7-30354458ef25_699x287.png)

Circling back to leading economic indicators - The Conference Board’s Leading Economic Index (LEI) suggests that we are nearing a recession.  The LEI is comprised of 10 indicators, including job market and housing market indicators, equity prices, and the slope of the Treasury yield curve.

On average, LEI leads the business cycle by approximately 11-12 months, and a rule of thumb for signaling an upcoming recession is at least a 4%+ drop over a 6-month period.  That threshold was just triggered in March as the drop accelerated from -3.9 to -4.5.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Ds3J!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fcab83b18-0358-45a6-be3e-9b201e023bc0_1081x593.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Ds3J!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fcab83b18-0358-45a6-be3e-9b201e023bc0_1081x593.png)

While leading indicators are pointing to an upcoming recession in the US, Q1 earnings have largely come in better than anticipated (so far).  As of April 30th, 267 members of the S&P 500 have reported their Q1 earnings (representing 60% of the index).

Before earnings season began, consensus expectations were calling for a -7% YoY decline in EPS, which would be the largest decline since Q3 2020, and a significant deterioration from the -1% YoY decline in Q4.  However, so far, 81% of S&P 500 companies have reported above consensus EPS and 73% have reported above consensus revenue.  Both of these numbers are a slight improvement from last quarter, which has helped the market to stay upright.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!dbNq!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F822f41e9-d233-4060-b0d6-5ebd826fcaf4_1085x745.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!dbNq!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F822f41e9-d233-4060-b0d6-5ebd826fcaf4_1085x745.png)

Forward guidance, on the other hand, has been a mixed bag, with many CEO/CFOs striking a more cautious tone towards the rest of 2023.  I have included some interesting C-level commentary below:

NET CEO: “Increasing Macro uncertainty resulted in a material lengthening of sales cycles & a significant backend-weighting of linearity…our guidance assumes these external headwinds will persist through the end of the fiscal year”.

AMZN CFO: “As expected, customers continue to evaluate ways to optimize their cloud spending in response to tough economic conditions in the first quarter.  And we are seeing these optimizations continue into the second quarter with April revenue growth rates about 500 basis points lower than what we saw in Q1”.

COF CEO: “We are assuming material worsening of labor markets with the unemployment rate rising from today’s very low level to above 5% by the end of 2023.  We are assuming adverse effects from inflation and some further worsening of consumer profiles.”

MA CEO on Consumer Spending: “Consumer spending overall remains healthy, albeit with some recent moderation in domestic spending in the US in part due to lower tax refunds this year”.

CMG: “Our base case does not include a recession or certainly not a meaningful recession, it looks like unemployment is holding up really well. It looks like consumer spending is strong right now.”

MCD: “And I would say, our view remains unchanged, with our base expectation is for a mild recession in the US.  In Europe, we expected it to be more challenging.  I think, things are looking better in Europe than they were certainly in Q3, but still compared to the US, I think more challenging in Europe.”

Elon Musk via Twitter (TSLA, TWTR, Starlink): “Fed data has too much latency.  Mild recession is already here…It’s not like just the canary in the coal mine (SVB) died, one of the staunchest miners (Credit Suisse) died too & the cemetery is filling up fast…Between Tesla, Starlink & Twitter, I may have more real-time global economic data in one head than anyone ever.”

Hedge funds are undoubtedly positioned for a recession (and not a minor one), which ironically, has helped to drive the market even higher.  Beta exposure among active US managers is at its lowest level since the GFC, illustrating how hedge funds refuse to take on any significant risk in the current environment.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!CQCP!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd664969e-8e7a-464f-a4db-5d0096c55e5c_817x254.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!CQCP!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd664969e-8e7a-464f-a4db-5d0096c55e5c_817x254.png)

Meanwhile, the Fed is widely expected to tack on their 3rd 25 BP hike of 2023 when the FOMC meeting concludes this Wednesday (5/3).  This would increase the Federal Funds Rate to 5-5.25% in what many expect to be the last and final rate hike of this cycle.

Many economists believe the Fed has gone too far already.  My research shows that, historically, Federal Reserve hiking cycles of this magnitude have always led to recessions.  Since 1955, the Fed has never increased the Federal Funds rate 400 BPs or more without a recession following.

**Technicals**

One of the major storylines of 2023 continues to be the resilience of the stock market.  In fact, 2023 has been the best “Buy the Dip” year in history.  So far in 2023, S&P 500 up sessions after a down day have been the largest ever.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!lt4s!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F01de9f77-8061-49f9-beb6-82144e1706aa_1284x859.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!lt4s!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F01de9f77-8061-49f9-beb6-82144e1706aa_1284x859.png)

With that said, the underlying market breadth (by some measures) is the weakest ever.  Last quarter’s (Q1 ’23) performance was led by the narrowest leadership ever in an up market.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!bdOG!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F412ce203-cd9e-4f6c-9379-a10ececd0917_1230x530.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!bdOG!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F412ce203-cd9e-4f6c-9379-a10ececd0917_1230x530.png)

2/3rds of this year's SPX return can be attributed to just 5 stocks.  This narrow breadth is a worrying sign because, historically, the broader market has struggled following a period where breadth has been so narrow.  Sharp declines in market breadth, like the one we are currently seeing, have often signaled large drawdowns to come.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!LL3T!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F44ab5db2-9152-4498-bb52-cdc6764dba24_1201x857.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!LL3T!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F44ab5db2-9152-4498-bb52-cdc6764dba24_1201x857.png)

This year’s performance is best illustrated by US mega cap tech names, which are now up 27% YTD.  Outside of mega cap tech, the action in individual stocks appears to be quite poor.  The % of Nasdaq names sitting above the 200 SMA continues to drop (and is now at YTD lows).

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!J9gy!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff06ff1dd-f2f6-476e-a854-704e5f1d7b30_1217x637.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!J9gy!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff06ff1dd-f2f6-476e-a854-704e5f1d7b30_1217x637.png)

With that said, the indices are continuing to hold up due to the action in the world’s largest companies. Many will recall we saw something similar as the market topped in late 2021/early 2022.  The current Bear Market actually began many months before the indices officially peaked, as a majority of stocks entered a Bear Market while mega cap tech continued making new highs.

I do not view this action as healthy for a prolonged uptrend.  On the other hand, I would not be fighting this action, as it may take a significant catalyst to disrupt the money flows to MAGMA stocks.  The saying on Wall Street goes “The trend is your friend”.  Stick with what’s working, until it stops working.

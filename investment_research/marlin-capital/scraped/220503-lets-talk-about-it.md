# Let's Talk About It

For this report, I decided to let my subscribers choose what to cover.  Here were a few questions I received from Marlin Capital subscribers, along with my detailed answers.

**1.       What ends this Bear Market?**

My Short Answer: The Fed will tell you.  Follow the Fed, and price.

The Billion Dollar question.  This is a difficult question to answer, because there are a multitude of different scenarios that could unfold from this point forward.  Let’s start with Scenario 1.  This scenario centers around the Fed sticking to their hawkish plan of 7+ rate hikes in 2022, along with $90B+ per month of balance sheet unwind.  If the Fed does indeed follow through with this plan, I believe equities still have plenty of downside to come.

In my view, such a rapid rise in rates will create havoc for the economy.  We are already seeing the economy deteriorate by many metrics, including the -1.4% GDP number for Q1.  It is very possible that we are already in a recession, as one more quarter of decelerating growth will constitute a recession.  If we are not already, the fastest tightening cycle since 2008 should help to put us there.

Many indicators of deteriorating economic health have started to flash, but instead of slowing down and thinking about easing, the Fed is just starting to tighten.  As I have written about at length for the past several months, the Fed is way behind the curve and must now tighten quickly so that they can stimulate the economy when need be.  Aggressive tightening into an already slowing economy is sure to cause problems in the future.

In this scenario, I believe the equity market would likely bottom when the worst of the recession is priced in.  So, when would that be? According to Bloomberg, the last 4 recessions have resulted in an average decline of 39.9% for the S&P 500.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!b1w4!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff9d24c04-488a-47b1-9dfa-0ce3569f9c11_1170x665.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!b1w4!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff9d24c04-488a-47b1-9dfa-0ce3569f9c11_1170x665.png)

If the S&P 500 follows the average recession drawdown during this cycle, it equates to a move to $2900 (40% drawdown from peak).  This would be a -30% downside move from current levels.

This also lines up with my view on valuations.  In my view, another 20-40% of Large Cap multiple contraction is likely to come before the end of 2022.  The below chart plots the S&P 500 NTM P/E Ratio against the 2-YR UST.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!W45A!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F871bc3ce-8596-4ad2-b99a-889d393f5b4e_1162x638.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!W45A!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F871bc3ce-8596-4ad2-b99a-889d393f5b4e_1162x638.png)

The overlay is suggesting a move to (at least) the 14x NTM P/E range in the S&P 500.  If you look at the last 2 major market selloffs (labeled above), both of them bottomed in the 13x NTM P/E range.  This suggests that another 20-30% of multiple contraction could in store for the S&P 500.

Summary Answer: If the Fed sticks to the script, I see 20-40% more downside in the S&P 500 before the Bear Market ends (Recession).  If inflation subsides and the Fed is able to pivot with rates under ~2.5%, stocks can bottom at higher levels.

**2.       What do you think will happen after the market flush? With QT, inflation etc. still lingering throughout 2022 and 2023, will markets move sideways for a long time?**

My Short Answer: It depends how quickly the economy deteriorates under tighter monetary conditions.  In a normal cycle, stocks bottom during the recession, and start the next bull phase before the economy starts to improve.

As I highlighted above, my Base Case is a further 20-40% downside in the S&P 500 before this cycle ends.  This is predicated on the fact that I do not believe the Fed will be able to reduce inflation from 8.6% to its 2% target unless they hike rates well above 3%.  This is the point where I believe things could begin to “break” in the economy.  A 3% rise in interest rates might not look like much historically, but greatly increased leverage has made the economy much more sensitive to interest rates over time, such that smaller hikes in the cost of borrowing become destructive.  This is highlighted in the below graphic from BofA:

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!uszD!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2b4c4123-e3e8-442b-ba62-1ab7183d1da4_941x679.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!uszD!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2b4c4123-e3e8-442b-ba62-1ab7183d1da4_941x679.png)

As you can see in the chart above, smaller and smaller absolute increases in the Federal Funds Rate have led to financial deleveraging “events”.  We are unlikely to escape the fastest global tightening cycle since 2008 without such an event.

In terms of Market cycles, I have been adamant that we are in a Bear Cycle since December.  Legendary investor Howard Marks describes the three stages of a Bear Market in “The Tide Goes Out” (March 2008):

> “The first stage, when just a few thoughtful investors recognize that, despite the prevailing bullishness, things won’t always be rosy,
>
> The second stage, when most investors recognize that things are deteriorating, and
>
> The third stage, when everyone’s convinced things can only get worse”

I have mentioned in several past reports that I expect a capitulation/panic downside move in stocks before this Bear Market ends.  However, this does not mean it will be the ultimate bottom, as indicated in the below graphic of a Market Cycle.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!PVd4!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F03daaccf-5ec4-40b1-9c1d-81b46ef3dfc2_1456x1155.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!PVd4!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F03daaccf-5ec4-40b1-9c1d-81b46ef3dfc2_1456x1155.png)

As you can see in the above chart, the “Capitulation” move is not the ultimate bottom, it is simply the point of peak panic.  This is also how the Dot Com Bust played out, as you can see below.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!_cDv!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2fefd22f-ab07-4fc0-990e-a243b7dfcf25_2048x1257.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!_cDv!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2fefd22f-ab07-4fc0-990e-a243b7dfcf25_2048x1257.png)

As the Nasdaq showed in 2000-01, bottoming can be a process that takes time, even after the worst of the declines have ended. Capitulation typically comes near a bottom, but not necessarily at a bottom. If the above overlay continues to play out, the carnage in ARKK could spill over into 2023 before bottoming.

Summary Answer: History shows that stocks tend to bottom during the recession, typically before the economic data begins to improve.  I expect to see something similar this cycle, even if we get a flush in the markets earlier.

**3.       Does a Powell pivot this week change the direction of the market?**

My Short Answer: Yes.  However, I do not view this scenario as likely given that there is no evidence of inflation diminishing.

In past reports, I have compared our current Macro backdrop to 2018 in the sense that we are dealing with 4+ rate hikes and QT.  However, as I discussed in [“How Late Is It?”](https://marlincapital.substack.com/p/getting-late?s=w), we are dealing with a much more aggressive Fed this time around.

In 2018, the S&P 500 bottomed near 13x P/E after Powell pivoted from his hawkish stance (see valuation graphic above).  This will be a tougher ask for Powell this time around, as (1) Inflation is 8.6%, and (2) valuations are currently 25% above where he pivoted in 2018.

Speaking of valuations, it is important to note that the Federal Reserve has cited the Forward P/E Ratio as something they look at when analyzing asset prices.  The below passage is from the January FOMC Minutes:

> “The staff judged that asset valuation pressures remained elevated. In particular, the forward price-to-earnings ratio for the S&P 500 index stood at the upper end of its historical distribution”

This is important to note because it gives us clues to where Powell could “step in” to save the market, if need be.  I believe Powell could pivot if we reach a 10x-13x P/E, as this would represent the lower end of its historical distribution.  I do not view a Powell pivot before that point as likely, unless inflation diminishes greatly.

Summary Answer: Yes, Powell can call a bottom, but not until the inflation crisis is under control and/or valuations are cheaper.
